Below is a Transportation & Logistics (Freight, Last‑Mile, 3PL) **industry module** you can drop into your kickoff-brief generator. It’s written for FDD use (what matters, what to ask, what to watch) and is **segment-aware** because “logistics” can mean very different economics depending on whether it’s brokered transportation, asset-based fleets, contract warehousing, or last‑mile delivery.

---

# Segment map the assistant should assume

Most deals in this bucket fall into one (or a mix) of:

1. **Freight brokerage / managed transportation (asset‑light 3PL):** sells capacity, buys capacity from carriers; spread business.
2. **Freight forwarding / customs brokerage:** international forwarding, documentation, brokerage fees, consolidation (ocean/air).
3. **Asset‑based trucking / dedicated / drayage:** owns/leases tractors, trailers; cost-per-mile economics; safety/regulatory heavy.
4. **Contract logistics / warehousing / fulfillment (3PL):** storage + handling + value-add; labor productivity + occupancy drives margin.
5. **Last‑mile / final‑mile / courier (parcel or bulky):** route density + stops/hour; heavy labor + SLA/claims penalties.

The module below covers all five, with emphasis on **Freight / Last‑Mile / 3PL**.

# Part 1: Industry Identification

## Question 1: What words/phrases/concepts signal this industry?

### Business model language

* “**3PL** / third-party logistics,” “**contract logistics**,” “**asset‑light** logistics”
* “**freight brokerage**,” “**managed transportation**,” “transportation management services (**TMS**)”
* “**freight forwarding**,” “**NVOCC**,” “customs brokerage,” “IATA,” “FMC,” “incoterms”
* “**LTL** (less-than-truckload), **TL/FTL** (truckload/full truckload), intermodal, drayage, expedited”
* “**linehaul**,” “**pickup & delivery (P&D)**,” “hub-and-spoke,” “cross-dock,” “sortation”
* “**last‑mile** / **final‑mile**,” “same-day/next-day,” “route-based delivery,” “white glove,” “installation,” “reverse logistics/returns”

### KPI / operating terminology

* “**loads / shipments**,” “tenders,” “lane,” “length-of-haul,” “headhaul/backhaul”
* “**gross profit per load**,” “**take rate**,” “net revenue,” “spread,” “buy rate / sell rate”
* “**revenue per mile** / **cost per mile**,” “**empty miles / deadhead**,” “tractor utilization”
* “**operating ratio (OR)**,” “yield (ex‑fuel),” “revenue per shipment”
* “**accessorials**” (detention, demurrage, lumper, layover, stop-off), “**fuel surcharge (FSC)**”
* “**OTD/OTIF**,” “claims ratio,” “cargo damage,” “chargebacks,” “service-level penalties”
* Warehouse: “pallet positions,” “square feet,” “throughput,” “picks,” “lines,” “units,” “dock-to-stock,” “WMS,” “labor standards”
* Last‑mile: “stops per route,” “stops/hour,” “packages per stop,” “route density,” “on-time window,” “POD”

### Customer / transaction descriptions

* Customers described as **shippers**, **retailers**, **manufacturers**, **importers/exporters**, **e-commerce marketplaces**, **big-box**, **healthcare**

### Revenue model indicators

* “**per load / per mile / per stop / per package**” pricing
* Warehouse billing: “**storage $/pallet/day**,” “**handling $/inbound pallet, $/pick**,” “value-added services”
* “pass-through” language: fuel surcharge, accessorial pass-through, “reimbursables,” “cost-plus”

# Part 2: Units That Matter

## Question 2: The 5–8 KPIs a generalist must understand

| KPI                                                                                     | Practitioner Definition                                                                                                                                                            | Why It Matters in Diligence                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Volume** (Loads/Shipments; Orders; Stops; Miles)                                      | The “throughput” of the business: how many loads/shipments moved, how many warehouse orders processed, how many last‑mile stops completed, and (for fleets) how many miles driven. | Separates **market cycle** vs **real share gains**, and tells you whether growth is from adding throughput or just price. Also anchors capacity/utilization analysis.                                                                                                                                           |
| **Yield / Price** (Rev per load / per mile / per stop; “yield ex‑fuel”)                 | What the company charges **per unit**, often tracked excluding fuel surcharge so you see “true” price.                                                                             | Core for **price/volume/mix** decomposition. Yield compression is often the earliest sign of competitive pressure or weak bid discipline.                                                                                                                                                                       |
| **Gross Profit / Net Revenue** (and **Take Rate**)                                      | For broker/forwarder: “gross profit” (often called “net revenue”) = customer billings minus purchased transportation; take rate = gross profit ÷ revenue.                          | In brokerage, revenue can be mostly pass-through; **gross profit per load** and take rate are what really drive EBITDA. Public broker models illustrate low operating margins (e.g., C.H. Robinson’s 2024 operating margin was ~3.8% with gross profits ~$2.7B on total revenues ~$17.7B). ([C.H. Robinson][1]) |
| **Contribution Margin** (per load / per route / per order)                              | Gross profit minus directly attributable variable ops costs (dispatch, dock labor, driver/contractor payout, packaging, etc.).                                                     | Lets you see whether “growth” is profitable **before** corporate overhead—and whether contracts/lane/route economics are fundamentally sound.                                                                                                                                                                   |
| **Utilization / Density** (tractor/driver utilization; warehouse occupancy; stops/hour) | How fully assets and labor are used: loaded miles vs available miles; warehouse space utilization; stops/hour and route density in last‑mile.                                      | Biggest driver of margin expansion: the best operators turn fixed costs into **absorbed capacity**. Low utilization = hidden EBITDA risk.                                                                                                                                                                       |
| **Empty Miles / Repositioning** (“deadhead %”)                                          | % of fleet miles without revenue load (or last‑mile driving between stops).                                                                                                        | Direct hit to cost-per-mile/stop. High deadhead often indicates weak network density, bad lane mix, or poor planning.                                                                                                                                                                                           |
| **Service & Claims** (OTD/OTIF, claims ratio, chargebacks)                              | Performance vs SLAs plus the cost of failures (cargo claims, damage, penalties, re-delivery).                                                                                      | Service problems create **margin leakage**, customer churn, and “one-time” charges that are often recurring. Also ties to safety and insurance costs.                                                                                                                                                           |
| **Operating Ratio (OR)** (mostly asset-based carriers)                                  | Operating costs ÷ revenue (lower is better). An OR of 85 implies ~15% operating margin before non-operating items.                                                                 | Fast way to benchmark asset-based performance. Many LTL carriers manage to OR targets; e.g., XPO disclosed an adjusted operating ratio of 82.9% in Q2 2025. ([investors.xpo.com][2])                                                                                                                            |

# Part 3: Value Driver Framework

## Question 3: EBITDA value driver tree

Below is a **modular tree** that works whether the target is broker, warehouse 3PL, last‑mile, or mixed.

```text
EBITDA
├─ Net Revenue (or Gross Profit, depending on accounting presentation)
│  ├─ Transportation Revenue (customer billings)
│  │  ├─ Volume × Yield
│  │  │  ├─ Freight: # loads/shipments × revenue per load (or per mile)
│  │  │  ├─ Last-mile: # stops/packages × revenue per stop/package
│  │  │  └─ Forwarding: # TEUs/tons/shipments × rate per shipment
│  │  ├─ Accessorials (detention, demurrage, lumper, inside delivery, redelivery)
│  │  ├─ Fuel Surcharge (FSC) (pass-through mechanics matter)
│  │  └─ Mix effects
│  │     ├─ Mode mix (TL vs LTL vs intermodal vs parcel)
│  │     ├─ Contract vs spot mix
│  │     ├─ Lane mix (length-of-haul; headhaul/backhaul balance)
│  │     ├─ Customer mix (enterprise vs SMB; industry vertical; SLA complexity)
│  │     └─ Geography mix (density; urban vs rural)
│  ├─ Warehousing / Fulfillment Revenue
│  │  ├─ Storage: pallet-positions-days (or sq ft) × rate
│  │  ├─ Handling: inbound pallets + outbound pallets + picks/lines × rate
│  │  ├─ Value-added services: kitting, labeling, returns processing, assembly
│  │  └─ Contract structure mix (fixed fee vs cost-plus vs transactional)
│  └─ Gross-to-Net Adjustments
│     ├─ Pricing concessions / rebates / RFP givebacks
│     ├─ Service failures (penalties, chargebacks)
│     ├─ Claims/allowances (damage, shortages)
│     └─ Bad debt / credit memos
│
├─ COGS / Direct Costs (segment-dependent)
│  ├─ Purchased Transportation (broker/forwarder)
│  │  ├─ Carrier linehaul cost (buy rate)
│  │  ├─ Carrier fuel component
│  │  └─ Accessorials paid to carriers
│  ├─ Fleet Ops (asset-based / last-mile owned fleet)
│  │  ├─ Driver wages/benefits or contractor payouts
│  │  ├─ Fuel, tolls, permits
│  │  ├─ Repairs & maintenance, tires
│  │  ├─ Insurance (auto liability, cargo), claims
│  │  └─ Equipment lease / depreciation (tractors, trailers, vans)
│  └─ Warehouse Ops (contract logistics)
│     ├─ Direct labor (warehouse associates; supervisors)
│     ├─ Temp labor / overtime
│     ├─ Facility rent & utilities (often site-level)
│     ├─ MHE lease/dep (forklifts, conveyors), maintenance
│     └─ Packaging & supplies
│
└─ OpEx (SG&A)
   ├─ Sales & marketing (bid desk, account management)
   ├─ G&A (finance, HR, legal)
   ├─ Technology (TMS/WMS/route optimization; IT staff; SaaS fees)
   ├─ Corporate overhead allocations / shared services
   └─ One-offs (restructuring, integration, litigation)
```

### What to explicitly teach the assistant (important)

* **Brokerage/forwarding:** EBITDA is typically driven by **gross profit (net revenue)**, not “top-line revenue,” because purchased transportation is pass-through.
* **Mix** is often more important than “growth”: TL vs LTL vs intermodal; enterprise vs SMB; urban last‑mile vs rural; high-touch warehouse verticals (healthcare) vs commoditized overflow.

## Question 4: Full cost structure and what drives margins

Because “Transportation & Logistics” P&Ls differ by subsegment, the module should present **four canonical templates**.

## A) Freight Brokerage / Managed Transportation (asset‑light 3PL)

### Revenue & COGS mechanics

* **Revenue (gross billings):** what you invoice shipper (linehaul + FSC + accessorials).
* **COGS (purchased transportation):** what you pay the carrier.
* **Gross profit / net revenue:** spread; KPI is often **gross profit per load** and **take rate**.

**Margin reality (benchmark anchors):**

* Asset-light brokers often show **low operating margins** on gross revenue because carrier costs are in COGS; for example, CH Robinson reported 2024 operating margin ~3.8%. ([C.H. Robinson][1])
* RXO’s 2024 press release shows adjusted EBITDA margin of **~2.6%** on revenue (company-wide). ([RXO][3])

### What moves gross profit & EBITDA

* **Rate environment:** sell rate vs buy rate spread (compresses in down cycles).
* **Carrier procurement:** ability to secure capacity at favorable buy rates.
* **Load mix:** shorter-haul, dense lanes can improve margin per hour; long-haul may have thinner spread.
* **Customer contract quality:** accessorial pass-through, FSC true-up terms, service penalty structure.
* **Productivity:** loads per head / GP per head (automation, pricing tools).

### Unit economics (brokerage)

* **Per load economics:**
  `Gross profit per load = (Customer linehaul + accessorials billed – carrier linehaul – carrier accessorials) ± claims/chargebacks`
* Key leverage points: **pricing discipline**, accessorial capture, automation that increases **loads per rep**.

---

## B) Asset‑based trucking / dedicated / drayage

### Typical cost structure (unit-driven)

* **Driver labor:** wages, benefits, overtime, recruiting, training, sign-on bonuses
* **Fuel:** largest variable; mitigated by FSC for contract freight (but not always perfect)
* **Maintenance & tires**
* **Insurance & claims:** auto liability, cargo; self-insured retentions can create volatility
* **Equipment:** lease costs or depreciation; interest; trailer pools
* **Network/facilities:** terminals, yards, dispatch

### Margin mechanics

* Margin is usually managed via **Operating Ratio (OR)** and **cost per mile**.
* Primary drivers:

  * **Utilization** (loaded miles, hours, tractor turns)
  * **Empty miles**
  * **Driver productivity** (miles per driver per week; detention time)
  * **Fuel recovery** effectiveness
  * **Maintenance discipline** and fleet age

### Unit economics (fleet)

* **Per mile:** revenue per mile (ex-fuel) – variable cost per mile (driver + fuel + maintenance) = contribution per mile.
* Leverage points: reduce deadhead, improve planning, reduce dwell/detention, optimize backhaul.

---

## C) Contract logistics / warehousing / fulfillment (3PL)

### Typical “COGS” / direct costs

* **Direct labor** is the biggest driver (esp. e-comm fulfillment).
* **Facility rent/utilities** often sit in direct costs (depending on reporting).
* **Temp labor & overtime** = major volatility lever.
* **Packaging supplies** (esp. parcel fulfillment).
* **MHE depreciation/lease** and maintenance.

**Benchmark anchor:** GXO disclosed full-year 2024 adjusted EBITDA margin of **~7.0%**. ([gxo.com][4])

### What moves margin

* **Occupancy and throughput** vs fixed site cost base.
* **Labor productivity:** picks/hour, lines/hour, units/hour, dock-to-stock time.
* **Contract structure:** cost-plus vs fixed fee vs transactional; pass-through of wage inflation.
* **Value-added mix:** kitting, returns, health sciences compliance can raise margin.

### Unit economics (warehouse)

* **Per order:** revenue per order – direct labor per order – packaging – site-level fixed absorption.
* Leverage points: automation, labor scheduling, engineered labor standards, slotting optimization.

---

## D) Last‑mile / final‑mile delivery (parcel or bulky)

### Direct costs

* **Driver model:** employees vs independent contractors (big risk lever).
* **Vehicle costs:** rentals/leases, maintenance, fuel/charging.
* **Dispatch + routing ops**
* **Claims/chargebacks:** damage, missed windows, failed attempts, return costs.
* **Insurance & workers’ comp** (or contractor insurance compliance).

### Margin mechanics

* **Route density** is everything:

  * stops/hour
  * average drive time between stops
  * first-attempt delivery rate
  * re-deliveries/returns
* SLA penalty structures can make “revenue growth” **negative contribution** if service slips.

### Unit economics (last‑mile)

* **Per stop:** revenue per stop – driver cost per stop – vehicle cost per stop – penalty/claims leakage.
* Leverage points: tighter routing, customer selection (urban density), better delivery window management, damage reduction.

---

## Margin differentiation: what separates high vs low performers

High-margin operators tend to have:

* **Density advantages** (repeat lanes/routes, clustered delivery geography, multi-client warehouses)
* **Contract quality** (indexation for fuel & wages, enforceable accessorials, limited penalty exposure)
* **Operational discipline** (engineered labor standards, strong dispatch, low claims)
* **Tech-enabled productivity** (routing, dynamic pricing, EDI automation, WMS/TMS utilization)
* **Value-add / specialization** (health sciences, hazmat, white glove, customs expertise)

Low-margin operators often show:

* Growth concentrated in **one mega-customer**, **spot market dependence**, or **penalty-heavy contracts**
* Weak accessorial capture, high disputes/credit memos
* Underinvested systems leading to manual work and billing delays

# Part 3b: Working Capital, Capex

## Question 5: Working Capital value driver tree

```text
Operating Working Capital
├─ Accounts Receivable (AR)
│  ├─ Billing cycle speed (POD/BOL readiness, EDI 210 accuracy)
│  ├─ Disputes/short pays (accessorial disputes, service failures)
│  ├─ Customer terms & credit quality (enterprise vs SMB)
│  └─ Mix effects (ocean/air forwarding can lengthen cycles)
│
├─ Accounts Payable (AP)
│  ├─ Carrier payment terms (standard vs quick-pay programs)
│  ├─ Fuel vendor terms, 3rd-party contractors, temp labor
│  └─ Concentration / bargaining power vs carriers
│
├─ Accruals & Other
│  ├─ Claims reserves (cargo damage, service penalties)
│  ├─ Self-insurance accruals (auto liability, workers comp)
│  ├─ Accrued purchased transportation (unbilled carrier invoices)
│  ├─ Deferred revenue / customer deposits (some warehouse contracts)
│  └─ Prepaids (insurance, permits) and payroll timing
│
└─ Inventory (usually limited)
   ├─ Packaging supplies / consumables
   └─ Spare parts (fleet) - if material
```

### Receivables (DSO): what drives it, what’s typical

* **Big driver:** documentation + disputes. Missing PODs, accessorial backup, or EDI errors delay invoicing.
* DSO varies widely; one U.S. dataset lists **Integrated Freight & Logistics ~52 days** and **Trucking ~53 days**. ([Risk Concern][5])
* For brokers specifically, the *structural* issue is often: **carriers want faster pay than shippers pay** → working-capital drag.

### Payables (DPO): nuance in this industry

* Carriers frequently use **quick-pay** or factoring; brokers may pay faster in exchange for a fee/discount.
* Example market practice: “standard” 30-day pay with optional 7-day or next-day pay for a discount. ([Sunset Transportation][6])
* Diligence focus: is the company *funding* quick-pay without adequate pricing/fees? Is this masking liquidity pressure?

### Cash conversion cycle & cash traps

* Brokerage can be **cash consumptive** in growth periods (AR grows faster than AP if paying carriers fast).
* Seasonality: Q4 peak shipping can spike AR and purchased transportation accruals.
* Cash traps: disputed accessorials, large claims/chargebacks, customer offsets, carrier invoice backlogs.

## Question 6: Capex in this industry

### 1) Growth Capex

**Asset-light broker/forwarder**

* Primarily **technology + integrations** (TMS, pricing engines, EDI/API integrations), minimal physical capex.

**Warehouse 3PL**

* New site start-up: leasehold improvements, racking, MHE, IT (RF scanners), automation (conveyors/sorters).
* Ramp to maturity typically driven by:

  * customer onboarding wave
  * hiring/training curve
  * inventory positioning & slotting stabilization

**Last-mile / asset-based**

* Fleet additions (vans/box trucks/tractors), depot openings, sortation equipment, handheld devices.
* Ramp depends on route density and customer volume commitments.

### 2) Maintenance Capex

* **Fleet-heavy:** steady replacement of vehicles, trailers, and major components.
* **Warehouse-heavy:** MHE refresh, automation maintenance, facility upkeep.

### 3) Technology / digital capex (or opex if SaaS)

* TMS/WMS implementations, route optimization tools, customer portals/visibility, telematics/ELDs.
* Watch-outs:

  * big bang WMS/TMS go-lives (service disruption + cost overruns)
  * aggressive capitalization of software development costs (accounting diligence)

## Question 7: Standard analyses in this industry

| Analysis                                                               | What It Shows                                                             | Good vs. Concerning                                                                                                                            |

| **Carrier/vendor concentration & performance**                         | Capacity risk, cost inflation risk, service risk.                         | Good: diversified carrier base + scorecards. Concerning: dependence on a few carriers/contractors or high carrier churn.                       |
| **Service level analysis (OTD/claims/penalties)**                      | Operational quality and margin leakage.                                   | Good: strong OTD and low claims with stable reserves. Concerning: rising claims accruals, recurring “one-time” penalties.                      |

| **Working capital walk (DSO/DPO/claims accrual)**                      | Cash needs and liquidity risk.                                            | Good: stable DSO and disciplined quick-pay economics. Concerning: DSO creep + faster carrier pay = cash squeeze.                               |

# Part 5: Red Flags

## Question 8: 8–10 red flags to look for

| Red Flag                                                  | Early Signal (CIM/VDD)                                            | Data Room Signal                                                   | Why It’s a Problem                                   | How to Investigate                                                     |
| --------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Growth driven by freight cycle**                        | “Benefited from favorable rate environment / tight capacity”      | Volume flat/declining, yield compression, GP/load down in downturn | EBITDA not durable; reverses with cycle              | Build PVM bridge; compare contract vs spot; trend GP per load monthly  |
| **Revenue vs GP confusion**                               | Heavy emphasis on “revenue growth” with little on gross profit    | Take rate falling; purchased transportation % rising               | You can “buy revenue” by underpricing                | Request GP per load, loads per head; reconcile revenue to GP           |

| **Penalty-heavy SLAs / chargebacks**                      | “Industry-leading service” but no KPI disclosure                  | Rising chargebacks, credits, claims; disputes backlog              | Margin leakage, churn, reputational risk             | Analyze penalties as % of revenue by customer; root-cause chart        |
| **Last-mile contractor classification risk**              | “Flexible independent contractor model”                           | High 1099 mix; legal reserves; audits; inconsistent documentation  | Potential back wages/taxes; margin blow-up           | Review contractor agreements, insurance/IC compliance, legal matters   |

| **Carrier capacity fragility**                            | “Strong carrier network” (no depth)                               | Top carriers concentrated; high turnover; reliance on spot         | Can’t fulfill loads in tight market; buy rates spike | Carrier concentration + churn; tender acceptance; spot coverage        |
| **Working capital squeeze masked by factoring/quick-pay** | “Strong cash generation” with little WC detail                    | DSO rising + faster carrier pay; increasing factoring              | Liquidity risk; debt-like costs                      | AR/AP aging trends; quick-pay economics; reconcile to cash flow        |
| **Aggressive QoE add-backs**                              | “Normalized EBITDA excludes start-up costs / one-time” repeatedly | Same “one-time” items every year (claims, recruiting, IT)          | Overstated run-rate EBITDA                           | Require add-back support; look-back recurrence test                    |

# Part 6: First Data Requests

## Question 9: Standard first-round data requests (P1/P2)

### P1 (Critical) — “kickoff pack” request

1. **Monthly P&L by segment** (TL/LTL/forwarding/warehouse/last-mile) + FSC separated (36 months).
2. **Load/shipment-level dataset** (24 months): date, customer, mode, O/D, miles, revenue components (linehaul/FSC/accessorial), carrier cost, accessorial cost, margin, status (delivered/in-transit).
3. **Customer profitability**: monthly revenue + gross profit by customer (36 months) + top 25 accounts, contract type, renewal dates.
4. **Claims/chargebacks** log (36 months): customer, amount, cause, resolution status.
5. **AR aging by customer** + billing lag metrics; **AP aging by carrier** (24 months).
6. If warehousing/fulfillment is material: **site-level monthly P&L** + throughput and labor hours (24 months).
7. If last-mile is material: **route/day-level KPI file** (12–24 months): stops, miles, hours, failed attempts, penalties.

### P2 (Important) — first data room batch

* Top customer contracts/MSAs, rate cards, SLAs, penalty schedules, indexation clauses.
* Carrier contracts / preferred carrier terms; quick-pay program terms and usage.
* Safety/compliance: DOT/FMCSA data, accident history, insurance loss runs.
* Fleet listing (age, lease vs own, maintenance history) if asset-based.
* Technology stack map (TMS/WMS/route tools), major implementations, capitalization policy.

# Part 9: Accounting & Recognition

## Question 12: Industry-specific accounting / revenue recognition issues

### Revenue recognition timing (operations reality)

* **Transportation:** revenue often recognized when the performance obligation is satisfied—commonly on **delivery**, but many operators accrue **in‑transit revenue** based on progress for longer hauls. Cutoff risk is high around period-end.
* **Warehousing:** storage is typically recognized over time (as space/time is provided); handling recognized as activities occur.
* **Accessorials & fuel surcharges:** may be billed later than linehaul—creates accrual and cutoff judgments.

### Principal vs agent (gross vs net) — major diligence issue

Many logistics models involve third parties (carriers). Under IFRS 15 / ASC 606 concepts, management must assess whether it controls the service before transfer (principal) or is arranging for another party (agent). This drives **gross vs net** presentation, affecting revenue size and gross margin percent (while often not changing profit dollars). ([IFRS Foundation][10])
Key diligence implication: comparability across targets can be terrible unless you normalize to **net revenue/gross profit**.

### Cost recognition & capitalization

* **Purchased transportation accruals:** carrier invoices often lag; ensure accrual completeness and reversal discipline.
* **Self-insurance / claims:** large judgment area (auto liability, cargo claims, workers’ comp).
* **Software capitalization:** internal-use software (TMS/WMS, optimization) capitalization policy can be aggressive; check useful lives, impairments, and whether “customer-serving” costs are being capitalized.

### Balance sheet reserves & estimates

* **Claims reserves** (cargo damage, penalties) and **allowances/credits**
* **Bad debt reserve** (especially SMB shipper base)
* **Insurance reserves** under high deductibles/self-insured retentions
* **Accrued payroll/bonuses** (driver incentives, peak season retention programs)

### Cash vs accrual considerations

* Broker models may show significant differences due to:

  * quick-pay / factoring programs (economics sometimes hidden in fees/discounts)
  * timing differences between shipper billing and carrier payables

# Part 12: Anything Else

## Question 15: What FDD teams often miss (and what trips up first-timers)

1. **Revenue is not the metric** in brokerage/forwarding—**gross profit (net revenue) per load** is. Many decks intentionally emphasize revenue growth because it looks bigger.
2. Logistics is **cyclical** and mean-reverting. Always ask: *what part of the freight cycle are we in* and *how does GP/load behave across cycles*?
3. **Fuel surcharge is not “neutral.”** Timing, index choice, and contract structure can create real margin gains/losses.
4. **Service failures create recurring “one-time” costs.** If claims/penalties show up every year, they’re not add-backs.
5. **Working capital can be a silent killer** (especially where carriers want fast pay and shippers pay net-30/45/60).
6. In last‑mile, **density beats scale**. Growth into low-density geographies without price lift often destroys margin.
7. **Comparability is hard** because of accounting presentation (gross vs net), lease accounting, and differing cost classifications—normalize early.
