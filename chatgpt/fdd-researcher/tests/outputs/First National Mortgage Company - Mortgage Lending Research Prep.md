### 2) The Canadian mortgage market’s “plumbing”: insured vs uninsured, and why securitization matters

#### Insured vs. uninsured mortgages

*   **Insured mortgages** generally refer to high loan-to-value loans (typically >80% LTV) requiring mortgage insurance, with insurers such as CMHC (and private insurers). Mortgage insurance is foundational to Canada’s securitization stack because **insured loans** are the main collateral for many government-supported securitization programs.
*   **Uninsured mortgages** generally have larger down payments (often ≥20%); underwriting expectations are heavily shaped by OSFI’s guidance for federally regulated institutions, and by market practice for non-banks.

#### Government-supported securitization: NHA MBS and Canada Mortgage Bonds

Canada’s securitization ecosystem is anchored by:

*   **NHA Mortgage-Backed Securities (NHA MBS):** CMHC provides a **timely payment guarantee of principal and interest** on NHA MBS, as agent of the Government of Canada, under the National Housing Act framework.
*   **Canada Mortgage Bonds (CMB):** A special purpose trust (Canada Housing Trust) issues bonds, uses proceeds to purchase NHA MBS, and the timely payment of interest/principal is guaranteed by the Government of Canada through CMHC. Public Accounts disclose **CMBs outstanding** (including accrued interest) and explicitly describe the guarantee structure.

**Why this matters for monolines:** NHA MBS/CMB funding can offer deep, scalable liquidity (at government-linked pricing), but it carries constraints: eligibility rules, pool composition, reporting, and fees. The NHA MBS Guide outlines issuer responsibilities, reporting requirements, fees, and linkage to CMB.

#### Non-bank mortgage lending is a meaningful part of Canada’s system

Statistics Canada reports that non-bank lenders held **$396.8B** of outstanding residential mortgages in **Q1 2025**.  
This matters because (i) regulators watch systemic growth in non-bank credit, and (ii) funding conditions and investor appetite can shift quickly in stressed markets (spreads, warehouse haircuts, ABCP rollover risk).

### 3) Regulation and policy: what can change your pipeline, mix, and margins

#### OSFI’s uninsured mortgage stress test (MQR)

OSFI’s **Minimum Qualifying Rate (MQR)** for uninsured mortgages is **the greater of contract rate + 2% or 5.25%** (as of OSFI’s page updated in late 2024).  
OSFI also introduced relief for certain **uninsured straight switches at renewal** (no longer requiring a set MQR in those cases, subject to B-20 principles).

**Diligence angle:** Stress-test rules affect approval rates, product design, and conversion. Even if non-banks aren’t OSFI-regulated, their underwriting often tracks OSFI standards due to investor expectations.

#### OSFI loan-to-income (LTI) limits (uninsured portfolios)

OSFI has implemented **portfolio-level LTI limits** for federally regulated lenders’ uninsured mortgage books (quarterly reporting and institution-specific thresholds).  
**Diligence angle:** Even if First National is not OSFI-regulated like a bank, LTI limits can still affect **competitive dynamics** because banks and federally regulated competitors may tighten at the margin—potentially pushing volume toward non-banks, or tightening broker flows depending on investor overlays.

#### Federal insured-mortgage rule changes (affecting eligible borrower pool and product strategy)

The federal government announced reforms including:

*   increasing the **insured mortgage price cap from $1.0M to $1.5M** effective **December 15, 2024**; and
*   expanding **30-year insured amortizations** for first-time buyers and buyers of new builds (with staged implementation beginning August 2024 and expansion effective December 2024).

**Diligence angle:** These changes can shift the insured/uninsured mix, insurer volumes, securitization eligibility, and broker conversion rates—especially in higher-priced metro markets.

#### Interest rate environment (renewal pressure + origination opportunity)

The Bank of Canada held its policy rate at **2.25%** on **December 10, 2025**.  
**Diligence angle:** For Canadian borrowers facing renewals from low-rate vintages, renewal affordability is a key driver of delinquency risk, product switching, and refinance/extend behavior—directly impacting servicing and renewal economics.

*   What is the loan-level distribution (FICO, LTV, GDS/TDS, LTI, DSCR for commercial, province, broker) by vintage?
*   What is the delinquency/arrears roll-forward and cure behavior by cohort? How does it compare to system indicators (CBA/CMHC)?

**B) Funding, liquidity, and securitization**

*   Full map of funding programs: warehouse lines, repo, ABCP conduits, NHA MBS/CMB usage, capacities, triggers, collateral/cash enhancements.
*   Stress test: what happens if securitization spreads widen or issuance volumes are constrained? (CMHC programs have defined rules/fees/reporting.)

**C) Hedging and interest rate risk**

*   What are the hedge objectives (pipeline vs retained portfolio)?
*   How is hedge effectiveness measured and governed?
*   How does the firm manage basis between mortgage coupons, funding indices (CORRA/CDOR transition impacts), and derivative hedges?

**D) Servicing operations & renewals**

*   Renewal retention and pricing discipline; how often are borrowers “shopped away”?
*   Collections staffing model and early-stage intervention effectiveness.
*   Servicing SLAs for institutional clients and penalties/termination rights.

**E) Commercial / multi-unit underwriting**

*   DSCR, rent roll quality, geographic/property type concentration, sponsor concentration.
*   CMHC-insured multi-unit exposure vs conventional commercial risk.

**F) Compliance / licensing**

*   Validate provincial licensing/registrations, complaint handling, and broker oversight; confirm any regulatory examinations or findings.
