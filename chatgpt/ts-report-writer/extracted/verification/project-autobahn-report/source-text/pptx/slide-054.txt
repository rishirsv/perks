Requirements of ASC 606
The objective of this step is to allocate the transaction price to each performance obligation in an amount that depicts the amount of consideration to which the entity expects to be entitled
for transferring the promised goods or service to the customer. Generally, an entity accomplishes this objective by allocating the transaction price to each performance obligation on a relative stand-alone selling price (SSP) basis.
The best evidence of a performance obligation’s SSP is an observable price from stand-alone sales. A contractually stated contract price or list price could be the SSP, but this is not presumed to be the case and would need to be supported by other evidence. If there is no observable SSP available, the entity is required to determine an estimated SSP.
Despite the abovementioned, an entity shall allocate a variable amount entirely to a performance obligation (or a distinct good or service that forms part of a single performance obligation) if the terms of the variable payment relate specifically to that performance obligation and if this allocation method is consistent with the overall allocation objective of ASC 606.
Implications of adoption
Contracts contain detailed overviews of all the activities to be performed in each phase. This overview also indicates the unit price of each item and the extended price that will be charged to the customer. Per discussion with Management, the amounts for each activity are generally based on the market rates – pricing for projects is consistent, subject to adjustments to reflect the requirements (e.g. number of patients, number of sites, complexity). Axiom may provide enterprise discounts to its customers, but such a discount percentage is typically provided over all of the goods and services. Because of this pricing structure, the allocation objective of ASC 606 would be met if all of the activities correspond to performance obligations.
However, if management concludes that certain promises in the ‘Think/Deploy’ phase relate to administrative tasks or set-up activities, the transaction price for these promises does not relate to a specific performance obligation. The transaction price for these activities will need to be allocated to ‘Think/Deploy’ and ‘Launch’ phase on a relative basis. This means that these revenues will be deferred partly compared to current accounting.
It is expected that the variable consideration included in the contracts can typically be allocated entirely to the performance obligation to which they relate. This is because the variable amounts relate specifically to a performance obligation, the benefit provided to the customer and the effort by Axiom to provide the services.
Key judgement areas
Management indicated that all activities included in the ‘Think/Deploy’ and ‘Launch’ phase are priced at market rates. However, to substantiate this representation management may need to provide support that the unit prices or stated prices included in the contracts can be used as a basis for determining the SSP for each of the performance obligations that are identified.
Management noted that providing various discounts on certain promises in the contract, as opposed to the entire contract, could happen occasionally. This may relate to adjustments to reflect the circumstances of the particular contract (e.g. complexity of that element), in which case the adjusted price may reflect the SSP for that particular project. However, if significant specific discounts were offered as an incentive or without a particular rationale, an allocation of the discount to between the performance obligations in the contract would be applicable. Given that these types of adjustments occur rarely, the impact is not expected to be material.
