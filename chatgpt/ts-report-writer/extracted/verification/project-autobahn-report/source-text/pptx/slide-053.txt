ASC 606 – Impact assessment
Step 3 – Determine the transaction price
Narrative
Current
accounting
The contracts contain
detailed budget calculations for each of the activities to be provided to the client. Typically for each of these activities a fixed price or an estimate is included in the budget. The estimated prices (usually based on expected hours to be incurred, the number of subjects, number of sites and complexity of the study) will be invoiced based on actuals as the activities are performed. Typically, if there is an adjustment compared to the estimated budget, it is due to additional effort required to complete the study (e.g. it may be necessary to exceed the planned allocation of patients). These overages are typically invoiced on the final invoice. If material, management estimates an accrual of the overages in the period in which they occur.
Other overages typically relate to services not included in the initial budget, such as additional modules. These add-ons are typically agreed to via a change order process and then invoiced as provided.
Requirements of ASC 606
The transaction price
is the amount of consideration to which an entity expects to be entitled in exchange for transferring goods or services. To determine the transaction price, an entity considers the terms of the contract, its customary business practices, the effects of (and the constraint on) variable consideration, the time value of money, noncash consideration and consideration payable to the customer.
In general, the transaction price includes an entity’s estimate of the variable consideration by using either the expected value or the most likely amount, depending on which method the entity expects to better predict the amount of consideration to which the entity will be entitled. However, an entity will generally not be required to make such an estimate for a variable consideration that will be recognized in the period in which it is earned based on the variable consideration allocation guidance (refer to step 4).
Implications of adoption
Axiom’s contracts contain promised goods and services with fixed pricing and variable pricing. The variable pricing is usually dependent on the underlying quantity
(e.g. hours, subjects, pass through costs), for which an estimate is included in the contract with the customer. Any overages compared to the estimate are typically caused by changes in the study that are therefore not expected at the contract inception date. As the
variable consideration allocation guidance (refer to step 4) is expected to be applicable, management would not be required to determine the transaction price for the variable components of the contract and rather additional consideration would recognized in the period in which the variance occurs.
If training services or other ‘add-ons’ are optional, they are not included in the transaction price. This is because the customer has a present contractual right (as opposed to an obligation) to purchase additional services and it is our understanding that the pricing for these services reflects their stand-alone selling price (i.e. the future optional services are not significantly discounted to incentivize the customer).
Key judgement areas
Estimation of overages is required in the period in which the additional services are provided; however, this is already considered by management as part of existing processes. Other judgemental areas related to the transaction price primarily relate to the allocation of the transaction price as discussed in step 4.
