SR&ED
– technical feasibility of claims
The CRA has requested for a financial and technical review of Target’s FY17 SR&ED filing, however Management represented the technical review has been postponed until May 2019. Based on our review of the FY17 SR&ED claim, the technical content does not appear to meet the requirements to qualify as eligible work performed for the purposes of claiming federal and provincial ITCs.
Given that fiscal FY17 SR&ED claim is under review (both technical and financial), and
as the content of the applications between FY16 and FY17 is very similar, this generally does not support SR&ED eligible activities for SR&ED ITCs.  In the event of a CRA reassessment, we estimate that as much as 70% of the FY17 claim is at risk.
Generally, subsequent SR&ED claims will not be processed once a review of the prior period claim is underway.  Management has not indicated any additional correspondence with the CRA in respect to the FY17 review but the outcome of FY18 SR&ED claim will be influenced significantly by the results of FY17 SR&ED review. Based on our review of the content of the FY18 claims, we estimated up to 50% of those claims may be at-risk as well. Contractors
in foreign jurisdictions
Based on Management representation, we understand Target engages individuals that are tax resident in countries outside of Canada, including India and various countries in Europe. We understand these individuals are contracted by Target and all agreed to sign an independent contractor agreement. However, Management represented that the contractors are in effect treated as employees of Target, as such persons have been working exclusively with Target as software developers and customer service representatives.
As the employment is exercised outside of Canada, Canadian payroll withholding taxes should not apply. However, based on Management’s representations, there is a medium to high risk that the relevant tax authority in the respective foreign jurisdictions may classify the independent contractors as employees of Target. Consequently, local payroll withholdings and other source deductions may need to be withheld and remitted to such tax authority. Target may also be exposed to penalties and interest on unremitted employee source deductions and certain social security taxes in the respective foreign jurisdictions. Further, subject to specific rules in the relevant jurisdictions, there may be secondary liability for Target or its directors for the principal portion of any unremitted taxes.
Based on review of a subcontractor listing provided in the VDR, and on the assumption that foreign employer payroll obligations are similar to Canadian payroll obligations, for illustrative purposes, we have estimated a potential cash tax exposure of $300,000-400,000.
As non-Canadian tax due diligence is outside of our scope, we recommend obtaining a full indemnity for any historical tax liabilities that could arise as a result of Target engaging contractors that may be more appropriately classified as employees in other jurisdictions.
