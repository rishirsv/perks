# Detailed Breakdown by Definition

## 1) Indebtedness

### Summary

“Indebtedness” is drafted as a broad, debt-and-debt-like bucket: borrowed money and note-type instruments, interest-bearing obligations (excluding ordinary-course trade payables), conditional sale/title retention obligations, deferred purchase price obligations (excluding ordinary-course trade creditors), drawn letters of credit/performance bonds, and guarantees—plus accrued interest/fees and any Closing-date **prepayment/termination/breakage costs**. It also contains a deal-specific exclusion: the **building lease cost** for the leased property is not Indebtedness. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41)

### Bias Rating

🟠 **Buyer-Favorable** (definition breadth), **with material carve-outs in the purchase price mechanics** that can shift practical economics back toward Sellers depending on scope interpretation. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Key Observations

- **“Without duplication”** is helpful to prevent internal double counting within the Indebtedness bucket. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41)
- Unusually, the definition hard-codes a **specific instrument type/vendor**: “all obligations of the Company under credit cards issued by Fifth Third Bank.” (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41)
- The LC/performance bond concept is **narrower than many deals** because it counts these instruments **only to the extent drawn upon**. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41)
- The inclusion of **breakage/prepayment/termination fees due at Closing** is buyer-protective (it avoids Sellers arguing those costs are “transaction costs” or “future costs” rather than net debt). (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41)
- **Economic scope-limitation in purchase price mechanics:** “Estimated Indebtedness” (used in the Base Purchase Price calculation) explicitly excludes: the U.S. Steel Note, Fifth Third credit card obligations, certain Soudronic obligations, and certain payments due to Severstal Wheeling and Kemeny Overseas Packaging (BAO Steel)—all of which the SPA says the **Company pays after Closing**. (SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
- The same carve-out concept appears in Closing conditions requiring the Company to discharge indebtedness agreements **other than** those carve-outs. (SPA §6.2(d)(i), Agreement p. 25 / PDF p. 29)
- **Indemnity backstop exists:** Sellers indemnify Buyer for “Company Transaction Expenses, the Indebtedness of the Company as of immediately prior to the Closing and the DN Advisory Fee,” **solely to the extent amounts are not included** in the §1.3(b) computation. This operates like a “catch” for omitted in-scope items. (SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)

### Double-Count Risks

- **Debt fees vs transaction fees overlap:** Indebtedness includes “accrued but unpaid interest, fees and expenses thereon” and Closing-date breakage/prepayment fees. Separately, “Company Transaction Expenses” includes various advisor/lender counsel fees. Depending on how amounts are invoiced and classified, the same lender-related costs could be argued into either bucket. (SPA §9.1 “Indebtedness” and “Company Transaction Expenses,” Agreement pp. 34–37 / PDF pp. 38–41)
- **Closing payoff vs estimate overlap:** Buyer-funded payoffs at Closing reduce Purchase Price (via the “without double-counting” clause in Base Purchase Price). If the same amounts are also embedded in Estimated Indebtedness, there’s a mechanical double-count risk unless the estimate and the §6.2(d) payoff schedule are aligned. (SPA §1.2(b)(v), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §6.2(d), Agreement p. 25 / PDF p. 29)

### Gap Risks

- **Undrawn LCs/performance bonds** are excluded (only drawn amounts count), which can leave off-balance-sheet exposure outside the Indebtedness bucket if the LC later draws. (SPA §9.1 “Indebtedness,” Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41)
- **Carve-out tension / scope ambiguity (key nuance):** The SPA expressly excludes certain obligations from “Estimated Indebtedness” and says the Company will pay them after Closing. Yet the indemnity clause broadly references pre-Closing Indebtedness to the extent not included in §1.3(b). A reasonable reading is that **§1.3(b) defines what is “in-scope”** for this indemnity backstop—meaning explicitly carved-out items are intentionally outside scope. But the drafting creates enough ambiguity that a buyer-side advisor should flag it. (SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §6.2(d)(i), Agreement p. 25 / PDF p. 29; SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)
- **Trade payables are excluded from Indebtedness**, and without a Working Capital mechanism, large “ordinary course” payables can still economically impact Buyer without being price-adjusted (unless captured by reps/indemnities). (SPA §9.1 “Indebtedness,” Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41; Working Capital absence evidenced by §1.2(b) and §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Market Comparison

- The core list is broadly consistent with market “Net Debt/Indebtedness” definitions, and the inclusion of **breakage/prepayment costs** is a common buyer-favorable refinement in more negotiated deals. (SPA §9.1 “Indebtedness,” Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41)
- It is **less expansive than some market forms** on contingent instruments (LCs only if drawn). (SPA §9.1 “Indebtedness,” Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41)
- It is **more bespoke than most** due to the explicit leased-property building lease carve-out and the Fifth Third credit card callout. (SPA §9.1 “Indebtedness,” Agreement pp. 36–37 / PDF pp. 40–41)

## 2) Cash and Cash Equivalents

### Summary

The SPA does **not** separately define “Cash Equivalents” (or “Cash and Cash Equivalents”). It defines **“Cash”** only as “cash and cash equivalents,” with no further inclusions/exclusions. Importantly, the Base Purchase Price formula adjusts for Indebtedness, Transaction Expenses, and other specified deductions—but **not Cash**. (SPA §9.1 “Cash,” Agreement p. 34 / PDF p. 38; SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Bias Rating

🟢 **Neutral** (primarily because cash is not a pricing lever in the stated mechanics). (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Key Observations

- Definition is **one line**; no detail on restricted cash, minimum cash, overdrafts, outstanding checks, swept accounts, or what qualifies as “cash equivalents.” (SPA §9.1 “Cash,” Agreement p. 34 / PDF p. 38)
- Even though “Cash” is defined, it is **not used** in the core Base Purchase Price adjustment formula (which focuses on Indebtedness and Transaction Expenses). (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
- Practical implication (structure-driven, not speculative): the deal economics appear to be framed as **Purchase Price less specified liability buckets**, rather than an explicit “enterprise value = equity value + net debt – cash” equation. (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Double-Count Risks

- If any party later tries to treat items like short-term investments, restricted deposits, or bank overdrafts as “Cash,” there’s risk of inconsistent treatment because there is no granular definition—especially if those same items are also considered in debt-like buckets in practice. (SPA §9.1 “Cash,” Agreement p. 34 / PDF p. 38; absence of Cash mechanics in SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Gap Risks

- Potential disputes on what qualifies as a “cash equivalent” (e.g., money market funds vs restricted deposits) if “Cash” becomes relevant elsewhere (even though not used in the price equation). (SPA §9.1 “Cash,” Agreement p. 34 / PDF p. 38)

### Market Comparison

- When cash is part of pricing (net-debt deals), market practice is a much more detailed “Cash” definition (restricted cash, overdrafts/netting, cut-off timing, cash in transit). This SPA’s minimal approach is typical only when cash is not a purchase price lever. (SPA §9.1 “Cash,” Agreement p. 34 / PDF p. 38; SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Source

- Definition: SPA **§9.1** (“Cash”), **Agreement p. 34 / PDF p. 38**
- Pricing mechanics: SPA **§1.2(b)**, **Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6**

## 3) Working Capital

### Summary

A Working Capital definition (and Working Capital mechanics) is **not present**. The purchase price adjustment and Base Purchase Price formula make no reference to Working Capital; instead, the adjustment is driven by **Estimated Indebtedness, Estimated Company Transaction Expenses, DN Advisory Fee, Preferred Stock Redemption Amount, and certain Buyer-funded Closing payments**. (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Bias Rating

🟠 **Seller-Favorable** (absence of a WC true-up is typically a seller benefit: fewer post-close price deductions tied to balance sheet movements). (SPA §1.2(b) and §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Key Observations

- No “target working capital,” no “closing working capital,” and no true-up process is described in Article 1’s economic mechanics. (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
- The SPA does include an indemnity framework for **Indebtedness / Company Transaction Expenses / DN Advisory Fee** that are not included in §1.3(b)’s computation, but that is **not** a general Working Capital adjustment. (SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)

### Double-Count Risks

- In the absence of an explicit WC bucket, parties often “argue classification” (e.g., is something an operating payable vs a debt-like item vs a transaction expense). That classification dispute can become economically material because only certain buckets flow through the §1.2(b)/§1.3(b)/§8.2(c) framework. (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)

### Gap Risks

- Without a WC adjustment, Buyer has limited contractual recourse if closing working capital is atypically low—other than through reps (e.g., undisclosed liabilities) and general covenants, which are not as precise as a WC true-up. (Economic mechanics evidenced by SPA §1.2(b) and §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Market Comparison

- Many SPAs with “completion-style” pricing include a Working Capital adjustment or at least define Working Capital to allocate balance sheet items between net debt and operating buckets. This SPA instead uses a **liability-bucket approach** (Indebtedness + Transaction Expenses + specific deductions) without a WC construct. (SPA §1.2(b) and §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Source

- Absence evidenced by economic mechanics: SPA **§1.2(b)** and **§1.3(b)**, **Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6**

## 4) Company Transaction Expenses

### Summary

“Company Transaction Expenses” is defined expansively to include (i) **all Company-incurred third-party fees and expenses** related to drafting/negotiating/executing the SPA and consummating the transaction (excluding the DN Advisory Fee), including Company advisors and the fees/expenses of lawyers for Fifth Third Bank and SW Pelham Fund II, plus certain post-Closing service amounts benefiting Sellers; and (ii) fees/expenses of the Sellers’ Representative and its affiliates that are payable by the Company. It also includes (inside the definition) a limitation that the Company will not be liable after Closing for Transaction Expenses above “Estimated Company Transaction Expenses,” except to the extent included in §1.3(b)’s computation. Finally, it contains a highly bespoke carve-out: multiple named advisor fees are **not** “Company Transaction Expenses” and are to be paid by the Company as ordinary-course payables. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)

### Bias Rating

🟢 **Neutral** (meaningfully mixed: broad inclusions and an indemnity backstop protect Buyer, but the named carve-outs shift real costs back onto the Company/Buyer). (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39; SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)

### Key Observations

- **Breadth is buyer-leaning**: “all third party fees and expenses” incurred by the Company in connection with the transaction documents and consummation is a wide net. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)
- The definition explicitly includes fees/expenses for **lender counsel** (Fifth Third Bank and SW Pelham Fund II), which many SPAs would not expressly name. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)
- It captures amounts payable for **post-Closing services** where the Company engaged someone pre-Closing but services are rendered post-Closing for the benefit of a Seller—this is buyer-protective anti-leakage language. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)
- It also sweeps in **Sellers’ Representative fees/expenses** (and its affiliates) to the extent payable by the Company—again buyer-protective if those costs would otherwise land in the business. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)
- **Seller-friendly carve-out (very notable):** it explicitly states certain professional service expenses (JP Morgan Chase & Stout Risius pension valuations; Baker Tilly; Huck Bouma; Cook Alex patent matters; Barnes Thornburg tax matters) are **not** Company Transaction Expenses and will be paid by the Company as ordinary-course payables. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)
- **Backstop via indemnity:** Sellers indemnify Buyer for Company Transaction Expenses to the extent not included in §1.3(b)’s computation—this functions like a safety net for missed in-scope deal costs. (SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Double-Count Risks

- **Overlap with Indebtedness fees:** Indebtedness includes accrued fees/expenses and breakage/prepayment costs; Transaction Expenses includes various third-party fees including lender counsel. Without a clear allocation rule, the same costs could be argued into either bucket. (SPA §9.1 “Indebtedness” and “Company Transaction Expenses,” Agreement pp. 34–37 / PDF pp. 38–41)
- **Estimate vs Buyer-funded Closing payments:** Base Purchase Price reduces Purchase Price by Estimated Company Transaction Expenses, and also (without double counting) by Buyer-funded payments under §6.2(d) / §6.2(i). If any transaction expenses are paid via §6.2(d) mechanics (or similar), they could be double-counted unless reconciled carefully. (SPA §1.2(b)(ii) and §1.2(b)(v), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §6.2(d), Agreement p. 25 / PDF p. 29)

### Gap Risks

- **Named excluded professional fees are the biggest “gap”:** because they are explicitly not Transaction Expenses, they will not be captured by the Transaction Expense bucket and (on the face of the definition) are intended to remain as Company payables. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)
- The definition focuses on **third-party** fees/expenses; it does not expressly sweep internal costs (e.g., management bonuses, severance/change-in-control payments) unless those are treated elsewhere or qualify under other defined terms. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)

### Market Comparison

- Market-standard “Transaction Expenses” definitions are usually broad, but it is **unusual** to (a) name specific excluded advisor firms and (b) affirmatively state those excluded costs are to be paid by the Company as ordinary-course payables. (SPA §9.1, Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)
- Embedding a **post-Closing liability limitation** inside the definition itself is also relatively bespoke (often that concept appears in a separate covenant/indemnity allocation). (SPA §9.1; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

## 5) Accounting Principles

### Summary

Accounting governance is **split** in this SPA:

- The **Financial Statements** are represented as prepared in accordance with **U.S. GAAP applied consistently**. (SPA §2.6(a), Agreement p. 6 / PDF p. 10)
- The Company is restricted from changing accounting methods/principles/practices between signing and Closing except due to a concurrent GAAP change. (SPA §2.8(i), Agreement p. 8 / PDF p. 12)
- The purchase price adjustment uses an **Estimated Closing Statement** prepared by Sellers’ Representative based on “all available information” and “reasonable detail,” plus a “closing of the books” concept at Closing—**without** an explicit GAAP/consistent-policy preparation mandate for the estimate. (SPA §1.3(a)–(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Bias Rating

🟢 **Neutral** (GAAP consistency protects Buyer on reported financials and reduces pre-close manipulation risk, but the estimate standard is not explicitly GAAP-based). (SPA §2.6(a), Agreement p. 6 / PDF p. 10; SPA §2.8(i), Agreement p. 8 / PDF p. 12; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Key Observations

- **GAAP is a defined concept by cross-reference:** “GAAP” is a defined term pointing back to §2.6(a). (SPA §9.1 “GAAP,” Agreement p. 36 / PDF p. 40; SPA §2.6(a), Agreement p. 6 / PDF p. 10)
- The Sellers’ Representative must deliver the Estimated Closing Statement at least one Business Day pre-Closing, and Sellers must use reasonable best efforts to do a “full balance sheet closing” as of the Closing Date. (SPA §1.3(a)–(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
- The Estimated Closing Statement is described as an “estimated calculation in reasonable detail” based on “all available information,” which is a **reasonableness standard** rather than an accounting-standards standard. (SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
- The SPA partially compensates for this lack of explicit GAAP mechanics via **indemnification**: Sellers indemnify for Indebtedness / Company Transaction Expenses / DN Advisory Fee to the extent not included in the §1.3(b) computation. (SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)

### Double-Count Risks

- Without explicit GAAP-based preparation rules for the estimate, the biggest “accounting” risk becomes **classification disputes** (is an item an operating payable vs Indebtedness vs Transaction Expense), which can create double-count or omission across buckets. (SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §9.1 “Indebtedness” and “Company Transaction Expenses,” Agreement pp. 34–37 / PDF pp. 38–41)

### Gap Risks

- The estimate standard itself (reasonable detail / available information) can create disputes about whether an item **should have** been included in the computation—especially given bespoke carve-outs and exclusions. (SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §9.1 “Company Transaction Expenses,” Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)
- Because Working Capital is not defined/mechanized, GAAP governance does not translate into a clean “completion accounts” adjustment framework. (SPA §1.2(b) and §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### Market Comparison

- Market “completion accounts” frameworks usually hardwire: GAAP consistency, defined line items, sample calculations, post-close true-up/dispute resolution. Here, GAAP is clear for Financial Statements and accounting consistency, but the purchase price adjustment is a **pre-close estimate + bucketed deductions + indemnity backstop** rather than a GAAP-driven true-up. (SPA §2.6(a), Agreement p. 6 / PDF p. 10; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)

# Additional Requested Analysis

## 1) Overall Assessment: Buyer-friendly or Seller-friendly financial framework?

Overall, the financial framework is **mixed / bespoke**:

- **Buyer-protective elements:** The deal economics explicitly deduct Estimated Indebtedness and Estimated Company Transaction Expenses from the cash paid to Sellers, and also reduce price for certain Buyer-funded Closing payments (with a no-double-count construct). (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6) Buyer also has a meaningful indemnity backstop for pre-Closing Indebtedness / Transaction Expenses / DN Advisory Fee not included in the §1.3(b) computation. (SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)
- **Seller-friendly elements:** Several specific obligations are explicitly carved out of the “Estimated Indebtedness” calculation and are stated to be paid by the Company after Closing. (SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §6.2(d)(i), Agreement p. 25 / PDF p. 29) And several named advisor fees are explicitly excluded from “Company Transaction Expenses” and are to be paid by the Company as ordinary-course payables. (SPA §9.1 “Company Transaction Expenses,” Agreement pp. 34–35 / PDF pp. 38–39)

## 2) The Actual Purchase Price Equation (as implemented at Closing)

### 2.1 Headline Purchase Price (defined term)

- **Purchase Price = $26,381,788.98**. (SPA §9.1 “Purchase Price”, Agreement p. 39 / PDF p. 43)

### 2.2 Base Purchase Price (cash wired to Sellers)

Buyer pays Sellers (allocated pro rata per Schedule I) an amount equal to:

**Base Purchase Price = Purchase Price**
**− Estimated Indebtedness**
**− Estimated Company Transaction Expenses**
**− DN Advisory Fee**
**− Preferred Stock Redemption Amount**
**− (without double-counting amounts in clauses (i)–(iii)) amounts paid by Buyer pursuant to §6.2(d) or §6.2(i)**

(Formula stated in SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

## 3) Closing Payment Flows (Step-by-Step Funds Flow)

### Pre-Closing (inputs to the funds flow)

1. **Wire instructions / allocation setup**

- Sellers’ Representative designates Sellers’ wire accounts at least **3 Business Days** prior; Buyer pays Sellers pro rata per Schedule I allocation. (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

2. **Estimated Closing Statement delivered**

- At least **1 Business Day** prior to Closing, Sellers’ Representative delivers the **Estimated Closing Statement** containing:
  - (i) **Estimated Indebtedness** (with explicit carve-outs)
  - (ii) **Estimated Company Transaction Expenses** (unpaid as of immediately prior to Closing)
  - (iii) **DN Advisory Fee**
  - (iv) Amount to be paid to Preferred holders per §6.2(i)
    (SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

3. **“Closing of the books”**

- Sellers use **reasonable best efforts** to cause a full balance sheet closing on the Closing Date “as if it were the last day of a fiscal period.” (SPA §1.3(a), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

### At Closing (actual cash movements and deliverables)

4. **Sellers deliver Shares**

- Sellers deliver share certificate(s) representing all Shares, duly endorsed / with stock powers and transfer stamps. (SPA §1.2(a), Agreement p. 1 / PDF p. 5)

5. **Buyer wires net proceeds to Sellers (Base Purchase Price)**

- Buyer wires the **Base Purchase Price** to Sellers (pro rata per Schedule I). (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

6. **Buyer pays Preferred redemption (on behalf of the Company)**

- Buyer pays the **Preferred Stock Redemption Amount** to each Preferred shareholder, in amounts on Schedule II. (SPA §1.2(c), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
- The Preferred Stock must be redeemed **immediately prior to Closing** for **$923,077** using Buyer funds; Preferred holders must deliver releases/waivers. (SPA §6.2(i), Agreement p. 26 / PDF p. 30)

7. **Buyer pays DN Partners (DN Advisory Fee)**

- Buyer pays DN Partners, LLC an amount equal to the **DN Advisory Fee**. (SPA §1.2(d), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
- DN Advisory Fee is defined (and capped at **$710,000**) as accrued/unpaid amounts under specified DN Partners letter agreements, including transaction-triggered amounts. (SPA §9.1 “DN Advisory Fee”, Agreement p. 35 / PDF p. 39)

8. **Buyer-funded payoffs / terminations to deliver a “clean” closing**

- As a condition to Buyer’s obligation, the Company must satisfy/discharge obligations under specified agreements, and the SPA states amounts needed to satisfy payment obligations “shall be satisfied with funds provided by Buyer at the Closing,” and that those funds reduce the Purchase Price. (SPA §6.2(d) (x), Agreement p. 25 / PDF p. 29)

- Any payment under the SPA is net of required withholding; withheld amounts are remitted and treated as paid. (SPA §1.4, Agreement p. 3 / PDF p. 7)

## B) Base Purchase Price (Net Proceeds Wired to Sellers)

**Summary (plain English)**
The Base Purchase Price is the **net cash wired to Sellers** at Closing. It is calculated by starting with the Purchase Price and subtracting: (i) Estimated Indebtedness, (ii) Estimated Company Transaction Expenses, (iii) DN Advisory Fee, (iv) Preferred Stock Redemption Amount, and (v) certain additional Buyer-funded payments made under §6.2(d) or §6.2(i) (subject to a “no double counting” rule vs clauses (i)–(iii)). (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

**Bias Rating**
🟢 **Mixed**

- Buyer-favorable because it explicitly strips out debt-like and deal-cost leakage from Sellers’ proceeds. (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
- Seller-favorable in execution because the estimate inputs come from Sellers’ Representative and there is no Article 1 post-close reconciliation process. (SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

**Key Observations**

- **Pro rata allocations** to Sellers follow Schedule I (not included in the visible text of this PDF), making the Seller distribution rules a referenced-but-not-visible dependency. (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
- Clause (v) is intended to prevent **double counting** of amounts already captured as Indebtedness / Transaction Expenses / DN Advisory Fee. (SPA §1.2(b)(v), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
- Drafting oddity: clause (v)’s “without double-counting” only references clauses **(i)-(iii)** (not clause (iv)), even though clause (v) includes payments under §6.2(i) and clause (iv) separately deducts the Preferred Stock Redemption Amount. (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §6.2(i), Agreement p. 26 / PDF p. 30)

**Double-Count Risks (practical)**

- **Preferred redemption double-count ambiguity**: Preferred redemption is deducted explicitly in clause (iv), and §6.2(i) is referenced again in clause (v) without an express carve-out for clause (iv). (SPA §1.2(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §6.2(i), Agreement p. 26 / PDF p. 30)
- **Debt payoff overlap**: If indebtedness payoffs are made under §6.2(d)(i), those could overlap with “Estimated Indebtedness” (clause (i)); clause (v) is meant to avoid double counting but requires careful mapping in the funds flow. (SPA §1.2(b)(i) and (v), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §6.2(d)(i), Agreement p. 25 / PDF p. 29)
- **DN fee overlap with termination payments**: DN Advisory Fee (clause (iii)) is defined around DN Partners letter agreements, which are separately terminated under §6.2(d)(iii)-(iv) for $650k and $60k. Correct classification is essential to avoid deducting the same DN-related amounts twice. (SPA §1.2(b)(iii) and (v), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §6.2(d)(iii)-(iv), Agreement pp. 25–26 / PDF pp. 29–30; SPA §9.1 “DN Advisory Fee”, Agreement p. 35 / PDF p. 39)

**Gap Risks**

- If estimates understate the deduction buckets, Buyer’s immediate remedy is not a post-close price true-up in Article 1; instead Buyer may need to rely on indemnity (see §8.2(c)). (SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)

**Market Comparison**

- Market completion-account deals typically include a **post-close closing statement and dispute process**. Here, Article 1’s mechanics are effectively **pre-close estimate + deduction waterfall**, supplemented by an indemnity backstop. (SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6; SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)

## G) Indemnity Backstop for Missed Deductions (Substitute for True-Up)

**Summary (plain English)**
If Indebtedness, Company Transaction Expenses, or the DN Advisory Fee exists immediately prior to Closing but was **not included** in the §1.3(b) computation, Sellers indemnify Buyer for that shortfall. This is a key “economic backstop” given the absence of a post-close true-up in Article 1. (SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)

**Bias Rating**
🟠 **Buyer-Favorable**

**Key Observations**

- The indemnity is explicitly tied to **what was or wasn’t included** in the §1.3(b) computation. (SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

**Double-Count Risks**

- Potential for disputes about whether an item was “included” in the computation, especially where bucket boundaries are fuzzy (e.g., lender fees vs transaction expenses vs indebtedness fees). (SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)

**Gap Risks**

- The backstop only covers the three categories listed (Company Transaction Expenses, Indebtedness immediately prior to Closing, DN Advisory Fee) and only to the extent tied to the §1.3(b) computation. (SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33)

**Market Comparison**

- Market completion accounts deals solve this via a closing statement / true-up process; this SPA instead uses indemnity as the correction mechanism. (SPA §8.2(c), Agreement p. 29 / PDF p. 33; SPA §1.3(b), Agreement p. 2 / PDF p. 6)
