Definition 3: Indebtedness / Debt

Definition Name: “Indebtedness”

Section Reference: Section 6.1, PDF p.56–57

Full Verbatim Text:

> “Indebtedness: means, with respect to any Person, without duplication: (a) all obligations of such Person for borrowed money; (b) all obligations of such Person evidenced by notes, bonds, debentures or other similar instruments; (c) all obligations of such Person under conditional sale or other title retention agreements relating to any property purchased by such Person (other than customary reservation of title provisions included in the terms and conditions of purchase); (d) all obligations of such Person incurred, issued or assumed as the deferred purchase price of property or services, including (without limitation) “capital lease” obligations; (e) all obligations of such Person under acceptance, letter of credit or similar facilities; (f) all obligations of such Person to purchase, redeem, retire or otherwise acquire for value any capital stock of such Person, including, in the case of redeemable preferred stock, the greater of its liquidation preference or its maximum fixed redemption price, in each case plus accrued and unpaid dividends; and (g) all obligations of the type referred to in clauses (a) through (f) of other Persons for the payment of which such Person is responsible or liable directly or indirectly as obligor, guarantor, surety or otherwise, including guarantees of such obligations, and including such obligations of other Persons secured by any Lien on the property of such Person. For purposes hereof, Indebtedness shall not include any defaulted Indebtedness assigned to Seller pursuant to Section 3.7.”

Plain-English Summary:
This is a defined bucket for debt-like obligations, covering borrowed money, debt instruments, certain deferred purchase price and capital lease obligations, and letter of credit facilities, plus guarantees and obligations secured on the target’s assets. It explicitly excludes defaulted loan items that Seller is allowed to take out via Section 3.7 (so those won’t be treated as “Indebtedness” for other purposes post-assignment).

3.1 Price Equation

Reconstructed economic formula (based on Sections 1.2, 1.5–1.7, 3.7, 6.7)

[Base Preliminary Cash Payment]
= $348,937,500 (includes Targeted Net Capital)

- (Estimated Net Capital − Targeted Net Capital) adjustment at Closing

- (Final Net Capital − Estimated Net Capital) true-up post-Closing (with interest at Applicable Rate)
  − Shortfall adjustment for Closed Foreign Accounts (up to $10,000,000 reduction to Preliminary Cash Payment)
  − Reduction for defaulted Indebtedness assigned to Seller under Section 3.7
  = Final Closing Cash for shares (excluding later earnout)

- [Contingent Payment]
  = $15,000,000 × Applicable Percentage (pro rata)
  subject to reduction of maximum Contingent Payment for Shortfall amounts over $10,000,000 (but not below $5,000,000)
  = Total consideration

Part 22: Suggested Revisions

Revision 1: Tighten Buyer Discretion in Contingent Payment

Current Language (Section 6.7.3, PDF p.71):

> “Buyer shall have the sole discretion, based on good faith business judgment, to refuse to enter into any agreement… that might lead to Qualifying Revenue…”

Issue:
This gives Buyer a broad veto over revenue that drives the contingent payment, materially increasing seller’s earnout risk.

Suggested Revision:
Replace with:
“Buyer shall operate the Business in the ordinary course consistent with past practice and shall not take any action, or omit to take any action, the primary purpose of which is to reduce the Contingent Payment. Buyer retains discretion to make business decisions in good faith; provided that Buyer shall use commercially reasonable efforts to maintain the Customer Accounts and the revenue streams that generate Qualifying Revenue.”

Rationale:
Balances buyer operational freedom with meaningful earnout protection.

Likely Pushback:
Buyer will argue it must retain discretion for regulatory/commercial reasons.

Fallback Position:
Limit covenant to “no bad faith / no primary purpose to reduce earnout” and add objective documentation requirements for customer terminations and product changes.

Revision 2: Clarify Shortfall determination and dispute routing

Current Language (Section 1.5.3, PDF p.8):

> “if any Customer Accounts have been closed as a result of… not being in compliance… agreed between Buyer and Seller…”

Issue:
Causation (“as a result of”) is subjective; schedule defining foreign accounts is missing in provided PDF; no explicit dispute mechanism.

Suggested Revision:
Add:

- Defined criteria for “Closed Foreign Account” and “non-compliance”
- Provide that disputes on classification and revenue computations go to the Accounting Firm under a defined timetable (mirroring 6.7.2).

Fallback:
At minimum, add a data package requirement and explicit accountant resolution process.

Revision 3: Constrain Schedule 1.6.1 override scope

Current Language (1.6.2, PDF p.8–9):

> “pre-closing adjustments agreed on Schedule 1.6.1… notwithstanding… may not be in accordance with GAAP…”

Issue:
Schedule-driven overrides can reallocate value without clear boundaries.

Suggested Revision:
Require Schedule 1.6.1 to specify: line item, rationale, direction/sign, and whether it overrides GAAP and/or Rule 15c3-1, and include an example.

Fallback:
Attach final Schedule 1.6.1 to the agreement and require mutual written amendment for any changes.
