#### Format Recommendations

**What senior people actually read (and why):**

1. **One-page “Decision Summary” (they read this)**

- **Best for:** Partner glance, client sponsor/CFO, deal lead
- **Format:** Tight bullets + a small table
- **Content that lands:**
  - **Top 5–10 issues** (only the ones that change _price, structure, timing, or go/no-go_)
  - **Your recommendation** (what to push for / accept / trade)
  - **Deal actions needed this week** (who needs to do what)

A simple structure that works:

- **Headline:** “SPA review – issues that impact value, closing certainty, and post-close leakage”
- **3 bullets:** What’s good / what’s risky / what needs a decision
- **Top Issues table:** Severity, clause ref, impact, recommended ask, owner

---

2. **Issue Log / Risk Register table (they scan this)**

- **Best for:** Engagement lead, deal team, partner who wants detail fast
- **Format:** A sortable table (Excel-style even if delivered in PPT/Word)
- **Why it works:** It’s skimmable, comparable, and forces clarity.

**Minimum viable columns that actually matter:**

- **Issue** (one-line headline)
- **Clause ref** (section + page)
- **Why it matters** (value / timing / control / liability)
- **Financial/operational impact** (quantify or bracket)
- **Recommended action** (exact negotiation ask or mitigation)
- **Timing** (pre-sign / pre-close / post-close)
- **Owner** (Legal / Finance / Tax / Ops)
- **Severity** (R/A/G or 1–5)

---

3. **“Asks & Trades” list (clients love this)**

- **Best for:** Negotiation prep, legal + business alignment
- **Format:** Bullets grouped by theme (purchase price & adjustments, indemnities, covenants, closing conditions, termination)
- **Why it works:** Converts analysis into negotiation posture.

Example:

- **Must-have asks (non-negotiable)**
- **Should-have asks (strong preference)**
- **Give-to-get trades (acceptable concessions if we get X)**

---

4. **Clause matrix / markup appendix (almost nobody reads, but everyone wants it to exist)**

- **Best for:** Associates, legal counsel, audit trail, “prove it” moments
- **Format:** Matrix + brief commentary + extracts
- **Use:** Backup, not the main deliverable.

✅ **Rule of thumb:** Put _inventory_ in the appendix, keep the _story_ up front.

#### Length Guidelines

**By audience (what gets read vs ignored):**

| Audience                            | What they realistically consume              |                            Ideal length | Detail level                               |
| ----------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------: | ------------------------------------------ |
| **Partner glance**                  | Executive summary + top issues               |                            **½–1 page** | Only issues that change value/terms/timing |
| **Engagement lead / deal captain**  | Exec summary + full issue log                |                           **2–4 pages** | Enough detail to run workstreams           |
| **Client sponsor (CFO/CEO)**        | Decisions + trade-offs + actions             |                           **1–2 pages** | Business impact, not legal commentary      |
| **Client legal counsel**            | Issue log + clause refs + suggested language |                           **3–8 pages** | Clause-level precision, redline-ready asks |
| **Associates / internal deep-dive** | Full inventory + notes + extracts            | **10–25+ pages** (often appendix-heavy) | Exhaustive, but structured and searchable  |

**Practical packaging that works well:**

- **Page 1:** Decision Summary (always)
- **Pages 2–4:** Issue Log (prioritized)
- **Appendix:** Clause inventory + excerpts + detailed rationale

#### Actionable vs Informational

**What makes findings actionable (the difference clients pay for):**

Actionable findings answer **“So what?” + “Now what?”** in the same breath.

**Informational (weak):**

- “The SPA includes a broad indemnity cap.”
- “There is a material adverse change clause.”

**Actionable (strong):**

- “**Indemnity cap is uncapped for X** → could create tail liability beyond model. **Recommendation:** cap at [range] or tie to purchase price; add basket/deductible; limit survival to [months]. **Owner:** Legal. **Timing:** pre-sign.”

**Checklist to force actionability (use this every time):**

- **Decision impact:** Does this affect **price, structure, timing, or go/no-go**?
- **Impact sizing:** Can you quantify (even roughly) or bracket?
  - “Expected value range,” “worst-case exposure,” “cash timing,” “working capital volatility,” etc.

- **Recommendation:** One clear sentence: **“Do X because Y.”**
- **Negotiation language:** Provide the **ask** (or sample clause language) when feasible.
- **Workplan hook:** **Who owns it** and **when** it must be resolved (pre-sign/pre-close/post-close).
- **Fallback position:** What’s acceptable if counterparty won’t move? (trade list)

**A useful template sentence:**

> “Because **[clause]** creates **[risk]** with **[impact]**, we recommend **[specific ask / mitigation]** by **[timing]**, owned by **[person/team]**.”

#### Severity Communication

**Visual/structural approaches that are actually effective:**

1. **RAG with explicit definitions (don’t leave it subjective)**

- **Red = Deal / value / closing certainty changer**
- **Amber = Material but manageable with mitigation**
- **Green = Market / informational / monitor**

Put the definition in a tiny legend once and stick to it.

---

2. **“Timing-based severity” is extremely intuitive**
   A lot of SPA risk is about _when_ it bites.

- **Pre-sign required** (cannot proceed without fix)
- **Pre-close required** (closing condition / bring-down risk)
- **Post-close** (integration / operational mitigation)
- **Monitor** (track but don’t negotiate hard)

This is often clearer than likelihood scoring for M&A teams.

---

3. **Likelihood × Impact matrix (use sparingly, but great for committees)**

- **Best for:** IC/board-style discussions
- **Avoid:** Overengineering the math

Simple 2×2 or 3×3 works. The key is **consistency**, not false precision.

---

4. **Design cues that work without being cheesy**

- **Left-hand “severity bar”** on each issue row (thin colored strip)
- **Icons**: ⛔ (deal stopper), ⚠️ (material), ✅ (clean)
- **Sorting:** Always sort by **severity then timing**, not by SPA section order
- **Callouts:** One “Deal stoppers / Must-fix” box at the top

**One high-impact move:** Add a column called **“Client decision needed?”** (Yes/No). Executives gravitate to it immediately.

#### Common Presentation Mistakes

**The biggest mistake analysts make:**
✅ **They summarize the contract instead of translating it into deal consequences and decisions.**

What that looks like in the wild:

- Long prose explaining what a clause says
- No quantified impact
- No recommended ask
- No owner/timing
- No prioritization (everything sounds equally important)

**Other frequent mistakes to avoid:**

- **Burying the lede:** The real issue is on page 9 of the deck.
- **No clause references:** If you can’t cite section/page, trust drops fast.
- **Inconsistent severity:** If you call five items “critical,” none are.
- **No “ask” language:** Findings without negotiation posture = informational noise.
- **Organizing by SPA order:** That’s how lawyers read; deal teams read by **impact**.
- **Overstuffed tables:** Too many columns, tiny font, no hierarchy.
- **No “what changed vs market” framing:** Clients care whether it’s off-market and worth fighting.

**A simple test:**
If a partner can’t skim your output in **60 seconds** and say, “Here are the top 3 issues and what we’re doing about them,” the format is wrong.
