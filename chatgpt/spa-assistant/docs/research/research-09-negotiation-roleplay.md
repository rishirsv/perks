#### Standard Arguments by Topic

> **Context note (so you can roleplay accurately):** what’s “standard” depends on auction vs. bilateral, sponsor vs. strategic, leverage in process, size/industry, and whether there’s RWI (rep & warranty insurance). The points below are the _most common_ buyer/seller scripts you’ll hear in North American private-company SPAs. 🙂

---

### 1) Definitions, Interpretation, Materiality, Knowledge

**Buyer position (standard arguments)**

- **Tight, buyer-friendly definitions** (e.g., “Material Adverse Effect” carved narrowly, “Indebtedness” broad, “Transaction Expenses” broad).
- **Pro-buyer interpretation mechanics**: “including” = “including without limitation”; broad affiliate definitions; broad “Liens” and “Losses.”
- **Materiality/MAE**: minimize “materiality” qualifiers in reps and covenants so breaches are actionable.
- **Knowledge**: prefer **actual + constructive** knowledge and knowledge of a larger group (officers, key managers).
- **Anti-sandbagging?** Buyer prefers **pro-sandbagging** (can claim even if aware) or silence (varies by jurisdiction).

**Seller position (standard arguments)**

- **Definitions that avoid double counting** (e.g., don’t count the same item in net debt and working capital and as a Transaction Expense).
- **MAE broader** / more protective: include industry-wide and macro carveouts; limit what counts as MAE.
- **Materiality & qualifiers**: keep “material” qualifiers and knowledge qualifiers to reduce strict liability.
- **Knowledge**: prefer **actual knowledge only**, limited to named individuals; exclude constructive knowledge.
- **Sandbagging**: seller prefers **anti-sandbagging** (no claim if buyer knew) or at least “no duty to investigate” clarity.

---

### 2) Deal Structure: Share vs Asset; Locked Box vs Completion Accounts

**Buyer**

- **Structure for risk control**: wants protections around unknown liabilities (often prefers asset deal where viable; or a share deal with heavier indemnity).
- **Completion accounts / closing adjustment**: argues fairness—price should reflect what actually transfers at closing.

**Seller**

- **Structure for simplicity & tax**: pushes structure that’s cleanest for seller and closing certainty.
- **Locked box (if used)**: argues it gives seller price certainty; leakage protections are enough.

---

### 3) Purchase Price Mechanics: Cash/Stock; Working Capital; Net Debt; Transaction Expenses

**Buyer**

- **Net debt “dollar-for-dollar”**: all debt-like items included; all cash included; avoid “cash-free/debt-free” ambiguity.
- **Working capital adjustment**: peg to a **normalized** target; strict GAAP/IFRS-based; buyer wants detailed accounting principles hierarchy.
- **Transaction expenses**: broad definition, deducted from purchase price (or treated as debt-like).
- **Cash consideration protection**: escrow/holdback to secure indemnity and price adjustments.

**Seller**

- **Narrow net debt**: exclude “operating” items; carve out lease liabilities (depending), customer deposits, or “ordinary course” accruals.
- **Working capital peg favorable**: peg reflects seller’s preferred seasonality; more flexibility in accounting policies.
- **Transaction expenses**: limit to third-party deal costs; exclude internal allocations; avoid double counting.
- **Minimize post-close fights**: prefer locked box or simplified true-up.

---

### 4) Earnouts / Contingent Consideration

**Buyer**

- **Earnout as risk bridge**: “If you believe the forecast, you should accept performance-based upside.”
- **Control & discretion**: buyer wants strong operational control post-close, limited affirmative covenants, broad discretion on integration.
- **Tight definitions**: clear metric definitions, GAAP consistency, dispute process; caps on seller audit rights.

**Seller**

- **Earnout = “we’re financing you”**: wants protections against buyer “gaming” the metric.
- **Operating covenants**: at least “commercially reasonable efforts” to achieve earnout; limits on reallocation of costs, transfer pricing changes, synergies allocation.
- **Transparency**: reporting, access, and dispute mechanisms that are usable.

---

### 5) Closing Conditions and Bring-Down Standards

**Buyer**

- **Robust conditions**: accuracy of reps (often MAE/materiality bring-down), no MAE, required consents, no injunction, regulatory approvals.
- **Walk rights**: wants clear outs if risks emerge.

**Seller**

- **Limit conditions**: bring-down standard should be **“true in all material respects”** and MAE-only for most reps.
- **Reduce buyer discretion**: minimize subjective conditions; push for “hell or high water” where buyer controls approvals.

---

### 6) Interim Operating Covenants (Between Signing and Closing)

**Buyer**

- **Tight operating covenants**: seller must operate “in the ordinary course” and get buyer consent for key actions (capex, hiring, pricing, contracts).
- **Information rights**: access, weekly updates, consultation.

**Seller**

- **Operational freedom**: “We can’t run the business by committee.”
- **Consent not unreasonably withheld/delayed**: thresholds and carveouts; emergency actions allowed.

---

### 7) Reps & Warranties (Scope, Qualifiers, Schedules)

**Buyer**

- **Broad reps**: financial statements, absence of undisclosed liabilities, compliance with laws, tax, IP, employment, cybersecurity/privacy, anti-corruption, sanctions, environmental (industry-dependent).
- **Limited qualifiers**: fewer knowledge/materiality qualifiers; “10b-5”/full disclosure rep sometimes requested (“no omission”) in some contexts.
- **Disclosure schedules**: complete and specific; no “catch-all” disclosures.

**Seller**

- **Narrow reps**: “We’ll tell you what we know; don’t make us insurers.”
- **Qualifiers**: knowledge/materiality; “to the extent required” language for compliance reps; limit “no undisclosed liabilities.”
- **Schedules**: allow general disclosures and data room referencing (common); cap burden of updates.

---

### 8) Indemnification (Caps, Baskets, Survival, Procedures)

**Buyer**

- **Higher caps and longer survival**: meaningful protection; push for:
  - **Fundamental reps** (title, authority, capitalization, taxes sometimes) = higher cap / longer survival
  - **General reps** = moderate cap, 12–24 months survival (varies)

- **Basket**: prefers **tipping/first-dollar** after threshold, not deductible.
- **Special indemnities**: known issues carved out with separate cap/survival.
- **Procedures**: buyer-friendly control of third-party claims; broad definition of Losses including consequential (sometimes).

**Seller**

- **Low cap / short survival**: “You diligenced; price reflects risk.”
- **Deductible basket**: only amounts above threshold paid.
- **Exclusive remedy**: indemnity is the sole remedy; limit equitable relief.
- **Limit Losses**: exclude consequential, punitive, multiple of damages; mitigate; net of insurance and tax benefits.

---

### 9) Escrow / Holdback / Security

**Buyer**

- **Escrow is non-negotiable** (unless strong seller credit/RWI): ensures collectability.
- **Size and duration**: wants enough to cover cap and survival periods.

**Seller**

- **Minimize amount and term**: cash now; avoid “trapped” value.
- **RWI replacement**: if buyer wants protection, use insurance instead of escrow.

---

### 10) Rep & Warranty Insurance (RWI)

**Buyer**

- **Use RWI to bridge gaps**: allows seller-friendly indemnity terms while still protecting buyer.
- **Seller pays?** Buyer may ask seller to fund part of premium (context-driven).

**Seller**

- **Clean exit**: pushes RWI to reduce escrow/cap and shorten survival.
- **Limit seller exposure to “fundamental + covenants + special indemnities only.”**

---

### 11) Tax Matters (pre-close taxes, audits, filings)

**Buyer**

- **Tax indemnity**: seller covers pre-close taxes; control over tax audits; cooperation obligations.
- **Transfer taxes**: allocate to seller or shared; buyer wants clarity.

**Seller**

- **Reasonable control**: seller wants a say in audits affecting them; limit indemnity to taxes of the target (not buyer group).
- **Caps/limitations**: align tax claims with indemnity framework unless truly special.

---

### 12) Employees, Benefits, Pensions, Termination/Severance

**Buyer**

- **Limit inherited liabilities**: accurate benefit/pension reps; no undisclosed liabilities; compliance.
- **Post-close flexibility**: no requirement to maintain specific plans; only comply with law/agreements.
- **Restrictive covenants for key talent**: non-solicit, sometimes non-compete.

**Seller**

- **Practical transition**: reasonable continuation of benefits; protect employee morale; clear allocation of pre-close obligations.
- **Avoid broad post-close obligations**: don’t lock buyer into wage/benefit commitments.

---

### 13) Restrictive Covenants (Non-compete / Non-solicit)

**Buyer**

- **Strong non-compete**: duration/territory broad enough to protect goodwill and price paid.
- **Non-solicit**: employees/customers, longer periods.

**Seller**

- **Reasonableness & enforceability**: narrower scope, shorter duration; carveouts for passive investments and existing lines.
- **Jurisdiction reality**: many places scrutinize non-competes; seller uses enforceability as leverage.

---

### 14) Conditions Around Consents, Change of Control, and Third-Party Contracts

**Buyer**

- **Consents as conditions**: “We’re buying the contracts; if they’re not transferable, economics change.”
- **Full disclosure**: all change-of-control triggers and required consents listed.

**Seller**

- **Material contract threshold**: only “material” consents should be conditions; best efforts not guarantees.
- **Risk sharing**: if a consent can’t be obtained, propose alternative (novation, subcontract, etc.).

---

### 15) Termination Rights, Reverse Break Fees, Specific Performance

**Buyer**

- **Ability to walk** if conditions unmet; limit seller’s specific performance rights (especially if financing risk exists).
- If seller asks for reverse break fee: buyer wants it **capped** and as **exclusive remedy**.

**Seller**

- **Closing certainty**: wants specific performance or a meaningful reverse break fee.
- **Tight “outs”**: prevent buyer from using minor issues to re-trade.

---

### 16) Dispute Resolution, Governing Law, Forum

**Buyer**

- Picks favorable forum/law; wants efficient resolution for purchase price adjustments (expert determination) and indemnity (courts/arbitration).
- Prefers buyer-friendly interim relief rights.

**Seller**

- Neutral forum; arbitration for confidentiality; tighter timelines/cost shifting rules.

#### Common Fallbacks

Below are the _usual_ concessions each side makes when they can’t “win” outright—useful for simulating realistic endgames. ✅

### Purchase price adjustment (WC/Net Debt/Expenses)

**Buyer commonly concedes to**

- Higher working capital peg (or collar around peg).
- Narrowing “debt-like items” list (exclude truly operating accruals).
- Tightening accounting principles to reduce ambiguity.

**Seller commonly concedes to**

- Accept a true-up but with:
  - clear principles hierarchy (GAAP/IFRS + consistent practices),
  - materiality thresholds/collars,
  - independent accountant dispute process with limited scope.

---

### Earnout

**Buyer commonly concedes to**

- Some guardrails: consistency, no intentional actions primarily to avoid earnout, reporting/audit rights.
- A “neutral” dispute accountant mechanism.

**Seller commonly concedes to**

- Buyer retains primary operational control (no “run it to hit earnout” handcuffs).
- Shorter earnout period, caps on payouts, and limited audit intrusiveness.

---

### Reps & warranties (scope/qualifiers)

**Buyer commonly concedes to**

- Knowledge qualifiers on certain operational reps.
- Materiality qualifiers _if_ paired with **materiality scrape** for indemnity (see trade-offs).
- Dropping “10b-5/no omissions” rep in many private deals.

**Seller commonly concedes to**

- Broader “fundamental reps” without qualifiers.
- More detailed schedules and a cleaner disclosure approach (less “general data room” reliance).

---

### Indemnity cap / basket / survival

**Buyer commonly concedes to**

- Lower general cap if:
  - escrow is meaningful, and/or
  - RWI is used, and/or
  - fundamental/tax/special indemnities remain strong.

- Deductible basket instead of tipping (sometimes).

**Seller commonly concedes to**

- Higher cap for fundamental reps and specific known risks.
- Longer survival for tax/fundamental; standard survival for general reps (often 12–18 months) if price is strong.

---

### Escrow / security

**Buyer commonly concedes to**

- Smaller escrow if RWI is in place.
- Faster releases (staged releases) and tighter claim notice mechanics.

**Seller commonly concedes to**

- Some escrow if it’s:
  - small, time-limited,
  - released in tranches,
  - and buyer accepts limitations on claims.

---

### Interim covenants

**Buyer commonly concedes to**

- Higher consent thresholds and “not unreasonably withheld/delayed.”
- Emergency and ordinary-course carveouts.

**Seller commonly concedes to**

- Notice + consultation rights even if consent not required.
- “Ordinary course consistent with past practice” language.

---

### Non-compete / non-solicit

**Buyer commonly concedes to**

- Narrower geography/duration; focus on non-solicit + confidentiality if non-compete is hard to enforce.

**Seller commonly concedes to**

- Stronger non-solicit of customers/employees; limited non-compete for key principals.

---

#### Typical Trade-Offs

These are the “package deals” that actually close SPAs. 💼🤝

1. **Price vs. Protection**

- Seller: “Higher price, but lower cap/shorter survival.”
- Buyer: “Lower price if you want a clean exit.”

2. **Cap vs. Basket**

- “If you want a lower cap, give me a tipping basket/materiality scrape.”
- “If you insist on a deductible basket, cap must come up.”

3. **Escrow vs. RWI**

- “We’ll reduce escrow dramatically if we do RWI.”
- “If seller won’t stand behind reps, buyer needs either escrow or RWI.”

4. **Materiality scrape in exchange for qualifiers**

- Seller keeps materiality qualifiers in reps,
- Buyer gets a **materiality scrape** for indemnity calculations (i.e., qualifiers ignored when determining damages), sometimes with tailored carveouts.

5. **Survival vs. Scope of reps**

- “We’ll accept broader reps if survival is shorter.”
- “If survival is longer, reps must be tighter.”

6. **Earnout guardrails vs. Operational freedom**

- Buyer: “We’ll add reporting and non-manipulation covenants, but no operational veto rights.”
- Seller: “We’ll accept your control, but need protections against obvious gaming.”

7. **Interim covenants vs. Closing certainty**

- Seller: “Fewer covenants if buyer commits to close (tight termination rights / specific performance).”
- Buyer: “More operating control if seller wants closing certainty.”

8. **Consents condition vs. price adjustment**

- “If a key consent isn’t obtained, either (a) closing condition, or (b) price reduction / escrow for contract-loss risk.”

9. **Known issue (special indemnity) vs. overall cap**

- Seller: “We’ll give you a special indemnity for the known issue, but overall cap comes down.”

10. **Reverse break fee vs. financing outs**

- Seller: “If you want a financing out, reverse break fee must be meaningful.”
- Buyer: “If reverse break fee is the only remedy, keep it capped.”

#### Posturing vs Red Lines

This is _the_ skill gap in most first-time negotiators. Here’s how experienced teams tell the difference. 🧠

### What posturing looks like

- **“Market” without proof**: “This is market” but they won’t cite credible comps or explain _why_.
- **Extreme opening anchors**: early positions that are obviously unworkable, meant to reset the midpoint.
- **Inconsistent prioritization**: they fight hard on a point one day, then drop it when a bigger issue comes up.
- **Conditional language**: “We can’t” becomes “we strongly prefer” once you offer a package deal.
- **They concede in small increments**: classic “concession choreography” to look tough.

### What genuine red lines look like

- **Tied to a real constraint**
  - sponsor investment committee policy,
  - lender requirements,
  - RWI underwriting,
  - board directive,
  - regulatory risk,
  - internal precedent in that business line.

- **They’re willing to pay for it**
  A true red line often comes with a willingness to trade value elsewhere (“If we get this, we’ll move on X and Y.”).
- **Consistency over time and across people**
  - same message from counsel + deal lead + principal,
  - repeated in writing,
  - not walked back casually.

- **They escalate early**
  - “I need to take this to IC/Board” is often a sign it’s real (not always, but often).

### Practical ways to test in real time (great for roleplay too)

- **Ask for the “because.”**
  “Help me understand the constraint behind that—policy, lender, insurance, or preference?”
- **Offer a near-equivalent alternative.**
  If they reject _all_ substitutes, it’s more likely a red line.
- **Propose a trade.**
  Postures soften when a deal is available; red lines remain even with a reasonable trade unless you address the underlying constraint.
- **Use a “three-option menu.”**
  “We can solve this via (A) cap, (B) escrow, or (C) RWI—what’s workable?” If they refuse the entire category, it’s probably structural.

#### Roleplay Structure

If you want this to _actually_ prepare someone (not just entertain), structure it like a flight simulator: clear objectives, realistic constraints, timed decisions, and a brutally honest debrief. ✈️

### 1) Pre-brief (10–15 min)

Give each side a one-pager:

- **Deal context**: auction vs bilateral; sponsor vs strategic; leverage points.
- **Objectives**: target outcomes by topic (cap, basket, survival, escrow, earnout terms, covenants).
- **BATNA** (best alternative): what happens if you don’t sign? walk-away price/terms.
- **True red lines** (2–4 max) + **nice-to-haves**.
- **Authority limits**: what each roleplayer can agree without “calling the principal.”

**Tip:** Make the “principal” role real—someone who can be called once or twice to approve exceptions. That forces realistic escalation.

---

### 2) Round-based negotiation (45–90 min total)

Run 2–3 rounds with timeboxes:

- **Round 1 (Positioning / anchors)**: each side states top 5 issues and their rationale.
- **Round 2 (Packaging)**: force proposals that move **at least 3 terms together** (prevents haggling one clause at a time).
- **Round 3 (Close / clean-up)**: resolve the last 2–3 issues with explicit trades.

**Mechanic that improves learning fast:**
Require each offer to be phrased as:

> “If you want **X**, we need **Y** (and we can flex on **Z**).”

---

### 3) Inject curveballs (optional but powerful)

Add 1–2 surprises midstream:

- diligence finding (tax exposure, customer concentration, pending claim),
- consent can’t be obtained,
- financing terms tighten,
- key employee threatens to leave.

This tests whether the negotiator can _re-price risk_ without melting down.

---

### 4) Scoring and tracking (throughout)

Track in a simple grid:

- issue,
- opening ask,
- counter,
- agreed term,
- “value impact” estimate (low/med/high),
- who conceded and what they got back.

This turns vibes into learnings.

---

### 5) Debrief (15–25 min) — the most important part

Debrief in this order:

1. **Outcome vs objectives**: did you protect red lines and trade for priorities?
2. **Concession discipline**: did every concession buy something?
3. **Information gathering**: did you uncover constraints or just argue?
4. **Tone and control**: did you stay calm, precise, and credible?
5. **Next time**: 2 behaviors to keep, 2 to change.

**One rule:** no blaming “legalese.” The negotiator owns the business intent and can direct counsel.
