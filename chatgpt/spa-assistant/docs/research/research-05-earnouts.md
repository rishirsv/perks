#### Earn-Out Metrics Taxonomy

Earn-outs can be tied to **(A) financial performance**, **(B) operating KPIs**, **(C) event/milestone outcomes**, or **(D) market/exit outcomes**. Almost every earn-out you’ll see is some combination of these—what matters (and what drives disputes) is **how tightly the metric is defined**, **who controls the levers**, and **how the calculation is policed**.

Below is a practical taxonomy (with the usual buyer/seller angles and dispute profile).

---

##### A) Financial statement metrics (most common)

**1) Revenue / Net sales (recognized revenue)**

- **What it is:** GAAP/IFRS “revenue” over a period; sometimes segmented to a product/region/customer set.
- **Pros**
  - _Seller:_ Often less sensitive to discretionary cost allocations; simpler story (“did we grow?”).
  - _Buyer:_ Ties to top-line commercial success; easier to forecast.

- **Cons / dispute profile**
  - Revenue recognition policy changes, cut-off timing, returns/credits, multi-element arrangements, channel stuffing, intercompany sales, pricing/concession changes.
  - If the business is integrated, disputes flare around “whose revenue counts” and allocation.

- **When it works best:** Standalone-ish businesses, simple revenue recognition, clean segmentation.
- **Common upgrades:** “Revenue as historically prepared,” revenue recognition schedule, customer list annex, explicit treatment of returns/deferred revenue.

**2) Bookings / Billings / Backlog / Committed ARR**

- **What it is:** Contracted value rather than recognized revenue.
- **Pros**
  - _Seller:_ Less hostage to revenue timing; can be better for long implementation cycles.
  - _Buyer:_ Aligns to sales engine build; can incent pipeline generation.

- **Cons / dispute profile**
  - Definition minefield: cancellations, amendments, ramp deals, term length assumptions, renewals, “binding” vs “non-binding,” collectability, credit approvals.

- **When it works best:** B2B SaaS/recurring models where contracting is the “value creation” moment.

**3) Gross profit / Gross margin**

- **What it is:** Revenue minus cost of sales (or margin %).
- **Pros**
  - _Seller:_ Rewards profitable growth; less sensitive than EBITDA to corporate overhead.
  - _Buyer:_ Reduces incentive to “buy revenue” with discounting; protects unit economics.

- **Cons / dispute profile**
  - The _definition of COGS_ becomes the battlefield (classification and allocations).
  - Survey evidence shows earn-outs based on gross profits tend to see higher dispute rates than the broader population (likely due to cost classification subjectivity). ([Grant Thornton][1])

**4) EBITDA / EBIT / Operating income**

- **What it is:** Earnings-based measures (sometimes adjusted).
- **Pros**
  - _Seller:_ Captures holistic performance; can be “fair” if buyer gives operational support.
  - _Buyer:_ Protects against unprofitable growth; aligns with valuation multiples.

- **Cons / dispute profile**
  - **Adjusted EBITDA is a dispute magnet** because the “adjustments” are often vaguely drafted (e.g., “non-recurring” items). A Grant Thornton dispute survey notes adjusted EBITDA produces slightly more disputes than EBITDA, and highlights overreliance on “extraordinary, non-recurring, or exceptional items” (concepts without a GAAP standard; “extraordinary items” were eliminated by FASB in 2015). ([Grant Thornton][1])

- **When it works best:** Where accounting policies are stable, integration allocations are controlled, and adjustments are tightly scheduled.

**5) Net income / EPS**

- **What it is:** Bottom-line performance.
- **Pros**
  - _Buyer:_ Harder to “game” with add-backs if adjustments are disallowed; aligns with true profitability.

- **Cons / dispute profile**
  - Highly sensitive to accounting judgments (tax, depreciation/amortization policy changes, purchase accounting, management fees/allocations).
  - Can become “accounting-driven” rather than “business-driven.”

**6) Cash-based metrics (Operating cash flow, Free cash flow, cash conversion)**

- **What it is:** Cash generation rather than earnings.
- **Pros**
  - _Buyer:_ Strong anti-manipulation feature vs accrual earnings; aligns to debt paydown / liquidity.
  - _Seller:_ Can be attractive if business has clean working capital dynamics.

- **Cons / dispute profile**
  - Working capital management becomes contentious (timing of collections, payables stretching, inventory builds, capex classification).
  - Requires extremely clear definitions (especially capex vs opex; treatment of one-time integration costs).

**7) Working-capital / balance-sheet condition metrics (rare, but real)**

- **What it is:** NWC targets, net debt, leverage, liquidity covenants as triggers.
- **Pros**
  - _Buyer:_ Protects downside.

- **Cons**
  - Sellers hate being paid on “how the buyer financed/integrated the deal.”
  - Often better handled via purchase price adjustment than earn-out.

---

##### B) Operating KPIs (common in growth businesses)

**8) Customer count / active users / subscriber metrics**

- **Pros**
  - _Seller:_ Aligns to product-market traction (even before monetization).
  - _Buyer:_ Useful for pre-profit or land-grab markets.

- **Cons / dispute profile**
  - Definitions (“active”), bot/fraud filtering, bundling, migrations, account merges, churn calculation.

**9) Retention metrics (NRR/GRR, churn, cohort retention)**

- **Pros**
  - Focuses on quality of revenue and customer satisfaction.

- **Cons**
  - Heavy definition burden: contraction vs churn, upsell attribution, cross-sell from buyer, pricing changes, cohort boundaries.

**10) Unit volumes / production / throughput**

- **Pros**
  - Objective in manufacturing/commodities; less accounting judgment.

- **Cons**
  - Quality rejects, returns, channel stuffing equivalents (shipping early), “sell-in” vs “sell-through.”

**11) Market expansion KPIs (new store openings, new geographies, sales headcount, distribution doors)**

- **Pros**
  - Can directly incent a build plan.

- **Cons**
  - Seller may push expansion that the buyer views as strategically wrong; can create “growth at any cost” behavior.

**12) Product performance KPIs (uptime, latency, release cadence, SLA compliance)**

- **Pros**
  - Useful where reliability unlocks revenue.

- **Cons**
  - Measurement methodology disputes; changes to tooling/definitions.

---

##### C) Milestone / event-based (very common in life sciences, regulated industries)

**13) Regulatory approvals / clinical trial endpoints / patent issuance**

- **Pros**
  - Binary, externally-verifiable, aligns to “value inflection points.”

- **Cons / dispute profile**
  - What counts as “approval” (conditional? label restrictions?), what happens if the buyer changes development strategy, timing delays, force majeure, and whether the buyer must spend money to pursue the milestone.

**14) Key contract wins / strategic partnerships / platform launches**

- **Pros**
  - Business-relevant and observable.

- **Cons**
  - “Signed” vs “effective,” minimum term/value, termination, pricing concessions.

**15) Integration or operational milestones (systems conversion, facility build, carve-out separation, ISO certification)**

- **Pros**
  - Can de-risk execution.

- **Cons**
  - Often under buyer control → sellers demand protective covenants; buyers resist.

**16) Employment/retention milestones (“retention earn-outs”)**

- **What it is:** Payment contingent on founder/key executive staying employed for X months/years (sometimes mislabeled as “earn-out”).
- **Pros**
  - _Buyer:_ Retains talent; aligns incentives.

- **Cons**
  - Looks and feels like compensation → tax/accounting complexity; huge disputes when termination/resignation happens (“good leaver/bad leaver” fights).

---

##### D) Market / exit / equity-linked metrics (less common, but high risk)

**17) Equity price / market cap targets**

- **Pros**
  - Simple observable metric (public buyer); aligns to investor outcome.

- **Cons**
  - Can be disconnected from operating performance; influenced by macro, capital structure, buybacks, disclosure cadence.

**18) Exit-event earn-outs (sale of business, IPO, refinancing proceeds)**

- **Pros**
  - Seller participates in realized value.

- **Cons**
  - Requires careful definition of proceeds, timing, and treatment of rollover equity; disputes around transaction structuring.

---

**Reality check:** In market data, **EBITDA/earnings and revenue dominate** as earn-out metrics. One study notes roughly **50%–80%** of earn-outs use **EBITDA or revenue** as the main metric. ([Kroll][2])

---

#### Buyer Protective Terms

Buyers generally want **control, flexibility, and insulation**—because the earn-out sits in the worst zone of M&A: the buyer runs the business, but the seller gets paid based on outcomes.

**1) Control of operations / no duty to maximize**

- “Buyer may operate the business in its discretion” (with guardrails) and **no obligation to maximize the earn-out**.
- Market data suggests covenants requiring the buyer to run the business to **maximize** earn-out payments are extremely rare in modern deals.

**2) Ability to integrate**

- Explicit right to integrate systems, salesforce, branding, procurement, and shared services.
- Rights to restructure/realign strategy (especially if buyer has a platform thesis).

**3) Narrow “efforts” standard (or none)**

- If any affirmative covenant exists: **commercially reasonable efforts** (often with qualifiers like “consistent with Buyer’s business judgment”).
- Buyers hate open-ended “best efforts” or “maximize” obligations because they create fiduciary-like arguments in litigation.

**4) Accounting policy control and flexibility**

- Define earn-out statements using buyer’s GAAP/IFRS framework, sometimes with “consistently applied” language.
- Buyer prefers fewer “frozen” past-practice constraints unless the purchase price heavily depends on the earn-out.

**5) Caps, thresholds, and anti-windfall mechanics**

- Hard **cap** on earn-out.
- Minimum threshold before any payment (to avoid paying for “normal variance”).
- Exclusion of buyer synergies from the metric unless expressly included.

**6) **Set-off / offset rights** against earn-out**

- Very common: ability to offset indemnity claims or other owed amounts against earn-out payments.
- Market references show offsets are frequently included (e.g., one dataset notes over **58%** include an indemnity set-off right). ([Kroll][2])
- Another dataset shows a large majority of deals with earn-outs expressly allow offsets.

**7) Dispute resolution that keeps courts out (or narrows the scope)**

- Independent accountant decides **only accounting/math** issues.
- Short objection windows; final-and-binding determination.
- Explicit carve-out: legal/interpretation issues go to arbitration/court (buyers often want Delaware/NY; in Canada, Toronto arbitration is common).

**8) No acceleration (or narrow acceleration)**

- Buyers often resist broad acceleration triggers (especially change-of-control).
- Still, acceleration upon a change in control appears in a meaningful minority of deals (varies by dataset). ([Kroll][2])

**9) Information control / confidentiality**

- Controlled reporting cadence; restrictions on seller contacting employees/customers; limits on audit scope to avoid operational disruption.

---

#### Seller Protective Terms

Sellers generally want **definition, transparency, and protection against buyer-controlled levers**—because a “fair” earn-out on paper can become unreachable if the buyer reallocates costs, changes strategy, or changes accounting.

**1) Extremely tight definitions (and examples)**

- A worked sample calculation (even a one-page example) is one of the best dispute reducers.
- Define:
  - Accounting basis (GAAP/IFRS **as of signing/closing**),
  - Specific line items included/excluded,
  - Treatment of revenue recognition changes,
  - Treatment of purchase accounting impacts,
  - Treatment of integration/restructuring costs.

**2) “Consistency” / “past practice” protections**

- Sellers push for “consistent with past practice” on revenue recognition and cost classifications.
- Market data suggests a minority of earn-outs include an explicit covenant to run the business in accordance with seller’s past practices, but it’s not rare. ([Kroll][2])

**3) Guardrails on integration allocations and intercompany charges**

- Limits on:
  - corporate overhead allocations,
  - management fees,
  - transfer pricing,
  - shared service markups,
  - one-time integration charges being dumped into the earn-out metric.

**4) Efforts / non-sabotage covenants**

- Sellers want an affirmative covenant: buyer will use **commercially reasonable efforts** to achieve the earn-out milestones, or at minimum will not take actions primarily intended to avoid earn-out payment.
- This is a core theme in drafting guidance: courts focus heavily on whether the operational covenant is specific enough to police conduct. ([SRS Acquiom][3])

**5) Separate books and reporting**

- Segment reporting, separate P&L, clear allocation principles.
- Regular reporting cadence (monthly/quarterly) and delivery deadlines.

**6) Audit / inspection rights**

- Right to review supporting schedules, access finance personnel, and inspect records.
- A clear process for objections and resolution.

**7) Limits on set-off**

- Sellers try to prohibit offsets entirely or limit them to:
  - final, non-appealable determinations,
  - capped amounts,
  - only against the specific earn-out tranche (not future periods).

**8) Acceleration triggers**
Common seller asks:

- **Change of control** of the buyer or the earn-out business.
- Buyer discontinuing a product line or materially changing strategy.
- Termination of key seller executives (especially without cause).
- Buyer breach of operational covenants.

Acceleration is common enough to be “market-known,” but still heavily negotiated (buyers resist broad triggers). ([Kroll][2])

**9) Payment protections**

- Interest on late payments.
- Escrow/holdback or guarantee (especially if buyer credit risk exists).

#### Common Dispute Sources

Ranked by frequency (what shows up most often in post-close fights and formal disputes):

**1) Metric definition and accounting mechanics (especially “Adjusted EBITDA”)**

- What counts as an “add-back,” what is “non-recurring,” whether integration costs are excluded, etc.
- Data and dispute surveys repeatedly flag higher dispute risk as you move from reported metrics into heavy adjustments. ([Grant Thornton][1])

**2) Cost allocation and classification (COGS vs opex; overhead allocations)**

- Particularly ugly in gross profit and EBITDA earn-outs (classification drives the outcome). ([Grant Thornton][1])

**3) Revenue recognition / timing and cut-offs**

- Cut-off games, contract modifications, returns/credits timing, bundling.
- Also: if the negotiators aren’t the calculators, undocumented assumptions get lost—fuel for disputes. ([Grant Thornton][1])

**4) Buyer conduct / integration and “control problem”**

- Seller alleges buyer redirected sales, cut spend, changed pricing, or integrated in a way that depressed the metric.
- This is where “efforts standards” and operational covenants get litigated. ([SRS Acquiom][3])

**5) Information rights and transparency**

- Seller distrusts buyer-prepared calculations; delayed reporting; limited access.
- Information asymmetry is a recurring driver of escalation. ([Kroll][2])

**6) Milestone ambiguity**

- “Approval,” “launch,” “commercialization,” “signed contract,” “first commercial sale,” etc. can be interpreted multiple ways.

**7) Set-off fights**

- Buyer offsets indemnity claims against earn-out; seller argues offsets are improper/premature/out of scope.

**8) Timing and payment mechanics**

- Late delivery of statements, short objection windows, interest, dispute timelines, who pays the accountant/arbitrator.

**9) Acceleration / change-of-control treatment**

- Whether acceleration is automatic, partial, or based on a formula.
- Whether a new buyer must assume the obligation.

**10) People/retention-linked provisions**

- Termination/resignation and “cause” definitions (especially where earn-out is effectively deferred comp).

Also worth noting: earn-outs are **frequently contested**. One dataset notes earn-outs are contested at least **28%** of the time. ([SRS Acquiom][4])

#### Red Flag Language

These are phrases that routinely create ambiguity or litigation risk because they are **underspecified**, **subjective**, or **internally inconsistent** with the rest of the agreement.

**Accounting / metric ambiguity**

- “**Adjusted EBITDA**” (with no schedule of permitted adjustments)
- “Adjusted for **extraordinary, non-recurring, or exceptional items**” (vague; not GAAP-defined; a known dispute driver) ([Grant Thornton][1])
- “Prepared in accordance with **GAAP**” (without stating _which_ GAAP version, whether changes apply, and whether “consistent application” is required)
- “Consistent with **past practice**” (without attaching that past practice—policies, allocations, historical schedules)
- “Reasonable allocations of overhead” (reasonable to whom? based on what method?)

**Control / conduct ambiguity**

- “Buyer shall use **commercially reasonable efforts** to achieve…” (without defining what that means, what spend is required, or what trade-offs buyer may consider) ([SRS Acquiom][3])
- “Buyer shall not take any action that would **materially** reduce the earn-out” (what is “material”; what is the counterfactual; what actions are carved out as normal-course?)
- “Buyer will operate the business in its **sole discretion**” (paired with an earn-out) → invites an implied covenant / bad faith fight
- “Buyer shall operate the business to **maximize** the earn-out” (creates a very high bar and invites litigation about strategy decisions; also increasingly uncommon)

**Process ambiguity**

- “As determined by Buyer” (with no audit rights, no calculation standards, and no dispute mechanism)
- “The parties will **mutually agree** on adjustments post-closing” (this is basically a future dispute pre-written into the contract)
- “Disputes shall be submitted to an independent accountant” (without limiting scope to accounting issues, or clarifying what happens to pure contract-interpretation disputes)

**Milestone ambiguity**

- “Regulatory approval” (conditional? label limitations?)
- “Commercial launch” (first shipment? first paid order? GA release?)
- “New customer” (new logo? new billing entity? minimum contract value? affiliates?)
- “Key contract executed” (signed vs effective; termination rights; minimum term/value)

#### Market Benchmarks

Earn-out “market” varies by sector and deal size, but there are some consistent patterns in published market summaries:

**How common are earn-outs?**

- Earn-outs appear in a meaningful minority of private-company deals; one overview cites **26%** in the most recent ABA private target study, and **33%** in the SRS Acquiom private deal terms study. ([Kroll][2])
- In SRS commentary, earn-out prevalence stayed elevated post-2023 but didn’t keep rising in 2024. ([SRS Acquiom][3])

**How big are they?**

- Median earn-out potential around **~31–32% of the closing payment** in cited datasets. ([Kroll][2])
  - Practically, that often translates to “material, but not majority” of total consideration in many middle-market deals (unless you’re in high-uncertainty sectors like life sciences).

**How long do they run?**

- Median earn-out length of **~24 months** is frequently cited. ([Kroll][2])
- A common framing is an earn-out period **up to ~3 years**. ([Kroll][2])
- Recent SRS commentary notes a trend toward **shorter earn-out performance periods**, with fewer deals extending beyond four years (and none beyond four years in their 2024-closed sample). ([SRS Acquiom][3])

**Single vs multiple metrics**

- Earn-outs are often multi-factor; SRS commentary notes **68%** of deals with earn-outs include **multiple earn-out metrics**. ([SRS Acquiom][3])

**Acceleration triggers**

- **Change-of-control acceleration** appears in a meaningful minority:
  - ~**22%** in one cited overview, ([Kroll][2])
  - and ~**30%** in another dataset for 2022 non-life sciences deals.

**Offsets / set-off rights**

- Set-off rights against earn-out payments are common:
  - one overview cites **58%+** including indemnity set-off rights, ([Kroll][2])
  - and another dataset shows a strong majority expressly allowing offsets.

**Dispute frequency / payout reality (important for analysts)**

- Earn-outs are often **contested** (at least ~**28%** in one dataset). ([SRS Acquiom][4])
- A dispute survey reports **~26%** of earn-outs are disputed on average, with **Adjusted EBITDA** earn-outs slightly higher (~28%). ([Grant Thornton][1])
- SRS commentary also notes earn-outs pay about **$0.21 per $1.00** of potential value on average, and even among deals with some achievement only about half the maximum earn-out dollars are paid. ([SRS Acquiom][3])

#### Must-Flag Checklist

When reviewing earn-out provisions (for diligence, QoE implications, modeling, or dispute risk), an analyst should **always flag** the following—because each one can swing value or create a post-close fight:

**Metric + calculation**

- [ ] Is the metric **defined with a formula**, not a label (“Adjusted EBITDA”)?
- [ ] Are **all adjustments enumerated** (not “including but not limited to”)?
- [ ] Is there a **worked example** or sample schedule?
- [ ] Are **caps, floors, thresholds, and tiering** clearly stated?
- [ ] Is there a **catch-up** mechanism (or does missing one year permanently kill the earn-out)?

**Accounting basis / policy hierarchy**

- [ ] GAAP/IFRS version and whether changes apply.
- [ ] “Consistently applied” relative to what baseline (pre-close financials? buyer policies?).
- [ ] Treatment of **purchase accounting** (step-ups, new amortization, fair value adjustments).
- [ ] Treatment of **intercompany transactions** and transfer pricing.
- [ ] Treatment of **corporate overhead allocations** and shared services charges.

**Revenue specifics (if revenue/ARR/bookings-based)**

- [ ] Cut-off rules, returns/credits, concessions, multi-element arrangements.
- [ ] Definition of customer, renewal, churn, cohort boundaries (for SaaS).
- [ ] Treatment of price increases, bundling, and cross-sell from buyer.

**Cost specifics (if gross profit/EBITDA-based)**

- [ ] Clear COGS definition; classification rules.
- [ ] Treatment of integration, restructuring, severance, and synergy initiatives.
- [ ] “Non-recurring/extraordinary” language—flag as high dispute risk. ([Grant Thornton][1])

**Operational covenants / control**

- [ ] Any “efforts” standard—does it have **teeth** or is it purely subjective?
- [ ] Does the agreement conflict (“buyer sole discretion” vs “must use efforts”)?
- [ ] Are there restrictions on discontinuing products, reallocating resources, or changing strategy?
- [ ] Are there “past practice” covenants without documentation (flag immediately)?

**Information rights + governance**

- [ ] Reporting cadence and deadlines (monthly/quarterly; delivery date).
- [ ] Seller inspection/audit rights (scope, confidentiality, timing).
- [ ] Objection window and what happens if seller misses it.

**Dispute resolution**

- [ ] Independent accountant scope limited to **accounting/math**; legal disputes routed appropriately.
- [ ] Who selects/appoints the accountant; who pays; timeline; final-and-binding language.
- [ ] Standard of review (de novo vs limited).

**Payment mechanics**

- [ ] Payment timing after period-end; interest on late payments.
- [ ] Escrow/holdback/security for earn-out obligations (or lack thereof).
- [ ] Set-off rights: permitted? capped? only for finally determined claims? (flag because it directly changes expected value). ([Kroll][2])

**Acceleration / change of control**

- [ ] Is acceleration triggered? Partial vs full? Formula or deemed achievement?
- [ ] If the business is sold, must the buyer ensure the successor assumes the earn-out? ([Kroll][2])

**Economic reality flags (model risk)**

- [ ] Targets set relative to a credible baseline (budget vs historical run-rate).
- [ ] Earn-out period length vs integration timeline (if integration will disrupt reporting).
- [ ] Whether the earn-out is likely to be renegotiated/contested (many are). ([SRS Acquiom][4])
