#### Financial DD-Relevant Reps

Ranked (roughly) by how often they move **QoE, Net Debt / debt-like, NWC, and “surprise liability” risk**. 🔍
_(Exact ranking can shift by industry: e.g., software vs. manufacturing vs. financial services.)_

1. **Financial Statements (FS) + Accounting Policies**
   - **Why FDD cares:** This is the “foundation rep” for QoE, NWC methodology, debt-like identification, and any argument about “the numbers were wrong.”
   - **What to look for:**
     - Is the rep **GAAP/IFRS compliance** or just “prepared consistent with past practice” (much weaker)?
     - “**Fairly present**” language (stronger) vs “in all material respects” (weaker).
     - Interim FS: are they covered the same way as annual/audited FS, or watered down?
     - Any carve-outs: “except as disclosed” / “except immaterial errors” / “subject to normal year-end adjustments.”

   - **FDD tie-in:** If you’re making **QoE adjustments** due to aggressive accounting, this is the rep family that matters most.

2. **Undisclosed Liabilities / No Off–Balance Sheet Liabilities**
   - **Why FDD cares:** This is the rep buyers rely on when the **balance sheet misses debt-like items, contingencies, accrual gaps, or unusual commitments**.
   - **What to look for:**
     - Does it cover **all liabilities** (accrued/absolute/contingent/known/unknown) or only “liabilities required to be recorded under GAAP” (narrower)?
     - Big carve-outs like “except liabilities incurred in the ordinary course since the balance sheet date.”
     - Any “**in the aggregate**” materiality thresholds (seller-favorable).

   - **FDD tie-in:** Maps directly to your **net debt / debt-like schedule** and “hidden items” workstream.

3. **Books & Records / Maintenance of Records**
   - **Why FDD cares:** When the data is messy, this rep becomes the “**can we trust the ledger?**” clause.
   - **What to look for:**
     - “Books and records are **true, correct, and complete**” (strong) vs “maintained in accordance with applicable law” (doesn’t guarantee accuracy).
     - Any linkage to **internal controls** or “maintained in reasonable detail.”

   - **FDD tie-in:** Supports (or undermines) comfort in revenue cut-off, accrual completeness, and working-cap accuracy.

4. **Taxes (filing, payment, audits, withholding, transfer pricing, sales tax, payroll tax)**
   - **Why FDD cares:** Tax exposures are classic “**post-close surprises**,” often large and often excluded from general baskets/caps via special treatment.
   - **What to look for:**
     - Scope: “all Taxes” vs specific types; includes **indirect taxes** (sales/VAT) and **payroll**?
     - Are there “no audits” / “no disputes” reps? Any known assessments?
     - Are tax liabilities carved into a **special indemnity** or separate escrow?

   - **FDD tie-in:** Connects to your **tax due diligence findings**, but also to debt-like (tax payable), contingencies, and cash leakage.

5. **Absence of Certain Changes / Ordinary Course (since balance sheet date)**
   - **Why FDD cares:** Buyers use this to protect against **value leakage** and “the business changed after the snapshot.”
   - **What to look for:**
     - What is the lookback date? Last audited balance sheet? Last month-end? Signing?
     - “Ordinary course **consistent with past practice**” (stronger) vs just “ordinary course” (more arguable).
     - COVID / macro carve-outs or broad exceptions that let the seller do a lot.

   - **FDD tie-in:** If you’re seeing a sharp margin drop, unusual expenses, pricing changes, or working-cap swings, this rep interacts with whether the seller can say “that’s allowed.”

6. **Indebtedness / Liens / Guarantees / Affiliate Debt**
   - **Why FDD cares:** This is the legal wrapper around your **net debt** conclusion.
   - **What to look for:**
     - Definition of “Indebtedness” (does it capture leases, earnouts, letters of credit, accrued interest/fees, change-of-control payments, customer advances, etc.?)
     - Lien rep: “no Liens” except permitted liens; check the **permitted lien list**.
     - Guarantees or “keepwell” / affiliate support obligations.

   - **FDD tie-in:** Makes or breaks the “net debt free” construct.

7. **Working Capital Components Reps (AR, inventory, payables, reserves)**
   - **Why FDD cares:** Impacts **NWC target disputes** and post-close adjustments.
   - **What to look for:**
     - AR rep: collectability, aging accuracy, write-off practices.
     - Inventory rep: existence, valuation, obsolescence reserves, consignment.
     - Payables rep: completeness, absence of extended terms / unusual accrual reversals.

   - **FDD tie-in:** You’ll use this to explain why a seller’s NWC schedule is “too pretty.”

8. **Material Contracts + No Default**
   - **Why FDD cares:** Revenue durability and margin assumptions often depend on a handful of contracts.
   - **What to look for:**
     - What qualifies as “Material Contract” (sometimes defined so narrowly it misses key revenue arrangements).
     - “No default” rep: does it cover **counterparty defaults** too, or only the company’s default?
     - Any “**to knowledge**” qualifier (weakens it materially).

   - **FDD tie-in:** Your customer concentration, pricing, renewal risk, and backlog analysis should map here.

9. **Employee Compensation / Bonuses / Benefits / Pension**
   - **Why FDD cares:** These are common **debt-like** and “accrual completeness” issues.
   - **What to look for:**
     - Unfunded pension/OPEB, bonus plans, commissions, PTO accruals, retention/change-of-control.
     - Benefit plan compliance statements (sometimes heavily lawyered and carve-out prone).

   - **FDD tie-in:** Often directly feeds your **debt-like schedule** and “one-time normalization” items.

10. **Litigation / Claims / Investigations**

- **Why FDD cares:** Contingent liabilities, settlement risk, and cost-to-remediate.
- **What to look for:**
  - “Pending or threatened” (threatened can be heavily qualified) and materiality thresholds.
  - Any specific carve-outs in schedules (these often drive special indemnities).

- **FDD tie-in:** Helps bound downside cases and informs whether risk is priced vs indemnified.

11. **Related-Party Transactions**

- **Why FDD cares:** Classic QoE adjustments + leakage risk (non-arm’s-length pricing, owner expenses, management fees).
- **What to look for:**
  - Does it require listing _all_ related-party arrangements, or only “material” ones?
  - Any commitment to terminate related-party arrangements at close?

- **FDD tie-in:** Directly supports QoE add-backs and ongoing run-rate assumptions.

12. **Compliance with Laws (financially meaningful subsets)**

- **Why FDD cares:** Only the parts that create **financial exposure** (e.g., anti-corruption, sanctions, wage/hour, data privacy fines, licensing).
- **What to look for:**
  - Broad “in compliance with all laws” reps are usually qualified (otherwise they’re too risky).
  - Look for MAE/materiality qualifiers and schedule carve-outs.

- **FDD tie-in:** Risk-adjusts forecasts and identifies where a “legal issue” becomes a financial reserve question.

#### Qualifier Weakening Patterns

Here’s how reps get diluted in practice—and how to spot it fast. 🕵️‍♂️

1. **Knowledge qualifiers** (“to Seller’s Knowledge…”, “to the best of…”)
   - **Why it weakens:** Converts a “fact” into “we don’t know of a problem,” which is much harder to claim against unless you can prove knowledge.
   - **Spot it:** Search for “Knowledge,” then read the **definition** (actual vs constructive; whose knowledge; duty of inquiry or not).

2. **Materiality qualifiers** (“in all material respects,” “material,” “material adverse”)
   - **Why it weakens:** Lets small-to-medium issues slip through, and invites fights about what’s “material.”
   - **Spot it:** Look for **double materiality**: materiality in the rep **and** a basket/deductible in indemnity (unless there’s a materiality scrape).

3. **MAE/Material Adverse Effect qualifiers**
   - **Why it weakens:** MAE is a **very high bar**, often negotiated with broad carve-outs.
   - **Spot it:** Find where “Material Adverse Effect” is defined, then check carve-outs (macro, industry, pandemics, etc.). Also see if rep “bring-down” is tied to MAE (seller-favorable).

4. **“Except as set forth in the Disclosure Schedules” (schedule qualifiers)**
   - **Why it weakens:** The real truth shifts into schedules; a “clean rep” can still be heavily carved out.
   - **Spot it:** Any rep ending with “except as disclosed…” → you must inspect the schedule itemization and specificity.

5. **“In the ordinary course” carve-outs**
   - **Why it weakens:** Sellers can argue a lot of liabilities/changes are “ordinary,” especially if “ordinary course” isn’t tied to “consistent with past practice.”
   - **Spot it:** Compare “ordinary course” definition and any “consistent with past practice” language.

6. **Time-boxing** (“since December 31, 20XX…”, “during the past 12 months…”)
   - **Why it weakens:** Narrows exposure window; can be fine, but sometimes it’s overly tight relative to risk (e.g., tax).
   - **Spot it:** Locate the “as of” dates and see if they conveniently dodge known historical periods.

7. **Aggregation qualifiers** (“individually or in the aggregate…”)
   - **Why it weakens:** Sellers try to avoid death-by-1,000-cuts claims; buyers want aggregation to count.
   - **Spot it:** Look for “in the aggregate” and how it interacts with the basket.

8. **Legal standard qualifiers** (“would not reasonably be expected to…”)
   - **Why it weakens:** Adds forecasting/causation debate; harder to prove.
   - **Spot it:** Phrases like “reasonably be expected to have” + MAE/materiality.

9. **“Material Contracts only” / narrow defined terms**
   - **Why it weakens:** The rep may look broad, but the definition of “Material Contract,” “Indebtedness,” “Liability,” etc. quietly shrinks the universe.
   - **Spot it:** Always read **definitions** of capitalized terms in reps you care about.

#### Onerous vs Weak Spectrum

Think of each rep on a spectrum from “seller taking real risk” to “buyer basically gets no usable protection.” ⚖️

**A) Financial Statements rep**

- **Unusually onerous (buyer-favorable):**
  - “FS are prepared in accordance with GAAP/IFRS **in all respects** and **fairly present** financial condition/results…”
  - Includes **interim FS** equally + includes **consistent application** of policies and estimates.
  - Sometimes coupled with **internal controls** reps (rare outside certain contexts).

- **Unusually weak (seller-favorable):**
  - “To the Knowledge of Seller, FS are prepared in accordance with GAAP **in all material respects**…”
  - Interim FS excluded, or “subject to normal year-end adjustments” without guardrails.
  - “Prepared consistent with past practice” instead of GAAP/IFRS.

**B) Undisclosed liabilities rep**

- **Unusually onerous:**
  - “No liabilities of any nature (absolute, accrued, contingent, known/unknown) **other than** those on the balance sheet or incurred in ordinary course…,” with tight ordinary-course definition.
  - No broad materiality threshold; includes off-balance sheet commitments.

- **Unusually weak:**
  - Only covers “liabilities **required to be recorded** under GAAP” (doesn’t catch many contingencies).
  - Broad carve-out: “except liabilities incurred in ordinary course since date” + “not material” + “in aggregate” stacked together.

**C) Tax rep**

- **Unusually onerous:**
  - Covers filing/payment/withholding/sales tax/payroll across jurisdictions with minimal qualifiers.
  - Includes “no disputes/audits,” “no liens,” “no aggressive positions,” etc.

- **Unusually weak:**
  - Heavy knowledge/materiality qualifiers; narrow scope (only income taxes).
  - Silent on indirect taxes and withholding (often where surprises live).

**D) Compliance with laws rep**

- **Unusually onerous:**
  - “In compliance with all laws **in all respects**” (rare because it’s so broad).
  - Includes anti-corruption/sanctions/privacy without MAE qualifiers.

- **Unusually weak:**
  - “In compliance in all material respects, except as would not reasonably be expected to have an MAE” (stacked qualifiers).
  - Defined “laws” excludes key regimes or periods.

**E) 10b-5 / “Full Disclosure” rep (often deal-specific)**

- **Unusually onerous:** Seller represents there are **no material misstatements or omissions** in the deal materials (this is a big risk shift to seller).
- **Unusually weak:** Expressly **excluded**, plus a strong **non-reliance** clause saying buyer didn’t rely on extra-contractual statements.

#### Reps-Indemnification Connection

This is the key “wiring diagram” an FDD analyst should have in their head. 🧩

1. **Reps = the “promises about the business.” Indemnification = the “remedy if the promise is false.”**
   - Without a meaningful indemnity package (or RWI), even a strong rep can be practically toothless.

2. **The same rep can be strong on paper but weak in economics**
   - Example: A solid “no undisclosed liabilities” rep doesn’t help much if:
     - the **cap** is tiny,
     - the **basket/deductible** is large,
     - damages are heavily limited (e.g., no consequential/lost profits), or
     - survival is very short.

3. **Materiality scrapes can “undo” rep-level materiality qualifiers**
   - Many agreements have a concept where, for indemnity purposes, materiality qualifiers are ignored when testing breach or calculating losses (“materiality scrape”).
   - If there’s **no scrape**, then rep-level materiality matters a lot more.

4. **Special indemnities are how parties “deal with known issues”**
   - If FDD finds a specific risk (tax exposure, litigation, environmental remediation), the resolution is often:
     - price adjustment, **or**
     - **special indemnity** (often uncapped or separately capped, longer survival), **or**
     - a **separate escrow/holdback**, **or**
     - explicit exclusion + buyer accepts it.

5. **FDD’s practical job: map findings → recourse**
   - For every major diligence issue, ask:
     - Is this covered by a rep?
     - Is it carved out by schedules?
     - If it’s a breach, is recovery blocked by knowledge/anti-sandbagging/exclusive remedy?
     - What’s the cap/basket/survival? Any RWI exclusions?

6. **Market reality: “walk-away” / no-survival structures exist**
   - Recent deal terms data show a meaningful portion of deals are structured so general reps don’t survive post-close (“walk-away”). ([SRS Acquiom][1])
   - That makes diligence and purchase price mechanics even more critical.

#### Market Standard Survival Periods

These are **typical North American private-company M&A ranges**, but the spread is real (deal size, auction dynamics, RWI, jurisdiction, and industry all matter). ⏳

**General / business reps (often includes FS, undisclosed liabilities, compliance, contracts)**

- **Common:** **12–18 months**.
- Data points:
  - For deals where indemnification protections survived post-closing, SRS Acquiom reported a **median survival period of 12 months** (in their 2024 deals discussion). ([SRS Acquiom][1])
  - In an ABA Deal Points study summary (deals signed/completed in 2020–Q1 2021), **12 months (43%)** and **18 months (37%)** were the most common express survival periods, and **33%** had no express survival period. ([Fasken][2])

**Fundamental reps (typically: organization, authority, capitalization/ownership of equity, title to shares; sometimes certain core IP/title reps depending on deal)**

- **Common:** **3–6 years**, and sometimes tied to the applicable statute of limitations.
- Practical note: Fundamental reps are often carved out for longer survival even when general reps are short.

**Tax reps**

- **Common:** Often **aligned to statute of limitations** for the relevant taxes, or a negotiated multi-year period (frequently in the **3–6+ year** range depending on jurisdiction and tax type).
- With RWI in the mix, some sources describe **6-year** terms for tax/fundamental coverage. ([Perkins Coie][3])

**Employee benefits / pension / ERISA-type reps**

- **Common:** Often **longer than general**, sometimes aligned with tax/fundamental timing (especially where tax-qualified plan status is at issue).

**Environmental reps**

- **Common:** Highly deal-specific; often **longer survival** and/or handled via **special indemnity** due to long-tail risk.

**RWI policy “coverage term” (important when RWI is the primary recourse)**

- A common framing is **~3 years for general reps** and **~6 years for fundamental and tax reps** under RWI policies. ([Perkins Coie][3])

**Jurisdictional sanity check (why “statute” matters):**

- Statutes of limitation differ by governing law. For example, one summary notes **New York** breach of contract limitations can be **six years**, while **Delaware** is generally **three years** for breach of contract claims (absent contractual extension mechanics). ([DarrowEverett LLP][4])
  _You don’t need to be the lawyer here—but you should realize survival periods sometimes “track statute,” and statute isn’t universal._

#### Flag for Legal Review

Use this as an FDD “issue-spotting checklist” to hand to deal counsel quickly. ⚠️✅

**A) Recourse / economics (the rep might not matter if recovery is blocked)**

- ☐ **No-survival / walk-away** structure for general reps
- ☐ **Exclusive remedy** clause (is indemnity/RWI the only path?)
- ☐ **Cap / basket / deductible** terms that make recovery unrealistic for the risk size
- ☐ **Materiality scrape** present or absent (changes how meaningful rep qualifiers are)
- ☐ **Limitations on damages** (consequential, lost profits, multiples, diminution in value, etc.)

**B) Knowledge / reliance / “what did buyer know?”**

- ☐ **Anti-sandbagging** (buyer can’t recover if it knew pre-close) vs pro-sandbagging
- ☐ **Non-reliance** / disclaimer of reliance on management presentations, CIM, Q&A, etc.
- ☐ “**Full disclosure / 10b-5**” rep included or expressly excluded (risk-shifting is huge) ([SRS Acquiom][1])

**C) Definition traps (quietly shrink rep scope)**

- ☐ Definitions of **Knowledge**, **Material Adverse Effect**, **Material Contracts**, **Indebtedness**, **Losses**, “ordinary course”
- ☐ MAE carve-outs that swallow protection (macro/industry/pandemic/interest rates, etc.)

**D) Disclosure schedules (where reps go to die)**

- ☐ Schedules that are **overbroad**, vague, or “data-dumped”
- ☐ Schedule items that appear inconsistent with financial diligence findings (e.g., debt-like items disclosed legally but not captured in net debt)

**E) Known issues needing bespoke treatment**

- ☐ Any diligence-identified risk being “handled” only by a generic rep (should it be a **special indemnity/escrow** instead?)
- ☐ Tax exposures, litigation, environmental remediation, wage/hour, pensions → **special indemnity** and/or longer survival

**F) RWI specifics (if applicable)**

- ☐ RWI **exclusions** that map to your biggest diligence risks (e.g., known issues, certain tax, pensions)
- ☐ Retention allocation and whether the deal’s indemnity structure aligns with the policy mechanics ([Perkins Coie][3])
