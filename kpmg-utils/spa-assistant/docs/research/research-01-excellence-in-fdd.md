#### Markers of Excellence

- **They anchor everything to the deal thesis + the SPA mechanics** 🧭
  They constantly translate findings into: _“So what does this do to price, structure, or terms?”_
  Example: not just “working capital is volatile,” but _“volatility means the peg needs seasonality normalization, or the buyer will overpay at close.”_

- **They quantify, even when the data is messy** 📏
  Excellent associates don’t hide behind “hard to tell.” They:
  - build ranges (low / base / high),
  - state assumptions explicitly,
  - show sensitivity (e.g., “EBITDA impact = $1.8–$3.0M depending on churn cohort definition”).

- **They triangulate instead of trusting any one source** 🔺
  They reconcile management narratives to _independent_ evidence:
  - GL ↔ bank statements ↔ AR subledger ↔ contract terms ↔ operational KPIs
    If the story only works in one dataset, they treat it as a risk.

- **They treat “Adjusted EBITDA” as a hypothesis, not an answer** 🧠
  They rebuild EBITDA from the ground up:
  - identify true recurring cost base,
  - test each add-back for recurrence, control, and buyer relevance,
  - assess whether the adjustment survives _ownership change_.

- **They understand what “normal” means for THIS business (not generic normal)** 🎯
  They define normalization using:
  - seasonality,
  - customer/product mix shifts,
  - one-time events,
  - growth phase (land/grab vs mature),
  - capacity constraints, pricing actions, etc.
    Mediocre work uses industry clichés; excellent work defines “normal” with evidence.

- **They actively hunt for “value transfer mechanisms”** 💸
  Where does cash/profit move between parties _outside_ headline EBITDA?
  - rebates/returns true-ups,
  - bill-and-hold / cut-off games,
  - capitalization vs expensing,
  - related-party leakage,
  - deferred revenue dynamics,
  - warranty/claims accrual adequacy.

- **They run a live “issues log” like a negotiation playbook** 📌
  Great associates maintain a clean, constantly updated tracker:
  - issue statement,
  - root cause,
  - quantified impact (range),
  - confidence level,
  - suggested deal protection (peg change / escrow / indemnity / covenant / closing condition),
  - who owns the next question.
    Partners love this because it converts chaos into decisions.

- **They communicate early and crisply (no surprises)** 📣
  The best associates escalate risks _as soon as they’re directionally confident_, even before perfect quantification:
  - “We think there’s a revenue cut-off pull-forward risk; early estimate is $2–4M EBITDA. We’re validating with shipment data tomorrow.”

- **Their writing is decision-grade, not report-grade** ✍️
  They write like they expect the client to act on it:
  - single-sentence headline conclusion,
  - quantified impact,
  - why it matters,
  - what to do next (deal lever).

- **They’re fluent in the buyer’s model and constraints** 🧩
  They understand valuation drivers and lender/investment committee requirements:
  - what counts as “recurring,”
  - what lenders haircut,
  - what the IC will challenge,
  - what will break the model / covenants.

- **They proactively define terms the SPA will fight about** ⚖️
  They don’t just analyze numbers—they anticipate definitional disputes:
  - debt-like items,
  - working capital definition,
  - EBITDA definition for earn-outs,
  - treatment of leases, bonuses, deferred revenue, accruals, tax liabilities, etc.

#### Common Mediocrity Traps

- **Summarizing management explanations instead of validating them** 😬
  “Management says churn is normal.”
  Great diligence asks: _show cohort churn by vintage, price change periods, and top customers._

- **Reporting variance without identifying the driver**
  “Gross margin down 200 bps.”
  So what? Was it:
  - price/mix,
  - labor efficiency,
  - one-time scrap,
  - change in revenue recognition,
  - contract repricing lag?

- **Treating materiality as a checkbox instead of a decision lens**
  Mediocre work flags 25 issues with no prioritization.
  Excellent work flags 5–8 issues that actually change outcomes.

- **Falling in love with precision and missing timing** ⏳
  Deals move fast. Waiting for perfect proof often means the buyer loses leverage.
  Better: give a directional call + range + plan to refine.

- **Not connecting QoE to cash** 💧
  EBITDA can look great while cash conversion is deteriorating.
  Mediocre work stops at P&L. Great work ties:
  - AR aging / DSO,
  - inventory quality,
  - accrual adequacy,
  - capex needs,
  - working capital seasonality
    …to sustainable free cash flow.

- **Using “adjusted EBITDA” add-backs that won’t survive ownership change**
  Example traps:
  - “temporary” labor that is actually structural,
  - “one-time” professional fees that recur every year,
  - founder comp adjustments that ignore required replacement management.

- **Ignoring the interaction effects** 🔁
  Example: revenue cut-off games inflate revenue _and_ reduce working capital needs temporarily _and_ distort deferred revenue.
  Average analysis treats each line item separately; excellent analysis follows the thread across statements.

- **Burying the lede in a 40-page narrative** 🥱
  Clients don’t pay for pages; they pay for clarity and leverage.

- **Failing to align findings to SPA levers**
  Identifying a liability is not enough. You must suggest:
  - purchase price adjustment,
  - escrow/holdback,
  - specific indemnity,
  - covenant/closing condition,
  - earn-out metric adjustment,
  - debt-like item classification.

- **“Tie-out hero, insight zero”**
  Clean schedules but no conclusions. It’s good hygiene, not diligence excellence.

#### What Moves the Needle

These are the insights that most often change price, structure, timing, or negotiation leverage 👇

- **True sustainable EBITDA (and what should be excluded from valuation)** 💥
  - revenue cut-off / pull-forward (channel stuffing, bill-and-hold, unusual quarter-end shipments)
  - customer churn masked by one-time wins
  - margin sustainability (temporary price increases, lagging wage inflation, supplier concessions ending)
  - one-time “savings” that are actually underinvestment (maintenance deferral, delayed hires)

- **Working capital peg normalization (especially seasonality and “managed” periods)** ⚙️
  The peg is one of the most powerful negotiation levers because it’s “math,” not “opinion.”
  Needle-movers include:
  - quarter-end AR collections pulled forward,
  - inventory obsolescence masked by slow-moving stock,
  - accrued expenses understated (bonuses, vacation, rebates, warranties),
  - deferred revenue treatment inconsistencies,
  - seasonality not reflected in a simple average.

- **Debt-like items and leakage that buyers didn’t price in** 🧨
  These often become direct purchase price adjustments:
  - unpaid taxes / sales tax exposures,
  - customer rebates/returns true-ups,
  - litigation reserves inadequacy,
  - off-balance-sheet commitments (leases, take-or-pay, unfavorable contracts),
  - related-party payables or unusual balance sheet items that behave like debt.

- **Revenue quality and concentration risk** 🎯
  What changes deals is not “top 10 customers are 55%”—it’s the _risk-adjusted_ view:
  - renewal terms and churn history,
  - pricing power vs discounting,
  - single-customer economics (margin by customer),
  - contract clauses (most-favored-nation, rights of return, service credits, termination for convenience),
  - backlog and pipeline conversion quality.

- **Carve-out reality and stand-alone costs** 🧩
  For carve-outs, “EBITDA” is often fiction until you price:
  - stranded costs,
  - TSA duration and cost inflation,
  - IT separation capex,
  - duplicated functions and required hires,
  - allocation methodology validity.

- **Capex needs vs reported earnings (maintenance capex gap)** 🏗️
  A business can “create” EBITDA by starving capex.
  The deal impact is usually valuation multiple compression or price adjustment rationale:
  - deferred maintenance,
  - aging fleet/equipment,
  - high breakdown rates,
  - capex-to-depreciation mismatch inconsistent with capacity reality.

- **Earnings vs cash conversion mismatch** 💧
  This changes lender appetite and equity returns:
  - high DSO / customer payment term creep,
  - inventory build required to sustain growth,
  - warranty claims lagging actual experience,
  - deferred revenue dynamics (especially in SaaS / services).

- **Forecast credibility (not just “management has a budget”)** 🔮
  Forecast diligence moves deals when it shows:
  - missing headcount/capacity constraints,
  - pipeline conversion assumptions inconsistent with history,
  - price increases assumed without evidence,
  - customer churn not modeled,
  - margin improvements with no operational plan.

- **Definitional landmines for earn-outs and purchase price adjustments** ⚖️
  Earn-outs and PPAs often fail because of definition games:
  - what counts as “revenue” (gross vs net, returns, rebates),
  - treatment of accounting changes post-close,
  - cost allocation policies,
  - extraordinary items and integration costs.

### The difference between “reading the document” and “analyzing the document” 📚➡️🧠

- **Reading** = _What does it say?_
  - repeats management explanation
  - restates trends (“Revenue up 12%”)
  - lists add-backs
  - ties out schedules
  - notes accounting policy without testing impact

- **Analyzing** = _Is it true? Why is it happening? What does it imply for value and terms?_
  - forms hypotheses and tests them (cut-off, pricing, churn, mix, accrual adequacy)
  - triangulates across datasets (GL ↔ subledgers ↔ contracts ↔ bank ↔ ops)
  - quantifies impact (range + confidence level)
  - connects to deal levers (price/peg/debt-like/indemnity/earn-out definitions)
  - makes a directional call and recommends action
