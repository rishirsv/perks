Definition 1: Purchase Price / Consideration

Definition Name: “Merger Consideration”

Section Reference: Section 2.01(c), PDF p.7 / A-3.

Full Verbatim Text:
“(c) Conversion of Company Common Stock. Each share of Company Common Stock issued and
outstanding immediately prior to the Effective Time (other than shares of Company Common Stock to be
canceled in accordance with Section 2.01(b)) shall be converted automatically into and shall thereafter
represent only the right to receive an amount in cash equal to $90.50 per share, without interest (the
“Merger Consideration”). As of the Effective Time, all such shares of Company Common Stock shall no
longer be outstanding and shall automatically be canceled and shall cease to exist, and each holder of a
certificate which immediately prior to the Effective Time represented any such shares of Company
Common Stock (each, a “Certificate”) or of non-certificated shares of Company Common Stock held in
book-entry form (each, a “Book-Entry Share”) shall cease to have any rights with respect thereto, except
the right to receive the Merger Consideration to be paid in consideration therefor upon surrender of
such Certificate or Book-Entry Share in accordance with Section 2.02(b).” (Section 2.01(c), PDF p.7 / A-3).

This is a fixed-price, cash-out merger: every eligible Company common share converts into a right to receive $90.50 in cash at closing (no interest). There is no net debt or working capital adjustment; the “mechanics” are purely about exchanging shares for cash through the Paying Agent.

3.1 Price Equation

Because this is a public-company merger, the “equation” is a fixed per-share cash-out, plus separate cash-out mechanics for equity awards. There is no net debt / cash / working capital / transaction expense adjustment.

Price equation (reconstructed):

[Merger Consideration per share = $90.50 cash] (Section 2.01(c), PDF p.7 / A-3)
× [# of shares of Company Common Stock outstanding immediately prior to Effective Time]
– [shares canceled per Section 2.01(b)] (PDF p.7 / A-3)
= [Aggregate cash payable to shareholders via Paying Agent Exchange Fund] (Section 2.02(a), PDF p.7 / A-3)

PLUS separate equity award cash-outs (paid through Surviving Corporation payroll system):

- Company Stock Options: (Merger Consideration – exercise price) × vested/treated-as-vested option shares, less withholding (Section 2.03(a), PDF pp.9–10 / A-5 to A-6; Section 2.04, PDF p.11 / A-7)
- Company Restricted Shares: Merger Consideration × # restricted shares (Section 2.03(b), PDF p.10 / A-6)
- Company RSUs: Merger Consideration × # RSU shares (Section 2.03(c), PDF p.10 / A-6)
- Company PSUs: Merger Consideration × # PSU shares (with performance assumptions) (Section 2.03(d), PDF p.10 / A-6)

Section Reference: Sections 2.01(c), 2.02(a), 2.03, 2.04 (PDF pp.6–11 / A-2 to A-7).

6.3 Escrow Mechanics Details

Exchange Fund (Paying Agent)

Funded from purchase price or in addition?
Funded as part of consideration funding at/ before closing: “At or prior to the Effective Time, Parent shall deposit… an amount in cash sufficient to pay the aggregate Merger Consideration… (the “Exchange Fund”).” (Section 2.02(a), PDF p.7 / A-3).

Claim mechanics:
Not a “claims” escrow; it is a payment fund. Key mechanics include:

- Investment and replenishment: “Exchange Fund shall be invested… as directed by Parent… [Parent] shall… promptly replace or restore the cash… to ensure… sufficient…” (Section 2.02(a), PDF p.7 / A-3).
- Interest: “Any and all interest earned… shall be paid… to the Surviving Corporation.” (Section 2.02(c), PDF p.8 / A-4).
- Termination: after one year, Surviving Corporation may require Paying Agent to deliver remaining funds back; holders then look to Surviving Corporation and Parent (Section 2.02(e), PDF p.9 / A-5).

Parent Termination Fee Escrow (Special Purpose)

Funded from purchase price or in addition?
In addition; only triggered upon a Parent Termination Fee event. Parent must deposit into escrow upon termination triggering Parent Termination Fee payment: “Parent shall deposit… the Parent Termination Fee… into an escrow account…” (Section 7.03(g)(i), PDF p.64 / A-60).

Release timing and conditions (high-level):

- Immediate release up to a tax-driven cap: Escrow Agent releases “the Maximum Payment Amount” to Company within 2 Business Days, with remainder staying in escrow (Section 7.03(g)(i), PDF p.64 / A-60).
- Remainder released only upon specified comfort events, and subject to the same payment cap logic (Section 7.03(g)(ii), PDF p.64 / A-60).
- Five-year sunset and reversion: “If… not released… within five (5) years… such amount shall be released… to Parent and… no other party shall have… obligations…” (Section 7.03(g)(ii), PDF p.64 / A-60).
- Interest: “Any earnings of the Escrow Account shall be for the account of Parent…” (Section 7.03(g)(ii), PDF p.64 / A-60).

Relationship to indemnification:

- Exclusive remedy? Not indemnity; it is a termination-fee security mechanism. Remedy limitations are in Section 7.03(d)–(f) (PDF pp.62–63 / A-58 to A-59).
- First source vs. sole source: N/A (not indemnity).
- Survival alignment: N/A (no survival; Section 8.01, PDF p.65 / A-61).

Part 22: Suggested Revisions

Revision 1: Eliminate or neutralize 5-year reversion of unpaid Parent Termination Fee escrow amounts

Current Language:
“If… not released… within five (5) years… such amount shall be released… to Parent and… no other party shall have… obligations…” (Section 7.03(g)(ii), PDF p.64 / A-60).

Issue:
This can materially reduce the practical value of the Parent Termination Fee as seller protection. Even if Parent owes $813.5m, the Company may only receive the “Maximum Payment Amount” initially, and the remainder can ultimately revert to Parent.

Suggested Revision:
Remove the five-year reversion; provide that escrowed amounts remain payable to the Company upon satisfaction of release conditions without a sunset, or require Parent to fund an alternative payment mechanism that does not create prohibited REIT income (e.g., pay to a trust for stockholders, or structure as a permitted distribution).

Rationale:
Preserves the deterrent and compensatory function of the reverse termination fee while respecting REIT income constraints.

Likely Pushback:
“REIT income rules create real risk; we need a hard stop.”

Fallback Position:
Extend to a longer period (e.g., 10 years) and/or add an obligation for Parent to use reasonable best efforts to obtain IRS ruling/opinion to enable release, with periodic reporting.

Revision 2: Relax the financing-funded condition to specific performance of equity funding/closing

Current Language:
Specific performance to enforce funding/closing “shall only be available” if, among other things, “the Debt Financing… has been funded… or will be funded… at the Closing if the Equity Financing is funded…” (Section 8.08(C), PDF p.67 / A-63).

Issue:
This reintroduces financing risk through the back door. The Company’s ability to force the buyer to close is constrained if debt financing is disrupted, even if equity is available.

Suggested Revision:
Allow specific performance to require Parent to fund the equity and close even if debt is not available, provided Company conditions are satisfied and Company is ready, willing, and able to close.

Rationale:
Aligns with the commercial expectation of “no financing condition” and ensures buyer bears financing risk.

Likely Pushback:
“Equity was sized assuming debt; forcing all-equity closing is not commercially feasible.”

Fallback Position:
Permit specific performance if Parent has sufficient cash/equity to close, with a cap on forced incremental equity contribution, or allow short extension to arrange alternative financing but preserve the right to force close thereafter.

Revision 3: Add willful breach/fraud carve-out to Parent Cap / exclusive remedy

Current Language:
“payment… shall be the sole and exclusive remedy…” and “in no event shall Parent… be liable… in excess of $813,500,000 (the “Parent Cap”)” (Sections 7.03(e)–(f), PDF pp.62–63 / A-58 to A-59).

Issue:
In extreme cases (deliberate misconduct), this may under-deter and under-compensate; it can also weaken enforceability leverage.

Suggested Revision:
Carve out fraud and intentional/willful breach from the Parent Cap and exclusive remedy limitation.

Rationale:
Market-standard protection for egregious conduct while preserving predictability for ordinary breaches.

Likely Pushback:
“We need certainty; reverse fee is the deal.”

Fallback Position:
Limit carve-out to intentional breach of closing obligations and/or financing covenants, with a higher cap rather than uncapped liability.

Revision 4: Improve D&O tail protection if premiums exceed 300% cap

Current Language:
Premium cap: “in no event shall the aggregate premiums… exceed… 300%… (the “Maximum Amount”)… if… exceeds… Company… procure… most advantageous… for the Maximum Amount.” (Section 5.07(c), PDF p.52 / A-48).

Issue:
Directors/officers could face reduced coverage precisely when risk is elevated (post-transaction litigation environment).

Suggested Revision:
Either raise the cap (e.g., 350%–400%) or require Parent to fund excess premium above the cap, or provide supplemental indemnity to match prior coverage levels.

Rationale:
Maintains continuity of protection; aligns with market expectations for D&O continuity in public-company M&A.

Likely Pushback:
“Cost unpredictability.”

Fallback Position:
Cost-sharing above the cap, or require Parent to use best efforts to secure equivalent coverage including alternative carriers/structures.
