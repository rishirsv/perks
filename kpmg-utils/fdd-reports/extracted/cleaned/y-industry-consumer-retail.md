# Business Overview

- Y was founded in 2003 as a digitally led retailer of contemporary womenswear.
- The brand is sold directly to consumers through three channels: online, boutiques and through concessions at two luxury department stores. The boutiques and concessions are currently principally yy centric, with six boutique stores in the yy.
- Y products are targeted at affluent women aged 35 – 55, with a key focus on high quality fabric, versatility and longevity. Products are designed to work across seasons, be dressed up or down and to be worn together in an attempt to increase basket size, AOV, and customer loyalty.
- All products are designed in house by a dedicated team and manufactured in high precision factories, with a focus on sustainability throughout the design, manufacturing and packaging process.
- Net revenue and contribution for the retail channel has been impacted by Covid-19 in LTM22.
- Note: Revenue and contribution relating to sample sales is not presented in the channel analysis above, resulting in a small reconciling difference in FY21 (£10,000) and LTM22 (£79,000) between the sum of the contribution from the channels and overall contribution.
- The key drivers of online sales are traffic, and the associated marketing cost, conversion and average order value. Y’s primary source of traffic is organic and email, driving 27.7% and 19.1% of website traffic in LTM22 respectively. Online sales represents product sales net of VAT and marked down discounts. The cost per acquisition was £72 in LTM22, with acquisition costs relating to brochures and digital media.

# Profit and Loss Overview / Financial Performance

## Overview

Revenue has increased by £24.8 million (CAGR 56.6%) across the historical period predominantly driven by the online channel, as investment by Y combined with the market shift of consumers to online during the Covid-19 pandemic (which has sustained post-pandemic) drove higher website traffic, transactions and conversion. Consequently, despite the closure of boutqiues and concessions for much of FY21, the decline in retail sales was more than offset by increased online revenue.
Y has displayed strong EBITDA growth across the historical period with both the online and retail channels generating positive contribution (with the exception of retail in FY21 when the channel was heavily impacted by store closures during the Covid-19 pandemic). The EBITDA margin has continually improved, from 0.5% in FY19 to 20.6% in LTM22, as the business has leveraged overheads (which are relatively fixed in nature) to grow the online channel.
EBITDA growth has historically been driven by sales volume rather than margin, with gross margin remaining broadly constant at  approx. 65.0% across the historical period, before increasing to 69.7% in LTM22 which management attributes to a higher intake margin and a higher mix of full priced sales, predominantly due to improved demand forecasting (see the Cost base section of the report).
The increase in sales volume is largely driven by the online channel, with transactions increasing from 73,000 in FY19 to 214,000 in LTM22.

## Key Drivers

1. Online: FY19 – FY20: The increase was largely driven by the launch of the yy website in March 2019 (£0.6 million), continued growth of the yy customer base and an expansion in the product range.
FY20 – FY21: The significant increase predominantly relates to customer acquisition both in yy and internationally from increased marketing spend and enhanced customer communication, but is also partially attributable to the closure of high street stores amidst the Covid-19 pandemic driving increased online demand.
FY21 – LTM22: Customer acquisition has continued, and Management assert factors including head to toe dressing, higher dress mix and more efficient demand planning has driven higher AOVs and order frequency.
2. Retail: FY19 – FY20: The increase was driven by both the opening of Boutique E and a LFL increase of 52.9%, which Management attributes to increased brand awareness, an improved product range in store and styling session offerings. Concession revenue increased due to the full year impact of the Department store A concessions and the increase in floor space in the yy concession.
FY20 – FY21: Both boutique and concession revenue declined as a result of store closures amidst the Covid-19 pandemic.
FY21 – LTM22: The increase is primarily driven by the recovery in boutique revenue, with YTD September 2021 LFL growth of 40.9% compared with September 2019.
3. Direct costs: As a percentage of sales has decreased from 23.1% in FY19 to 17.1% in LTM22, principally due a higher mix of online sales (FY19; 71.4%, LTM22; 85.2%) which typically have lower direct costs due to no direct staff or occupancy costs).
4. Marketing costs: Increased by £4.0 million over the historical period, driven by a £3.3 million increase in digital marketing following a greater focus on this marketing channel for customer acquisition.
Brochure costs have also increased over the historical period by £0.6 million, £0.5 million of which came in LTM22 as a result of increased demand in the US. Brochure costs have not increased in line with sales growth due to the increased focus on digital marketing.
5. Other overheads: Increased by £3.0 million over the historical period. Largely driven by central staff costs (£2.1 million) driven by a higher headcount to support business growth, particularly in the online channel. Other overheads such as head office rent, general and IT costs have increased as a result of, or to support, business growth.
6. Returns policy: Sales from all channels can be returned within 28 days for a full refund. At Christmas, an extended returns policy is offered. Returns as a percentage of sales is much higher for the online channel than retail, due in part to customers not being able to see/feel the product and ordering multiple sizes.
Customers can return online orders in store, although these are processed by head office and recorded within the online channel accounts. Concession returns can only be processed by the concession partner.
7. Online returns: Online returns as a percentage of sales decreased to 34.5% in FY21 which Management attributes to fewer customers choosing to return orders during the lockdown periods and an increased mix in casual wear, which typically has lower rates of return.
From FY22, free returns are offered to all customers, regardless of location. The customer delivery charge is refunded by Y when the entire online order is returned.
8. Boutique returns: Boutique returns as a percentage of sales increased to 12.0% in FY21 which Management attributes to the extended returns policy during the pandemic to 90 days, with the returns rate increasing to 30.5% in June 2020 when boutiques first reopened.
