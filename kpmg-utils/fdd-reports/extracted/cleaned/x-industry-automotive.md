## Appendix 2: Fixed assets

The NBV of fixed assets has fallen by £0.4 million over the historical period despite a large scale refresh of the vehicle fleet initiated in FY21, which saw additions to the Vehicle fleet of £2.5 million in FY21. The reduction is driven by supply chain disruption resulting in increased lead times for new vehicle delivery and meant that 200 vehicles are on order currently, to be delivered by the end of FY23.
Fixed assets consists mainly of crash cushion vehicles (18t IPVs) and other vehicles (3.5t, 7.5t vehicles etc). Both vehicles for hire and for sale are reflected in the FAR.
Crash cushions reflect the 18t IPV vehicles and are depreciated at a rate of 15%, lower than other vehicle hire assets, this reflects the longevity of this asset class with some 18t IPVs aged over 10 years old still in use.
New vehicles coming into the vehicle hire fleet are now recognised in the New vehicles line of the fixed asset register, having been recognised in the Vehicles for hire line previously. This separation occurred when management increased the depreciation rate to 27.5% on new vehicle additions (previously 20%) to reflect a mix shift away from larger vehicles with lower depreciation rates.

## Appendix 3: Stock

Stock, which amounts to £0.4 million at Ap-22, consists of crash cushions, marker posts, other parts and consumables. Vehicles for sale remain in the fixed assets register until sold, these are not re-classified into stock when a decision to sell is made. Stock is mainly held at [Location 1], however some marker posts are held at a third party warehouse in xx.
In Nov-20, along with the movement to the new premises, a stock controller was employed to improve stock management. This led to the implementation of monthly stock takes and the use of the xx software system which allowed more efficient digital categorisation of stock between warehouse and specific stock. The stock classifications presented on this page reflect:
Marker posts – Marker post stock are parts and consumables associated with the production of marker posts for sale. This stock is kept at a warehouse in xx (third party premises) and is transported to the site for modification prior to sale.
General stock – Consists of smaller items of stock. The increase of these items of £0.1 million between Apr-21 and Jan-22 reflects a build in the safety stock levels.
Warehouse stock – Parts, crash cushions and other consumables which are held at the warehouse and are managed by the xx system.
Specific stock – Parts, crash cushions and other consumables which are held at the warehouse, but are currently managed by way of manual spreadsheets.
Provisioning: Management does not make stock provisions because long lead times and the niche nature of projects means stock can always be sold at cost price, consistent with no stock write-offs made in the historical period.
There continues to be a level of manual stock management, with specific stock levels updated manually, as this stock is not currently migrated to xx, presenting an opportunity for automation post deal.

Stock is recognised in [Target]’s balance sheet upon receipt into the warehouse. As such, whilst on water and until delivered to [Target]’s facility, it remains largely off balance sheet, with only a prepayment recognised against it upon placing of the order (1/3 of total invoice) and subsequently on arrival to xx (a further 1/3 of total).
When the stock is received and booked in the prepayment (reflecting 2/3 of the total cost) is unwound. This also results in a recognition of a trade creditor balance for the last 1/3 of the stock value, until settled typically 45 days after this point.
We understand from discussion with Management that this treatment of stock on water reflects Management’s assumption that stock on water was the responsibility of the supplier. However, more recently xx supplier has advised that title to stock passed at the point of dispatch, creating a discrepancy between the two methodologies.
We recommend that the terms of supplier agreements are reviewed as part of the legal due diligence.
Stock on water reflects mostly crash cushions ordered from xx and amounted to £0.2 million at Apr-22 (reflecting 12 cushions at a total cost of £163,000 which is inclusive of import costs).
We understand the value of stock on water has recently increased with more crash cushions on water (driven by the supply chain disruptions). We have considered this further in net working capital.

## Appendix 4: Trade debtors

Trade debtor days have reduced from an average of 75 days in FY20 to 45 in FY22 a result of the increased prevalence of new vehicle sales in the revenue mix which are invoiced upon vehicle delivery.
There have been no bad debt write-offs in the historical period, this is due to tight credit control with new customers without trading history invoiced on a proforma basis, after this the majority of customers are on 30 day EOM terms with a few operating on 60 day terms. [Target] also has debtor insurance which covers the majority of debtor balances although, this has been used infrequently.
The peaks in debtor days in Jan-20, Jan-21 and Jan-22 are driven by reduced trading activity in December of each year which sees a significant number of customers off-hire vehicles for a two week period of industry shut down.

## Appendix 5: Prepayments and accrued income

The build in prepayments Jan-22- Apr-22 is driven by prepayments of crash cushions on water. These items are mostly prepaid for prior to dispatch from xx (2/3 of invoice), however are recognised in stock upon receipt into the warehouse.

## Appendix 6: Trade creditors

Trade creditors and average creditor days have increased by £0.2 million (10 days) over the historical period, driven by increased purchases of crash cushions from a xx based supplier and supply chain disruptions resulting in longer lead times.
Crash cushion stock has long lead times compared to other stock (increased further by Covid-19 supply chain disruption) and is invoiced 33% on order, 33% on xx arrival and 33% 45 days after final delivery meaning the balance remains within creditors for longer periods. Other suppliers are paid 30 days after the invoice date. All suppliers are paid as part of weekly BACS runs.
Whilst it could be argued that trade creditors are currently higher than the ‘norm’ (with average days in FY21 and FY22 10 days in excess of FY20 levels) driven by supply chain issues which are likely to unwind over the longer term, it is likely that any adjustment to trade creditors will be offset to a large degree by the opposite adjustment to stock.
We therefore have not proposed to normalise NWC for trade creditors.
See earlier in the report for further discussion around NWC.
