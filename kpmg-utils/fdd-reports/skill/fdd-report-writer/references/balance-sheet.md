## Section objective

The Balance sheet section explains the balance-sheet boundary as at the relevant date in terms that matter for diligence. In most cases, it starts with a dated balance-sheet schedule or stratified view, explains the basis of presentation, and then walks the captions or grouped balances that matter most for the economic reading of the business.

It should explain what is on the balance sheet, how the main balances are grouped or stratified, which captions require special interpretation, and where classification, estimate, perimeter, source-basis, or financing issues affect the reader's understanding.

Global writing, placeholder, and language rules are defined in `references/global-writing-conventions.md` and apply here.

## Core principles

1. **Anchor the section to the right balance-sheet basis:** Be clear on the relevant date, the source basis, and whether the section is presenting a single-date snapshot, a historical balance-sheet series, or a stratified view.
2. **Use the schedule as the anchor exhibit:** Strong balance-sheet sections are built around a dated schedule, with commentary drafted beside it rather than as free-form prose.
3. **Explain material captions through composition and interpretation:** For each caption or grouped balance that matters, explain what it includes, quantify the main components where useful, and state the point that changes the diligence reading.
4. **Add interpretation selectively:** Include classification, related-party, estimate, financing, source-basis, comparability, or metric-based context only when it materially improves how the balance should be read.
5. **Keep the section centered on the balance sheet:** Explain the balance-sheet position and how the main captions fit together, without drifting into a full net working capital, net debt, or reporting environment discussion.

## Analytical workflow

1. **Confirm the balance-sheet basis:** Identify the relevant date, periods shown, reporting basis, and whether the source uses a reported balance sheet or a stratified presentation.
2. **Review the fullest support first:** Start from the full balance sheet and trial-balance or caption support where available so the section can identify the complete caption set and underlying composition.
3. **Choose the section shape and preserve useful grouping:** Determine whether the draft should read as a straightforward schedule-backed section or whether classification, related-party, financing, or estimate issues need more emphasis, while preserving source groupings such as net working capital, net debt, and other where helpful.
4. **Identify the material captions:** Determine which captions or grouped balances drive the economic reading and therefore need commentary.
5. **Map the underlying accounts into usable buckets:** Where support allows, group smaller accounts into sensible composition buckets so the commentary can explain what sits within the larger captions.
6. **Draft the section in final reading order:** Start with the basis and boundary, present the anchor schedule, and then walk the material captions or grouped balances in schedule order.
7. **Add only the supporting context that changes the reading:** Include classification, estimate, financing, source-basis, comparability, or metric-based context only where it materially improves interpretation of the balance.
8. **Stop when the balance sheet is interpretable:** Include enough detail for the reader to understand the boundary, the main balances, and the key interpretation points without turning the section into a full account listing.

## Section architecture

Scale the section based on the number of material captions, the usefulness of stratification, and the extent of classification, financing, estimate, or source-basis issues.

**Verbosity:**

- Stop adding detail when the reader can understand the balance-sheet boundary, the main caption groupings, and any issues that materially affect interpretation. In most cases, aim for one anchor schedule plus 4-8 commentary bullets and roughly 150-400 words of commentary. Move above that range only when the source supports a fuller caption-by-caption explanation.

**Required content areas:**

- Balance-sheet basis and boundary
  - State the relevant date, the source basis, and how the section is organizing the balance sheet.
- Balance-sheet schedule and stratification
  - Present the anchor schedule and preserve any useful source grouping, such as net working capital, net debt, and other, where relevant.
- Material caption commentary
  - Explain the captions or grouped balances that matter most for interpretation, usually in 4-8 bullets aligned to the schedule.

**Optional content areas:**

- Classification or perimeter context
  - Add when related-party balances, restricted items, deposits, advances, deferred balances, or unusual presentation materially change the reading.
- Estimate or close-process context
  - Add when provisions, annual-only updates, true-ups, or incomplete-close mechanics affect interpretation.
- Financing and encumbrance context
  - Add when collateral, guarantees, restrictions, lease obligations, or maturity profile help explain the balance-sheet position.
- Source basis or comparability note
  - Add when bridged reporting, management-only balances, timing, accounting policy, perimeter, or one-date limitations are relevant to how the balance should be interpreted.

## Data and drafting inputs

**Data / information typically needed:**

- The balance sheet as of the relevant date, with balance amounts for each reported caption
- Preferably the full balance sheet with all underlying trial-balance accounts as of that date
- Composition detail showing what sits within the larger captions, so smaller accounts can be grouped into logical buckets where useful
- A clear as-at date anchor and source basis, such as audited, management-reported, trial-balance based, or bridged
- Stratification logic if the balance sheet is being presented as net working capital, net debt, and other components or another grouped view
- Commentary or support for the material captions, including what they contain and how they should be interpreted
- Classification, related-party, estimate-sensitive, or financing-related facts where relevant

**Data mapping considerations:**

- Start from the full balance sheet and identify the largest captions or grouped balances first.
- Preserve the source stratification when it improves readability or reflects the underlying analysis.
- Quantify the main components within a caption when that helps the reader understand what the balance represents.
- Add only the supporting detail that changes the interpretation of the balance.
- If source information is thin, keep the section concise and stay close to the reported captions.
- If a relevant detail is missing but the point still matters, use an inline placeholder in square brackets. Example: `Inventory is primarily comprised of [...]`

**Formatting principle:**

- Use a short framing line, one anchor schedule, and caption commentary aligned to the way the schedule is presented.
- When the source uses grouped buckets, keep those labels short and source-consistent, such as `Net working capital`, `Net debt`, or `Other balances`.
- When discussing a balance that maps directly to the schedule, use the schedule label followed by a colon in bold, then explain what it includes and how it should be interpreted.

## Typical content areas

Use these as the main building blocks for the section. Choose only the content areas the draft actually needs.

The standard pattern is a short framing line, the balance-sheet schedule, and caption explanation bullets aligned to the schedule. Build any additional interpretation into the relevant caption explanation or add a brief supporting note only when needed.

### Opening framing line

- Purpose: state the relevant date, the source basis, and how the schedule is framing or stratifying the balance sheet.
- Target length: 15-35 words.
- Source note: usually not needed if the source note follows immediately.
- Sample wording: `The table below presents the reported balance sheet as at June 30, 2024, segregated into net working capital, net debt, and other components.`

### Group lead-in

- Purpose: introduce a grouped set of captions under a useful label that matches the schedule or source analysis.
- Use when: the section is organized into buckets such as net working capital, net debt, other, or another clear grouping.
- Skip when: the bullets already scan clearly without a group label.
- Target length: 5-20 words.
- Source note: not needed.
- Sample wording: `Net working capital is comprised of the following balances:`

### Caption explanation

- Purpose: explain one material caption by stating what it includes, quantifying the significant components where useful, and adding the operating, accounting, or financing detail needed to interpret it.
- Target length: 35-90 words.
- Source note: recommended when based on management explanation or non-obvious support.
- Sample wording: `**Accounts receivable:** Comprised of $0.5 million in trade receivables and $0.2 million in other receivables. The Company reports average DSO of 45 days, and the majority of balances relate to counterparties with limited historic bad debt.`

### Optional supporting notes

- Add these only when they materially improve the reader's interpretation of the schedule.
- `Classification or perimeter note:` Use when a balance includes related-party, restricted, deposit, advance, deferred, or other items whose classification changes the economic reading. Sample wording: `**Trade receivables:** Includes parent deposits recorded as credit balances in AR and therefore includes balances beyond billed-and-uncollected operating receivables.`
- `Estimate or close-process note:` Use when a balance depends on annual-only updates, provisions, or incomplete-close mechanics. Sample wording: `**Employee benefit obligations:** Updated annually rather than monthly, and the period-end balance therefore reflects the latest completed actuarial update rather than a monthly remeasurement.`
- `Financing or encumbrance note:` Use when collateral, restrictions, guarantees, or maturity profile help explain the balance-sheet position. Sample wording: `**Property, plant and equipment:** Land and buildings are pledged against the senior facility and form part of the lender security package.`
- `Source basis or comparability note:` Use when a caption depends on bridged or management-reported balances, or when timing, accounting policy, or one-date limitations affect interpretation. Sample wording: `**Trade payables and accruals:** FY24 is management-reported and reconciled to the trial balance, while the interim balance is based on management reporting supported by the latest close.`
- `Terms, metrics, or mechanics note:` Use when commercial or accounting mechanics help explain the balance. Sample wording: `**Accounts payable:** Comprised mainly of product, logistics, and marketing suppliers. The Company reports average DPO of 63 days in LTM Dec-24, reflecting 60-90 day credit terms with certain larger vendors.`

## Section-specific writing guidance

1. Use the balance-sheet schedule as the structural anchor and keep the commentary aligned to the way the schedule is presented.
2. Preserve grouped source labels, such as net working capital, net debt, and other, when they help the section scan clearly.
3. Explain each material caption through what it contains first, then the interpretation point that matters for diligence.
4. Keep cross-workstream references brief and focused on how they affect the balance-sheet reading.
5. Add supporting notes for classification, estimates, financing, source basis, or comparability only when they materially improve interpretation.

## Verification and review checks

**Verification questions:**

- Is the section anchored to a clear date basis and supported by one readable balance-sheet schedule with a source note?
- Where relevant, does the section preserve the source stratification, such as net working capital, net debt, and other components?
- Do the bullets explain the captions that matter by describing their composition, quantifying the significant balances, and adding only the detail needed to interpret them?
- Where relevant, are classification, related-party, estimate, financing, source-basis, and comparability points explained clearly within the relevant caption commentary?
- Is the section easy to scan, with grouped caption commentary and no unnecessary internal headings or repeated boilerplate?
- Where relevant information is missing, does the draft use inline placeholders in square brackets?

## Full examples

These examples show content flow, not required headings or exact bullet counts.

The examples below show a type of balance-sheet exhibit the skill might typically receive, followed by the commentary it would draft from that exhibit. The exhibit is included here to show the incoming data shape; the skill does not need to recreate the table unless the user asks for it.

The increase from FY22 to FY24 was driven mainly by higher billing volume and slightly longer settlement timing on larger customer programs.
- **Inventory:** Comprised of $17.6 million in finished goods, $7.8 million in work-in-progress, and $3.5 million in raw materials and spare parts. The increase from $22.1 million in FY22 to $27.5 million in FY24 reflects higher safety stock and a broader product mix ahead of peak seasonal demand.

- **Accrued liabilities:** Comprised primarily of payroll and bonus accruals, freight and utility accruals, and other operating expenses incurred but not yet invoiced. The increase from FY22 to FY24 is consistent with higher operating spend and headcount growth.
- **Deferred revenue and other current liabilities:** Comprised primarily of upfront billings on annual service contracts and implementation-related contract liabilities. Revenue is recognized over the service period or as performance obligations are satisfied, and the balance increased to $23.1 million in Sep-25 as customer program volume expanded.

Net debt and financing-related balances are comprised of the following captions:

- **Lease liabilities (non-current):** Represents the longer-dated portion of facility and equipment lease obligations and remained broadly stable through the period at $22.7 million as at Sep-25.

Other balances are comprised of the following captions:

- **Property, plant and equipment:** Comprised primarily of $61.4 million in distribution-hub infrastructure, $34.2 million in technology-enabled equipment, and $24.3 million in leasehold improvements. The increase over the historical period was driven by facility upgrades and equipment additions, net of depreciation.
- **Intangible assets:** Comprised mainly of capitalized platform and application development, with a smaller balance relating to acquired software and customer-related intangibles. The increase to $73.6 million reflects continued investment in software capabilities supporting product expansion.
- **Provisions and other non-current liabilities:** Comprised mainly of employee-related obligations, long-term service commitments, and other non-current operating provisions. The balance increased modestly over the period in line with operating scale.
- **Equity / (deficit):** Equity increased from $84.2 million in FY22 to $108.8 million in Sep-25, reflecting earnings generation partially offset by the funding needs of the business and broader capital structure.

The Apr-25 balance is lower than FY24 because invoices for the next academic term are typically issued in May, and the caption therefore does not reflect a steady-state collections position.
- **Inventory:** Comprised primarily of textbooks and uniforms used in the school operation. The balance remained modest at $4.6 million and is not a major working-capital driver.
- **Prepaid expenses and deposits:** Comprised of $16.2 million in deposits for campus expansion and advance project payments, with the remaining balance relating primarily to insurance, software, and other annual contracts. A portion of these balances is expected to clear into fixed assets as construction progresses.
- **Trade payables and accruals:** Comprised mainly of supplier payables, payroll accruals, utilities, and other school-related operating expenses. The Apr-25 profile reflects supplier spend and payroll cadence associated with the school calendar.
- **Deferred / unearned revenue:** Represents tuition and other fees billed or collected in advance of revenue recognition. The sharp reduction from FY24 to Apr-25 reflects the timing of billings and revenue recognition across academic terms.
