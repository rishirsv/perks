## Section objective

The Balance Sheet section explains the balance-sheet boundary as at the relevant date in terms that matter for diligence. It should tell the reader what is on the balance sheet, how the main balances are grouped or stratified, which captions require special interpretation, and where classification, estimate, perimeter, or financing issues affect the economic reading of the business.

Global writing, placeholder, and language rules are defined in `references/global-writing-conventions.md` and apply here.

## Core principles

1. **Lead with the date boundary and basis:** Make clear what date the balance sheet reflects and what reporting basis or stratification the section is using.
2. **Treat the schedule as the anchor exhibit:** Strong balance-sheet sections are built around a dated schedule or stratified table, with the commentary organized around that structure.
3. **Explain captions through composition and interpretation:** For each major caption, state what it contains, quantify the main components where useful, and explain why that matters for diligence. Add movement commentary when it helps explain the balance.
4. **Include the terms or mechanics that matter for the caption:** Where relevant, include points such as DSO, DPO, credit terms, aging, revenue-recognition mechanics, allowance policy, or facility terms within the caption explanation.
5. **Explain balances that need added context:** Related-party balances, restricted cash, deferred balances, advances, and estimate-sensitive captions should be described clearly when they are present and material.
6. **Add financing context where relevant:** Include collateral, guarantees, restrictions, or repayment profile when those facts help explain the balance-sheet position.
7. **Add source-basis or comparability context where relevant:** Include timing, annual-only adjustments, or reporting-basis differences when they help explain confidence or interpretation.
8. **Keep the section centered on the balance sheet:** Explain what the main balances represent and how they fit together, while keeping deeper workstream analysis in its own section.

## Analytical workflow

1. **Set the date basis and source basis:** Determine what date the balance sheet reflects, what reporting basis it uses, and whether the section is snapshot-based or multi-period.
2. **Start from the fullest balance-sheet support available:** Prefer the full balance sheet with all trial-balance accounts as of the relevant date so the section can identify every caption and its underlying composition.
3. **Build the anchor schedule and preserve stratification:** Organize the reported or reconciled balances into one readable schedule and keep any useful grouped view such as net working capital, net debt, and other.
4. **Identify the largest captions or subtotals:** Use the full balance sheet to determine which captions or grouped balances drive the economic reading and therefore need commentary.
5. **Map underlying accounts into logical buckets:** Where the support allows it, group smaller accounts into sensible composition buckets such as work-in-progress, finished goods, customer receivables, or accrued payroll.
6. **Write caption commentary around composition and interpretation:** Explain what each major caption includes, quantify the main components where useful, and add classification, estimate, financing, or source-basis context only where it changes the reading.
7. **Keep the section proportionate to the evidence:** For simpler sections, stay close to the main caption groups. For more complex sections, expand the caption explanations with the additional context supported by the source materials.

## Section architecture

Scale the section based on the number of balance-sheet captions that need explanation, the usefulness of stratification, and the extent of classification, financing, estimate, or reporting-basis issues.

**Verbosity:**

- Stop adding detail when the reader can understand the balance-sheet boundary as at the relevant date, the main caption groupings, and any classification, funding, or estimate issues that materially affect interpretation. In most cases, aim for one anchor schedule plus 4-8 bullets and roughly 150-400 words of commentary. Move above that range only when the source clearly supports a more detailed caption-by-caption discussion.

**Required content areas:**

- Balance-sheet basis and boundary
  - State the relevant date, the source basis, and how the section is organizing the balance sheet, usually in 1-2 opening lines or bullets.
- Balance-sheet schedule and stratification
  - Present the anchor schedule and, where relevant, show the balance sheet stratified into net working capital, net debt, and other components or another useful grouping.
- Material caption commentary
  - Explain the captions or caption groups that matter most for interpretation, usually in 4-8 bullets grouped in the clearest order for the section.

**Optional caption-level considerations:**

- Use these as interpretation lenses within the relevant caption explanation.
- Classification and interpretation
  - Explain the economic meaning of a balance when related-party, restricted, deferred, deposit, advance, or unusual classification issues are relevant.
- Estimate sensitivity
  - Explain when a balance depends on annual-only updates, provisions, true-ups, or incomplete-close processes that affect interpretation.
- Financing and encumbrance
  - Explain when collateral, guarantees, lease obligations, restrictions, or maturity profile help explain the balance-sheet position.
- Source basis and reconciliation
  - Explain when a caption depends on bridged reporting, management-only balances, or reconciliation to the underlying support.
- Comparability or limitation
  - Explain when timing, accounting policy, perimeter, or one-date limitations are relevant to how the balance should be interpreted.

**Data / information typically needed:**

- The balance sheet as of the relevant date, with balance amounts for each reported caption
- Preferably the full balance sheet with all underlying trial-balance accounts as of that date
- Composition detail showing what sits within the larger captions, so smaller accounts can be grouped into logical buckets where useful
- A clear as-at date anchor and the source basis, such as audited, management-reported, trial-balance based, or bridged
- Stratification logic if the balance sheet is being presented as net working capital, net debt, and other components or another grouped view
- Commentary or support for the material captions, including what they contain and how they should be interpreted
- Classification or interpretation issues, such as related-party balances, restricted cash, deposits, deferred balances, or unusual current / non-current presentation
- Estimate-sensitive items, annual-only entries, or incomplete-close processes when relevant
- Financing facts, such as collateral, guarantees, maturity profile, lease obligations, or restrictions, when relevant

**Data mapping considerations:**

- Treat the full balance sheet, trial-balance support, and date basis as the core inputs. Build the commentary directly from those materials.
- Use the full balance sheet to identify the largest captions or subtotals first, then decide which ones need commentary.
- Where caption support is detailed enough, group smaller accounts into logical buckets and quantify the main components within those buckets.
- If the source stratifies the balance sheet into net working capital, net debt, and other, preserve that grouping unless there is a good reason not to.
- Focus on the captions that drive the economic reading of the balance sheet and keep the commentary concentrated on those balances.
- Add estimate, financing, related-party, reconciliation, and comparability commentary where it helps explain the caption.
- If source information is thin, keep the section concise and stay close to the reported captions.
- If a relevant detail is missing but the point still matters, use an inline placeholder in square brackets. Example: `Inventory is primarily comprised of [...]`

**Formatting principle:**

- Default to one source note, one anchor schedule, and supporting bullets. Use grouped lead-ins such as `Net working capital`, `Net debt`, or `Other` when they match the structure of the underlying analysis.
- Format fiscal years as `FY24`, `FY25`, etc. Format monetary amounts as `$x.x million`, and use full amounts such as `$90,000` for balances below `$0.1 million`.

**Ordering rules:**

- Start with Balance-sheet basis and boundary.
- Follow with Balance-sheet schedule and stratification.
- Follow with Material caption commentary.
- When the schedule is stratified, follow the source grouping order. In most cases this means net working capital first, then net debt if relevant, then other balances.
- Start with the main caption explanations and build in caption-level considerations where they help explain the balances.
- Present the section as a source note plus a schedule and grouped bullets or labeled caption commentary.
- Keep the emphasis on the balances and interpretations that matter most for diligence.

## Typical content areas

Use these as the main building blocks for the section. Choose only the content areas the draft actually needs.

The standard pattern is a date / schedule framing line, optional group lead-ins, and caption explanation bullets. Build any additional interpretation into the relevant caption explanation.

Placeholder usage follows `references/global-writing-conventions.md` and is not restated in each content-area definition.

### Date basis and schedule framing

- Purpose: state the relevant date, the source basis, and how the schedule is framing or stratifying the balance sheet.
- Target length: 15-35 words.
- Source note: usually not needed if the source note follows immediately.
- Example: `The table below presents the reported balance sheet segregated into net working capital, net debt and other components, as at June 30, 2024.`

### Caption group lead-in

- Purpose: introduce a grouped set of balance-sheet captions under a useful label.
- Use when: the section is organized into buckets such as net working capital, net debt, other, or another clear grouping.
- Skip when: the bullets already scan clearly without a group label.
- Target length: 5-20 words.
- Source note: not needed.
- Example: `Net working capital is comprised of the following balances:`

### Caption explanation

- Purpose: explain one material caption by stating what it includes, quantifying the significant components where useful, and adding the operating, accounting, or financing detail needed to interpret it. Add movement when it helps interpretation.
- Target length: 35-90 words.
- Source note: recommended when based on management explanation or non-obvious support.
- Example: `**Accounts receivable:** Comprised of $0.5 million in trade receivables and $0.2 million in other receivables. The Company reports average DSO of 45 days, and the majority of balances relate to counterparties with limited historic bad debt.`

### Caption-level writing considerations

- Classification and interpretation: Where classification affects the economic meaning of a balance, explain that directly in the caption explanation. Example: `**Trade receivables:** Includes parent deposits recorded as credit balances in AR and therefore includes balances beyond billed-and-uncollected operating receivables.`
- Estimate sensitivity: Where a balance depends on annual-only updates, provisions, or incomplete-close mechanics, explain that within the caption explanation. Example: `**Employee benefit obligations:** Updated annually rather than monthly, and the period-end balance therefore reflects the latest completed actuarial update rather than a monthly remeasurement.`
- Financing and encumbrance: Where collateral, restrictions, guarantees, or maturity profile help explain the balance-sheet position, include that within the caption explanation. Example: `**Property, plant and equipment:** Land and buildings are pledged against the senior facility and form part of the lender security package.`
- Source basis and reconciliation: Where a caption depends on bridged or management-reported balances, explain that within the caption explanation. Example: `**Trade payables and accruals:** FY24 is management-reported and reconciled to the trial balance, while the interim balance is based on management reporting supported by the latest close.`
- Comparability or limitation: Where timing, policy, perimeter, or one-date limitations are relevant, explain that within the relevant caption explanation or as a brief closing note. Example: `**Trade payables:** Interim balances reflect the normal accrual timing of the business and should be interpreted together with the year-end close process.`
- Terms, metrics, and mechanics: Where a caption has relevant commercial or accounting terms, include them in the caption explanation. Example: `**Accounts payable:** Comprised mainly of product, logistics, and marketing suppliers. The Company reports average DPO of 63 days in LTM Dec-24, reflecting 60-90 day credit terms with certain larger vendors.`

## Assembly patterns

Use one of these patterns based on the section's complexity. These are practical guides, not fixed templates.

### Simple schedule-backed walkthrough

- Usual flow: one opening line establishes the date basis, followed by the anchor schedule and concise commentary on the main caption groups.
- Add comparability context only if timing or reporting-basis issues materially change the reading.
- Aim for 4-7 bullets plus one schedule.
- Stop adding detail when the reader understands the main caption groups, the balances that matter, and the one or two facts that change the diligence reading.

### Related-party or classification-heavy balance sheet

- Usual flow: establish the date basis, show the schedule, then focus the commentary on balances where related-party, perimeter, or classification context is important.
- In practice, this pattern often builds classification commentary, related-party context, and comparability context into the relevant caption explanations.
- Aim for 6-9 bullets plus one schedule.
- Stop adding detail when the reader can separate true operating balances from related-party or perimeter-driven balances and understand the implications for NWC, net debt, or closing mechanics.

### Financing or estimate-sensitive balance sheet

- Usual flow: establish the date basis, show the schedule, then use the commentary to separate ordinary caption explanation from estimate-sensitive balances and financing constraints.
- In practice, this pattern often builds estimate-sensitive commentary, financing context, and source-basis or comparability context into the relevant caption explanations.
- Aim for 7-10 bullets plus one fuller schedule.
- Stop adding detail when the reader can identify the balances requiring judgement, understand the debt or security constraints, and see whether reporting-basis issues affect confidence.

## Section-specific writing guidance

1. When discussing a balance that maps directly to a schedule caption, use the caption name followed by a colon in bold, then the explanation.
2. When the source uses grouped buckets such as net working capital, net debt, and other, keep those labels as short lead-ins for the commentary.
3. Keep cross-workstream references brief and focused on how they affect the balance-sheet reading.

## Verification and review checks

**Verification questions:**

- Is the section anchored to a clear date basis and supported by one readable balance-sheet schedule with a source note?
- Where relevant, does the section preserve the source stratification, such as net working capital, net debt, and other components?
- Do the bullets explain the captions that matter by describing their composition, quantifying the significant balances, and adding the terms or mechanics needed to interpret them?
- Where relevant, are classification, related-party, estimate, financing, reconciliation, and comparability points explained clearly within the caption commentary?
- Is the section easy to scan, with grouped caption commentary and no unnecessary internal headings or boilerplate?
- Where relevant information is missing, does the draft use inline placeholders in square brackets?

These examples show content flow, not required headings or exact bullet counts.
