Operational Performance Analysis – First National
Financial (Q1 2023 – Q1 2025)
Executive Overview
First National Financial Corporation (“FNFC”) demonstrated resilient operational performance from Q1 2023 through Q1 2025 despite a volatile interest rate environment. Over this nine-quarter span, FNFC’s
mortgages under administration (MUA) grew steadily to record levels, reaching $155.4 billion by Q1 2025
(up ~7% year-over-year). This growth in scale was achieved even as origination volumes fluctuated significantly with market conditions – contracting sharply through 2022–2023 and rebounding by early
2025. Throughout the period, FNFC leveraged its diversified business model (prime single-family and
insured multi-unit lending) to navigate the housing market cycle, with strong mortgage renewal activity and a stable servicing portfolio supporting MUA expansion and future revenue opportunities.
Profitability remained positive and well-managed, but headline earnings were distorted by interest rate hedging volatility. Reported net income varied widely quarter-to-quarter (e.g. ranging from ~$24 million to
$89 million), reflecting swings in unrealized gains/losses on financial instruments used to hedge interest rates. To assess underlying performance, management and this analysis focus on “Pre-FMV Income” –
income before fair-value gains/losses. On this basis, core pre-tax earnings were more consistent and showed moderate trends (peaking in mid-2023 and easing thereafter). For instance, in Q1 2025 FNFC’s Pre-
FMV pre-tax income was $52.6 million (–16% YoY) versus a much larger –51% YoY drop in IFRS pre-tax income once hedging losses are included. Gross revenue grew to ~$529 million in Q1 2025 (up 2% YoY)
, supported by a 12% annual increase in interest revenues from securitized mortgages, which now contribute ~70% of total revenue. However , profitability was tempered by tighter mortgage spreads,
reduced third-party underwriting fees, and higher operating costs (notably planned technology investments). Overall, quality-of-earnings considerations are important – core earnings remained solid,
but IFRS results were temporarily inflated in 2023 and depressed in 2024 by the timing of hedge mark-tomarket effects.
FNFC maintained a conservative capital structure and shareholder-friendly capital allocation throughout the period. The company’s funding model combines a $1.5 billion syndicated warehouse credit
facility (extended to 2029) and term unsecured notes, which were augmented by a new $200 million issuance in 2023 (bringing total medium-term notes to ~$600 million). These resources, along with
internally generated cash, financed FNFC’s growing portfolio of securitized mortgages without the need for equity infusion. Despite earnings volatility, FNFC sustained its regular common dividend (in fact raising it
modestly twice, to an annualized $2.50 per share by late 2024) and declared special dividends in both 2023 and 2024 – signaling confidence in the adequacy of capital and long-term earnings power. Dividend
payout ratios fluctuated when measured against IFRS net income (ranging from ~44% up to 158%), but on an after‐tax Pre-FMV earnings basis the payout remained around 80–100%, indicating that dividends were
covered by normalized earnings. This approach allowed FNFC to return excess capital to shareholders while still investing in growth (e.g. funding more mortgages on-balance sheet via securitization).

Key operational themes over the period include a shift in funding and revenue mix and a stable risk profile. FNFC leaned further into direct securitization of mortgages (particularly prime single-family loans)
to capture future net interest income, which had the short-term effect of deferring some earnings (lower immediate gain-on-sale revenue) but will enhance earnings stability going forward. Meanwhile, insured
multi-unit residential mortgages comprised an increasing share of originations and MUA, contributing upfront placement fees and diversifying the asset base. Credit quality remained a strong point – credit
losses were immaterial and ECL allowances, while rising slightly with macroeconomic uncertainty, stayed very low (the allowance was ~$7 million against a $155 billion portfolio at Q1 2025). FNFC’s predominantly
insured portfolio and prudent underwriting resulted in no significant impairment issues or credit-related earnings volatility.
Finally, FNFC expanded its ESG and public accountability initiatives. The company issued annual Public
Accountability/Sustainability reports (with Board oversight) in 2023 and 2024, complementing financial disclosures with information on its environmental, social, and governance practices. This emphasis on
transparency and stakeholder responsibility – alongside solid financial execution – positions FNFC as a reliable, well-managed player in the Canadian mortgage market. In summary, from Q1 2023 to Q1 2025
FNFC delivered consistent operating performance and asset growth, managed through cyclical headwinds with stable underlying earnings, and took deliberate actions in capital management and risk
mitigation that underpin its long-term value proposition, albeit with certain short-term QoE adjustments needed to understand true performance. The following sections provide a detailed thematic analysis of
FNFC’s quarterly performance across eight key areas, with supporting data and commentary for financial due diligence purposes.
Operating Scale & Origination Volume
Thematic Summary: FNFC’s operating scale – as measured by mortgages under administration – expanded continuously through Q1 2025, even during periods of origination weakness. Total MUA grew from the mid-
$130 billion range in early 2023 to over $155 billion by Q1 2025. This growth was driven by sustained mortgage retention and servicing, as well as renewal volumes, which helped offset cyclicality in new
originations. Origination volume (inclusive of renewals) saw a volatile trajectory: it contracted significantly year-over-year in 2022–2023 amid rising interest rates, stabilized in 2024, then rebounded sharply by early
2025 as the housing market showed signs of recovery. Single-family residential lending bore the brunt of the downturn in 2023, while the commercial (multi-unit) segment provided partial offset with comparatively
stronger volumes. By Q1 2025, origination activity had accelerated, with single-family originations leading the growth. Overall, FNFC’s platform proved scalable and adaptive – the servicing portfolio continued to
reach record levels, positioning the company for future revenue growth when origination momentum returns to more normal levels.

, and reached $155.4 billion at Q1 2025 (up 7% YoY from $145.1 billion). Sequential growth was positive albeit modest; Q1 2025 MUA was ~1.1% higher than Q4 2024 (4% annualized growth)

Origination Volume Trough and Recovery: Total mortgage originations (including renewals) hit a trough in early 2023 and then recovered by 2025. In Q1 2023, volumes were 21% lower than a year
prior – a result of the housing market downturn and higher interest rates. Volumes remained soft through mid-2023 (e.g. Q2 2023 likely saw double-digit YoY declines, inferred from Q2 2024
being flat YoY). By Q1 2024, total origination equaled the prior year’s level, as a 39% surge in commercial mortgages offset a 20% decline in single-family originations. Momentum improved

Segment Trends – Single-Family vs Commercial: Single-family origination volumes saw the most pronounced swings. In early 2023 they dropped sharply (e.g. $4.4 billion in Q1 2023 vs $5.8 billion in
Q1 2022, –25% YoY). In contrast, commercial (multi-unit) originations were more resilient, decreasing by a lesser 12% in Q1 2023 and even growing in certain quarters of 2023–24. For
instance, commercial origination grew +35% YoY in Q2 2024. The trend reversed by Q1 2025:
single-family originations jumped +34% YoY to $4.7 billion, outpacing the commercial segment’s
+18% growth (to $3.6 billion). This rotation indicates that single-family volumes are highly sensitive to interest rate cycles (contracting during high-rate periods and then leading the recovery
when rates start to ease), whereas commercial volumes remained steadier and sometimes countercyclical.
Renewals and Retention: A significant portion of FNFC’s volume each quarter came from mortgage renewals, which bolstered MUA even when new origination slowed. In late 2024 and into 2025,
renewal activity was particularly robust as mortgages originated during the low-rate era of 2018–
2020 reached maturity. Q1 2025 saw an “even stronger renewal experience” alongside new originations, as many borrowers with shorter-term mortgages from prior years returned for
refinancing. This contributed to the 8.3 billion total funding in that quarter and supported MUA growth independent of new housing market growth.
Seasonality and Quarterly Progression: Origination volumes exhibited typical seasonal patterns amplified by rate-driven behavior . Sequentially, volumes tended to rise from Q1 to the spring/
summer quarters and then moderate in Q4. For example, Q3 2023 single-family originations were
$8.3 billion (including renewals), significantly higher than the run-rate earlier in the year (26% above
Q3 2022) , partly due to seasonality and perhaps a brief uptick in home sales before the impact of higher rates fully set in. Conversely, management anticipated a slower Q4 2023 compared to Q4
2022 as the high-rate environment dampened activity. By Q4 2024, with interest rate relief on the horizon, commitments picked up (FNFC noted a 50% jump in single-family mortgage commitments
in Q3 2024), likely translating into stronger fundings in late 2024 and early 2025. Overall, quarter-to-quarter movements were driven by seasonal home buying patterns and shifts in
borrower sentiment as interest rate expectations changed.

It’s important to note the composition of originations in interpreting these trends. During the downturn,
FNFC’s multi-unit/commercial segment provided a buffer – for example, in Q1 2024, commercial mortgage volume grew 39% YoY while single-family fell 20%, resulting in flat total volume. This suggests that
financing activity in the multi-unit residential sector (often driven by government-backed affordable housing programs or refinancing of apartment portfolios) was robust even as individual homebuying slowed. The
company effectively shifted focus to segments with relative strength (commercial lending) to sustain business. Conversely, in the recovery phase by Q1 2025, pent-up demand in the single-family market led
that segment to surge more dramatically. FNFC capitalized on this with its broad broker relationships and efficient underwriting platform, which can scale up volume quickly without proportional cost increases.
Such operating leverage is inherent in FNFC’s model – as volumes rise, the largely fixed-cost underwriting infrastructure yields improved efficiency (notably, management has mentioned that higher origination
volume leverages their fixed costs and improves margins over time).
From a due diligence perspective, the swings in origination volumes highlight the importance of macroeconomic normalization in evaluating FNFC’s performance. The YoY drops in 2023 were not company- specific issues but industry-wide trends given high interest rates. FNFC’s continued growth in assets under
administration and its maintained presence in the market indicate that it did not cede competitive ground; rather , it absorbed the cyclical slowdown. Analysts would normalize revenue and earnings expectations by
considering a mid-cycle origination volume, rather than extrapolating from the low point of early 2023. By
Q1 2025, volumes were trending upward again, suggesting the cycle was turning in FNFC’s favor , with potentially significant operating momentum if interest rates remain at or below late-2024 levels (indeed,
management noted a 50% increase in commitments in Q3 2024 once rate cuts were anticipated).
Finally, FNFC’s growing MUA also underscores long-term value creation beyond immediate origination fees. As of Q3 2024, $104 billion of the MUA represented the servicing portfolio of mortgages for third
parties , which generates steady fee income. The remaining portion (e.g. $44 billion of mortgages pledged under securitization at that date) sits on FNFC’s balance sheet earning net interest margin. This
blend of on- and off-balance sheet assets means FNFC can weather short-term originations volatility: when new sales are slow, the large book of administered mortgages still contributes revenue (servicing fees,
renewal opportunities, and interest earnings). In summary, operating scale has been maintained and even enhanced through the period, laying a strong foundation for FNFC to benefit from any sustained recovery in
mortgage demand. The company’s volume trends should be viewed in context of industry cyclicality, and its performance through the cycle demonstrates robust fundamentals.

hedges. At the core operating level, the company remained profitable each quarter , with Pre-FMV Income
(a non-GAAP measure that excludes gains/losses on financial instruments) generally in the $50–$95 million range per quarter . This metric grew strongly in 2023 – reflecting higher net interest income from the
growing securitized portfolio and disciplined cost control – and then moderated in 2024 as mortgage spreads tightened and volume growth stalled. In contrast, IFRS net income oscillated dramatically: early
2023 saw depressed net income from large hedge losses, mid-2023 saw a temporary earnings surge
(boosted by hedge gains and high revenue), and 2024 saw considerably lower net income as those gains
