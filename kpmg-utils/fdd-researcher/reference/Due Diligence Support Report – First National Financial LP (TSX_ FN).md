2. Net Interest Margin (NIM) – GAAP vs. Treasury View (Capitalized
vs. Cash Basis)
FDD Context and Approach:
- Relevance: Net Interest Margin is a critical indicator of profitability for mortgage lenders, measuring the
spread between interest income on mortgages and the interest cost of funding those mortgages. For First
National, NIM analysis is complex because of different accounting treatments: some mortgages are sold
(generating upfront fee income) while others are securitized and held on-balance sheet (generating interest income over time). A due diligence team must distinguish between GAAP NIM (as reported in financial
statements) and the “economic” or cash NIM the company earns, which might be managed by the
Treasury function. Timing differences arise from IFRS accounting (e.g. capitalizing certain costs or deferring income), so QoE analysis will adjust for these to understand the true spread business.
- Due Diligence Procedures: Key steps include reconstructing the net interest margin on a cash basis: for
securitized mortgages, calculate the yield earned minus actual cash funding costs (including the effect of hedges and any deferred costs amortization). For placed mortgages, recognize that economic NIM is
realized immediately as placement fees (so while GAAP shows no ongoing NII for sold loans, the Treasury view might consider the implicit spread earned upfront). Diligence would involve reviewing the effective
interest rate (EIR) accounting – e.g. broker commissions on securitized loans are capitalized and amortized, and securitization debt issuance costs are deferred. An understanding of these practices
is needed to adjust EBITDA to a cash basis.
- Management Information Requests: The team would ask for breakdowns of net interest income by
portfolio (perhaps a yield analysis by cohort of mortgages). For example, a schedule of average mortgage rates and funding costs for prime insured, Alt-A, and commercial loans – compared to what is reported
under IFRS. Additionally, details of any capitalized interest or costs (such as interest incurred during warehousing or deferred hedging gains/losses) would be requested to quantify differences between
accounting income and cash flow. Management might also be asked for internal metrics like “spread income earned on assets vs. placements” that they use to run the business.
Analysis of NIM and Recognition Differences:
- GAAP Net Interest Income: Under IFRS, First National reports “Net interest – securitized mortgages” as
a key line item, which was $226.4 million in 2024 (up ~5% from $216.6 million in 2023). This represents the interest earned on mortgages FN holds in its securitization vehicles minus the interest paid
on the associated securitization debt. GAAP NII steadily grew through 2021–2024 as FN’s portfolio of securitized mortgages expanded (12% growth in 2024). However , the NIM (margin) within that NII
tightened, especially in late 2023 and 2024. Management noted that residential NIM was narrower because many high-margin mortgages originated during the ultra-low-rate pandemic period are now amortizing or
renewing at lower spreads, and because funding costs (like Asset-Backed Commercial Paper for short-term funding) did not immediately decrease in tandem with the central bank’s rate cuts. This dynamic caused
a slight decline in residential NII in Q1 2025 despite a larger asset base, as seen by a 2% NII drop on 12% asset growth.
- Economic vs. Accounting Timing: Due to accounting rules, some interest spread is effectively recognized
upfront as a gain on sale instead of over time. Specifically, when FN places mortgages with institutional investors, it may earn an excess spread (if the mortgage rate exceeds the investor’s required yield) which is

3. Hedges – Impact on Cash and Earnings (QoE Implications)
FDD Context and Approach:
- Relevance: First National employs a substantial hedging program to manage interest rate risk on its
mortgage pipeline and securitizations. These hedges (primarily short sales of Government of Canada bonds and repurchase agreements) can introduce significant volatility in reported earnings due to fair value
accounting. For due diligence, it is important to distinguish between real economic performance and markto-market gains or losses on hedging instruments, which may reverse over time. QoE analysis often treats these fair value changes as non-recurring or timing differences, adjusting EBITDA to better reflect
underlying cash earnings. Additionally, hedging activities can have cash flow implications (e.g. margin calls or cash settlements) that affect working capital and need to be understood.
- Due Diligence Procedures: The team would review FN’s hedge accounting policies and results: Which
hedges qualify for hedge accounting and which are recorded at FVTPL (fair value through profit or loss)?
They would quantify the impact of hedge gains/losses on each period’s earnings and cash (e.g. realized vs.
unrealized portions). A common procedure is to reconstruct earnings “without fair value noise” by removing gains/losses on financial instruments from EBITDA – essentially mirroring management’s “Pre-FMV” non-
IFRS metric. We also examine policy documents or risk management disclosures to see how FN manages interest rate risk (what instruments, tenors, and how they performed relative to mortgage
funding).
- Management Information Requests: Diligence would request a schedule of realized and unrealized
gains/losses on financial instruments over the period FY2021–Q1 2025 (FN discloses these in notes and
MD&A). Understanding whether hedge gains were consistently positive in some years and turned negative in others is key (as this can swing earnings). Additionally, the team might ask if any cash collateral
postings were required for hedges (which would be a use of cash not obvious from income statements). If applicable, details on hedge ineffectiveness or any significant derivative positions at quarter-end would be
requested to evaluate residual risk.
Analysis of Hedging Impact:
- Hedge Program Overview: First National’s primary exposure is the interest rate risk between the time a
mortgage rate is committed to a borrower and the time the loan is sold or securitized. To mitigate this, FN engages in hedging – notably by shorting Government of Canada bonds or using bond forward agreements
for single-family commitments. For multi-unit residential (commercial) commitments, FN has been able to apply hedge accounting (designating bond forwards as hedges of the actual securitization), thus largely
avoiding P&L volatility on those. However , for single-family mortgages, due to the optionality
(borrowers often can float down their rate if market rates drop before closing), FN elects not to apply hedge accounting on those commitments. As a result, gains and losses on economic hedges for singlefamily commitments hit earnings immediately each period. These fall under “Realized and unrealized
gains (losses) on financial instruments” in the income statement.
- Earnings Volatility: In the past three years (2021–2023), FN consistently recorded net gains on these
hedging instruments, as interest rates generally rose or remained volatile in a way that made FN’s short bond positions profitable. For instance, 2023 began with bond yields rising (economic sentiment
tightening), leading to hedge gains, then later yields fell, reversing some gains – but overall 2023 still saw a net $22.1 million gain on financial instruments for the year. Each gain on the hedge effectively
offset what would have been a higher interest margin earned later (as discussed in Section 2). By contrast, in Q1 2025 the situation flipped: FN faced significant losses on its hedges as the interest rate environment
turned more uncertain with yields falling. The company recorded a $19.6 million loss on financial instruments in Q1 2025 (implied from the drop from Pre-FMV $52.6M to IFRS $33.0M pre-tax). This

handle these swings; the company remained in compliance with bank covenants (debt ratios) throughout, with a healthy margin.
- Hedge Effectiveness: Another QoE consideration is whether the hedges are truly effective in protecting
margin (which affects future earnings quality). FN’s approach of taking mark-to-market hits does result in lower volatility over the full life of the mortgages – essentially, earnings timing differences aside, the
hedge ensures the company locks in a known spread on its commitments. Diligence should verify that there have been no material hedge mishaps (e.g. being under-hedged or over-hedged). The disclosures show FN’s
hedging largely achieved its goal: during the 2022–2023 rate spike, FN was protected (recording gains when otherwise future margin would have been squeezed). This gives comfort that the core earnings, once

if hedge accounting is extended) could change how gains/losses are reported. For QoE, we would watch for such changes and ensure consistency in our adjustments.

4. Debt and Debt-like Items (Corporate vs. Working Capital Debt,
Repo Mechanics)
FDD Context and Approach:
- Relevance: An essential part of due diligence is identifying all forms of debt and debt-like obligations that
