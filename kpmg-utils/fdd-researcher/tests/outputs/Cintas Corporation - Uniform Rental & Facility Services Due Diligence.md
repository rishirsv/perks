Industry Context
----------------

### 1) How route-based facility services economics work

**Mental model:** A route-based uniform/facility services business is a _distributed annuity_ built on (i) recurring service contracts, (ii) upfront investment in “in-service” inventory (uniforms/mats/etc.), and (iii) a last-mile delivery network that creates strong economies of density.

**A. The operating flywheel (sell → install → service → retain → expand)**

1.  **Customer acquisition + install (“front book”):** A new account typically requires issuing garments/items into service (sizes, embroidery, lockers, floor mats, etc.). This is a real cash and working-capital outlay that gets recovered over the life of the account through weekly/periodic rental fees.
2.  **Weekly route servicing (“annuity book”):** The core service is performed at the customer’s location: swap clean/soiled product, replenish supplies, capture add-on orders, and resolve issues. Cintas discloses that ~**95% of company revenue** is from **route servicing** performed at customer locations, recognized as service is performed.
3.  **Plant processing:** Soiled product is processed at plants (wash/dry/finish, repairs/alterations). Plant utilization and labor productivity drive gross margin; underutilization is a silent margin killer.
4.  **Retention + cross-sell (“same stop, more wallet share”):** The “best” incremental growth is adding products to an existing stop because delivery costs are already sunk (marginal profitability is often much higher than new-route growth). Vestis explicitly frames strategy around **increasing revenue per stop** and **adding customers on existing routes to increase route density**. [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)

**B. Where the money is made (and lost): unit economics by stop and by route**  
A practical way to diligence this model is to decompose **profit per stop**:

*   **Revenue per stop (weekly or monthly):** base garment rental + facility services (mats, mops, towels) + restroom supplies/service + first aid/safety, etc., less credits/allowances. Cintas notes URFS revenue is predominantly uniforms and other garments plus mats/mops/shop towels/restroom supplies and other rental services.
*   **Direct costs per stop:**
    *   **Route delivery cost per stop:** driver labor + vehicle + fuel + route supervision + “dead miles.”
    *   **Plant cost per stop (or per pound):** production labor, utilities, chemicals, maintenance, and plant overhead.
    *   **In-service inventory cost per stop:** depreciation/amortization and replacement of uniforms/mats/etc. (driven by loss/ruin, customer turnover, and quality issues).  
        Cintas explicitly describes URFS cost structure as primarily **production expenses, delivery expenses, and amortization of in-service inventory**.

**C. Density is the superpower (and the moat)**  
Route-based services behave like a local network business:

*   **High fixed cost base:** plants, route infrastructure, managers, repair/alterations capability, fleet, and service systems.
*   **Strong operating leverage within a catchment:** the incremental stop on a route is cheap _until_ you hit capacity constraints (route time, vehicle cube, plant throughput).
*   **Key implication for a private target:** the “quality” of growth matters. Growth that improves density is fundamentally different from growth that creates thin out-and-back routes. Vestis calls out route density as a scale advantage and explicitly targets growth that “increases route density and lowers cost to serve.” [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)

**D. Why retention is existential**  
This model is front-loaded (inventory issuance, onboarding, sometimes higher early service cost). If a customer churns before payback, the economics degrade fast. Vestis highlights retention as a key driver and reports **customer retention improved from 90.4% (FY2023) to 91.9% (FY2024)**, defining it as **lost annualized recurring revenue ÷ total trailing-52-week annualized recurring revenue** (a “leading indicator” because it annualizes the impact immediately). [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)

**E. Industry backdrop (what “normal” feels like)**  
The Textile Rental Services Association (TRSA) / Baird survey (Q4 2025) suggests a steady, essential-services demand profile, with respondents expecting **~3.6% uniform rental revenue growth** over the next 12 months and modest pricing actions on existing accounts in that survey sample.

### 2) Key terms and definitions (use these in diligence)

*   **Stop:** a customer delivery location visited on a recurring cadence (often weekly).
*   **Route density:** how many revenue-generating stops can be serviced within a geographic area and time window; common proxies include **stops per mile**, **miles per stop**, and **route hours per stop**.
*   **Stops per route (per day/week):** productivity metric for route service reps; constrained by geography, service complexity, and time windows.
*   **Revenue per stop:** average billings per stop per service period (weekly/monthly), including add-ons; track gross and net of credits.
*   **In-service inventory (“merchandise in service”):** uniforms/mats/etc. currently issued to customers and circulating through the system. In Cintas’ balance sheet this is a large asset line (**$1.14B at May 31, 2025**)—a reminder this is an inventory- and capex-heavy service model.
*   **Retention (gross):** share of recurring revenue retained from the prior year cohort (definitions vary; align on a single definition early). Vestis’ definition above is a useful template because it annualizes churn impact. [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)
*   **Revenue per stop expansion / cross-sell:** adding categories (e.g., first aid, restroom supplies) to an existing stop to leverage fixed delivery cost. Vestis notes customers on average use ~**30–40%** of its full product line—an explicit “white space” thesis. [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)

### 3) Key metrics (what they mean, how they move, and how to diligence them)

#### A. Route density

**Why it matters:** Density is the biggest swing factor in delivery cost per stop and the biggest structural advantage large platforms have over local independents.

**How to measure (ask for route-level extracts):**

*   Stops per route-day / route-week
*   Miles driven per route-day and **miles per stop**
*   Route service hours per day; overtime %
*   “Deadhead” miles (depot → first stop, last stop → depot)
*   Revenue per mile and gross profit per mile

**Diligence focus:**

*   Identify sparse routes (high miles/stop) vs dense routes (high stops/day).
*   Confirm whether growth strategy improves density or creates “edge routes.”
*   Look for capability investments that enable density (routing software, customer portals, service check-in). Vestis explicitly calls out network optimization and routing/scheduling initiatives to lower cost to serve. [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)

#### B. Stops per route (and route productivity)

**Why it matters:** Stops per route is the operational bridge between revenue growth and labor/fleet inflation.

**What moves it:**

*   Geography and stop dispersion
*   Service complexity (uniform counts, restroom program, mat swaps)
*   Time-window constraints
*   Route rep tenure/turnover
*   Service failures (revisits, callbacks)

**Diligence focus:**

*   Compare stops/day across routes and depots; isolate the bottom quartile.
*   Analyze service “touch time” per stop (driver scan/check-in timestamps if available).
*   Test for hidden productivity leakage: frequent credits, re-deliveries, shortage runs.

#### C. Revenue per stop

**Why it matters:** The best businesses compound by increasing wallet share at existing stops and maintaining pricing discipline.

**How to measure:**

*   Revenue per stop per week (or per service)
*   Revenue mix by category (uniform core vs facility services vs add-ons)
*   Add-on attach rate (first aid, restroom, mats, safety)
*   Credits/allowances as % of billings (service quality proxy)

**Comparable signals:**

*   Cintas attributes URFS growth to **new business, penetration of additional products/services into existing customers, and price increases** (offset by lost business). That’s a useful growth decomposition lens for a private target.
*   Vestis frames its strategy similarly: cross-sell to raise revenue per stop and add customers on existing routes to increase density. [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)

#### D. Retention (and churn mechanics)

**Why it matters:** Retention underwrites payback on in-service inventory and selling costs.

**Comparable definition to adopt (highly recommended):**  
Use Vestis’ approach as a starting point because it is precise and operational: **customer retention = lost annualized recurring revenue ÷ trailing-52-week annualized recurring revenue**, taking the full annualized impact of a loss in the period it’s reported. [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)

**Benchmarks / “sanity check”:**  
A scaled competitor reporting **~low 90s retention** (Vestis: 91.9% FY2024; improved vs FY2023) gives you an external anchor for what a mature book can look like—though definition alignment is critical. [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)

**Diligence focus:**

*   Build a churn waterfall: lost accounts vs downsizes vs price-downs vs service failures.
*   Cohort retention by vintage and by route/branch.
*   Identify “at-risk” cohorts: large multi-site customers, customers with frequent credits, routes with high rep turnover.

#### D. “Competition happens at the stop” — what customers actually buy

Across scaled competitors, customer value propositions tend to converge: consistent on-time service, quality, safety/compliance support, and reduced hassle vs in-house. The battle is usually fought through (i) service reliability, (ii) price/fees, (iii) route rep relationship, and (iv) breadth of product bundle.

Diligence Playbook (private-company deal lens)
----------------------------------------------

### Value driver tree (route-based services)

**Revenue**

*   **Active stops** × **weekly revenue per stop**
    *   Base rental (uniforms + facility services)
    *   Add-ons (restroom, first aid/safety, mats, etc.)
    *   **Less:** credits/allowances, concessions, bad debt
*   **Growth decomposition:**
    *   New stops on existing routes (best) vs new routes/territories (riskier)
    *   Revenue per stop expansion (cross-sell + price)

**Gross profit**

*   **Route cost to serve:** driver labor, vehicle, fuel, route supervision
*   **Plant cost to process:** production labor + utilities + maintenance
*   **In-service inventory cost:** depreciation/amortization + replacement + loss/ruin  
    (These map directly to how Cintas describes URFS cost drivers and how Vestis frames “merchandise in service costs.”) [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)

**SG&A**

*   Salesforce productivity and tenure
*   Branch overhead and corporate allocation
*   Technology investments (routing, portals, service check-in) [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)

### P&L walkthrough: what to test in diligence

1.  **Revenue quality and pricing mechanics**
    *   Contract structure (term, escalation, surcharges, minimums), and how often pricing is actually realized.
    *   Invoice sampling: identify “silent” discounting via credits, waived fees, off-cycle replacements.
    *   Mix shift: uniforms vs facility services vs add-ons; understand gross margin profile by category.
2.  **Retention + churn root causes (your #1 underwriting workstream)**
    *   Reconcile a retention KPI to the billing system (choose a definition; Vestis’ is a strong template). [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)
    *   Cohort analysis: churn by customer vintage, geography, rep, and vertical.
    *   “Service health” analytics: credits per stop, missed services, re-deliveries, complaint volume.
3.  **Route economics and density**
    *   Route profitability model: stops, miles, hours, wage rates, overtime, vehicle cost, and allocated plant cost.
    *   Map routes against competitors/metro clusters: where does the target have structural density vs “edge routes”?
    *   Capacity constraints: are routes capped by time windows, truck cube, or driver shortages?
4.  **Plant operations and capacity**
    *   Throughput and utilization: pounds processed per labor hour, downtime, rewash/rework, repair/alteration capacity.
    *   Utility exposure: energy and water usage intensity; sensitivity analysis (public comps cite energy efficiency as a lever).
    *   Capex sufficiency: maintenance vs growth capex; deferred maintenance risk.
5.  **In-service inventory accounting and cash conversion**
    *   Policy review: capitalization, useful lives, and impairment triggers for rental inventory.
    *   Loss/ruin and replacement discipline: are costs booked consistently vs “managed” through capitalization?
    *   Installation economics: payback period by account cohort (large installs can mask churn-driven value destruction).
6.  **Working capital + “hidden financing”**
    *   AR aging by customer/route; bad debt trends; concentration in slower-paying verticals.
    *   Any securitization/factoring arrangements, and normalization to “cash EBITDA.”
    *   If contract acquisition costs (e.g., commissions) are capitalized, quantify and normalize vs comparables.

### Common EBITDA adjustments seen in route-based services

*   One-time legal settlements / gains on property sales (public comps disclose these items; private targets often have analogs).
*   Restructuring / route optimization initiatives (severance, facility rationalization)
*   Insurance claim volatility (workers’ comp, auto) and unusual safety incidents
*   Standalone/public-company costs (if a carve-out) and rebranding/separation costs (seen in Vestis disclosures) [SEC](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1967649/000196764924000060/vsts-20240927.htm)
