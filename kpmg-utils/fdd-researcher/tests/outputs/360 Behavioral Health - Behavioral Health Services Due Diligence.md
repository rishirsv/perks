Industry Context
----------------

### 1) Behavioral health “facility economics” vs. community-based economics

Behavioral health spans multiple “levels of care,” each with distinct unit economics and operating constraints. Higher-acuity settings (inpatient psych hospitals/units, residential treatment centers) behave like classic facilities: fixed capacity, high fixed costs, and revenue tied to occupancy and length of stay. Lower-acuity settings (IOP/PHP, outpatient therapy, ABA/autism services) behave more like clinician-capacity businesses: revenue tied to clinician time, authorizations, attendance, and reimbursement per visit/hour/unit.

**Inpatient psychiatric facilities (IPF) economics (bed-based):**

*   **Revenue model:** Medicare’s IPF PPS is explicitly **per diem**—a standardized federal daily rate per patient day with adjustments for patient and facility characteristics. That structure is a good conceptual anchor even when the target is not Medicare-heavy, because many commercial/Medicaid arrangements also function as per-diem or case-rate constructs for higher-acuity programs.
*   **Cost model:** Nursing/clinical staffing, milieu/tech staff, and facility overhead create meaningful fixed cost. As a result, **occupancy** (average daily census vs. staffed/available beds) is often the single biggest driver of margin variability; second-order drivers include **length of stay (LOS)**, patient acuity mix, and staffing ratios (including overtime and agency usage).

**PHP/IOP economics (day-based, per-diem payments in Medicare OPPS framework):**

*   CMS describes IOP as a structured outpatient psychiatric program paid **on a per diem basis** (with minimum weekly hours) and with rate structures often tied to services per day. CMS similarly describes PHP as an intensive structured outpatient alternative to psychiatric hospitalization, also paid **per diem** with minimum weekly hours.
*   Operationally, PHP/IOP economics are driven by **attendance**, **group census**, clinician schedules, and the ability to maintain program “throughput” while meeting medical necessity and documentation standards.

**ABA/autism services economics (hour/unit-based, authorization-driven):**

*   Medi-Cal covers medically necessary Behavioral Health Treatment (BHT) for eligible members under 21, including ABA and other evidence-based behavioral interventions; delivery differs based on whether the member is in managed care vs. fee-for-service arrangements (and, notably, California expanded pathways for fee-for-service members to access enrolled Qualified Autism Service providers).
*   For ABA providers, the “facility” is often a clinic site rather than a licensed bed facility. The economic engine is **billable clinical hours/units**, constrained by (i) patient authorization and attendance, (ii) technician/BCBA capacity and productivity, and (iii) reimbursement yield by payor and code mix.

### 2) How behavioral health economics work (core mechanics)

Across behavioral health settings, EBITDA is typically a function of four levers: **volume × yield − clinical labor − overhead**, with working capital often material because of documentation, authorizations, denials, and payor payment lag.

**A. Revenue = capacity utilization × reimbursement yield**

*   **Facility-based (inpatient/residential):**
    *   _Capacity_ = licensed beds (or staffed beds).
    *   _Utilization_ = occupancy (average daily census) and LOS.
    *   _Yield_ = per diem/case rate by payor, plus add-ons (e.g., outlier policies in Medicare IPF PPS).
*   **Program-based (PHP/IOP):**
    *   _Capacity_ = clinician schedules and program slots.
    *   _Utilization_ = attended treatment days and group fill rates.
    *   _Yield_ = per diem program payments / contracted rates (with Medicare’s per-diem framework as a reference point).
*   **ABA/community-based:**
    *   _Capacity_ = technician (e.g., RBT) billable hours and BCBA supervision capacity.
    *   _Utilization_ = scheduled vs delivered hours (cancellations/no-shows, staffing gaps, travel time).
    *   _Yield_ = reimbursement per unit/hour by payor and code mix; payor rules (documentation, authorization, UR) directly affect realizable yield.

**B. Clinical labor is usually the dominant cost line**  
Behavioral health is labor-intensive and credential-dependent. For ABA models, technicians deliver much of the direct care under supervision, while BCBAs (and similar licensed/certified clinicians) provide assessment, program design, supervision, and higher-acuity interventions. BACB describes RBTs as paraprofessionals practicing under close, ongoing supervision, and BCBAs as graduate-level independent practitioners who can supervise RBTs—this supervision stack is a core economic constraint because it shapes caseload capacity and required overhead clinical time.

**C. Mix matters: payor and authorization dynamics can change margin more than volume**

*   **Commercial vs Medicaid:** Commercial reimbursement may be higher, but it can come with heavy utilization management and network constraints. Medicaid programs may have standardized rates and evolving policy, but typically involve robust program integrity controls.
*   In California, **Medi-Cal BHT** eligibility and access rules are explicit (under-21, physician/psychologist medical necessity determination), and delivery pathways differ by managed care vs fee-for-service (including the July 1, 2025 option for certain FFS members to use enrolled QAS providers).

**D. Working capital and compliance are “silent” value drivers**  
Behavioral health often features longer AR cycles due to: prior authorizations, documentation requirements, denials/appeals, periodic re-authorization, and retrospective audits. Strong revenue-cycle processes (timely notes, coding accuracy, authorization tracking, denial management) are frequently the difference between “paper revenue” and cash EBITDA.

### 3) Key healthcare services metrics (definitions + what “good” looks like in diligence)

#### A) Facility metrics (inpatient/residential/PHP/IOP)

Even if 360 Behavioral Health is primarily ABA-oriented, deal teams often ask for facility-style metrics because they are common across behavioral health.

*   **Patient days:** Total days of care delivered in a period.
    *   _Average Daily Census (ADC)_ = Patient days ÷ Days in period.
*   **Occupancy:** ADC ÷ Available (staffed) beds.
*   **Length of stay (LOS):** Patient days ÷ Discharges.
*   **Revenue per patient day (RPPD):** Net revenue ÷ Patient days.

*   **Payor mix:** Patient days or revenue by payor category (Commercial, Medicare, Medicaid/Medi-Cal, Self-pay/Other).
*   **Program intensity metrics (PHP/IOP):** Attended program days; average group size; “3 services/day vs 4+ services/day” mix (relevant in Medicare’s OPPS rate constructs).
