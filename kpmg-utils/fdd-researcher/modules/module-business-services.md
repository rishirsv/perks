# Business Services Industry Module: Professional Services, Outsourced Services, Staffing

This module is designed for **kickoff briefs** and early diligence when you have sparse materials (teaser/CIM/VDD excerpt/limited financials). It’s written to be practical for generalists and to translate quickly into **data requests**, **management questions**, and **value driver trees**.

---

## Part 1: Industry Identification

### Question 1: What words, phrases, or concepts signal “Business Services (Professional Services, Outsourced Services, Staffing)”?

Think “**people + time + rate + utilization**” (professional services), “**seats/transactions + SLA**” (outsourced/BPO), and “**pay/bill spread + weekly payroll**” (staffing).

**Business model language / descriptors**

* **Professional services / IT services / consulting**

  * “Consulting,” “advisory,” “implementation,” “managed services,” “systems integration,” “digital transformation”
  * “Statement of work (SOW),” “MSA,” “change orders,” “deliverables,” “project-based,” “time & materials (T&M),” “fixed fee”
  * “Onshore/offshore delivery,” “nearshore,” “global delivery model,” “resource pyramid”
* **Outsourced services / BPO**

  * “Outsourcing,” “BPO,” “business process services,” “customer experience (CX),” “call center,” “contact center”
  * “Per seat,” “per transaction,” “per claim,” “per ticket,” “outcome-based pricing”
  * “SLA,” “KPI penalties,” “service credits,” “quality scores,” “AHT,” “occupancy”
* **Staffing / recruiting**

  * “Staffing,” “temporary staffing,” “contract staffing,” “staff augmentation,” “contingent labor,” “temp-to-perm”
  * “Permanent placement,” “retained search,” “contingent search,” “success fee”
  * “MSP,” “VMS,” “rate card,” “markup,” “spread,” “bill rate,” “pay rate,” “agency margin”

**KPI terminology that shows up in CIMs/VDDs**

* “**Utilization**,” “billable utilization,” “billable hours,” “bench”
* “**Realization**” (billing realization / project realization)
* “**Blended rate / average bill rate**”
* “**Gross profit**,” “gross margin,” “contribution margin,” “spread,” “take rate”
* “**Headcount**,” “FTE,” “billable FTE,” “contractor headcount,” “consultant pyramid”
* “**Backlog**,” “bookings,” “book-to-bill,” “pipeline,” “TCV/ACV” (in managed services contexts)
* “**Fill rate**,” “time-to-fill,” “redeployment,” “submittals-to-interviews-to-offers”
* “**DSO**,” “unbilled AR,” “WIP,” “contract assets” (ASC 606 wording)

**Customer / transaction descriptions**

* “Top accounts,” “enterprise clients,” “MSP programs,” “preferred vendor,” “vendor consolidation”
* “Long-term master service agreements,” “renewals,” “rate renegotiations”
* “Workforce solutions,” “onsite programs,” “RPO” (recruitment process outsourcing)

**Revenue model indicators**

* “Hourly billings,” “rate card,” “timesheets approved,” “weekly/biweekly invoicing”
* “Retainers,” “monthly recurring managed services fees”
* “Pass-through costs,” “reimbursables,” “subcontractors” (and whether they are shown **gross** or **net**)

## Part 2: Units That Matter

### Question 2: The 5–8 KPIs a generalist must understand

These are the “don’t embarrass yourself” metrics across **professional services + outsourced services + staffing**.

| KPI                                                                    | Plain-language definition (how operators use it)                                                                                                                                                                                                               | Why it matters in diligence                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Utilization (Util % / Billable Utilization)**                        | % of available delivery time that is **billable to clients** (e.g., billable hours ÷ available hours). Often excludes PTO/holidays; definitions vary.                                                                                                          | In people businesses, **1–3 pts of utilization** can be the difference between meeting plan and missing badly. Also reveals “bench” risk and whether EBITDA is being propped up by unsustainable overtime.                 |
| **Bill Rate / Blended Bill Rate ($/hr)**                               | What clients actually pay per hour (or per day) on average **after discounts and mix**. Usually tracked by role, client, geography, contract type.                                                                                                             | Core pricing lever. Needed for **price/volume/mix** analysis. Also signals bargaining power and whether wage inflation can be passed through.                                                                              |
| **Pay Rate / Cost Rate + Burden ($/hr)**                               | For staffing: what you pay the worker/contractor. For pro services: fully loaded delivery labor cost (salary + benefits + payroll taxes + bonus) per billable hour. “Burden” often includes employer taxes, benefits, workers comp, health insurance.          | Margin is often a **spread game**. If wages rise faster than bill rates, gross profit compresses even if revenue grows. Burden assumptions can hide underwriting errors.                                                   |
| **Gross Profit / Gross Margin / Spread / Take Rate**                   | The “real revenue” in many business services. For staffing, gross profit is typically **(bill rate − pay rate − burden) × hours**; revenue may be “gross billings” including wages. For pro services/BPO, gross profit is revenue minus direct delivery costs. | Prevents the classic mistake: focusing on “revenue growth” when **gross profit per hour/seat** is deteriorating. Also critical for comps and valuation, because some markets talk in **gross profit** rather than revenue. |
| **Realization (Billing & Project Realization %)**                      | How much of standard time/cost is actually recovered: **billed amount ÷ standard billable value** (billing realization), and/or **delivered margin vs expected margin**. Often impacted by write-downs, change orders, scope creep.                            | The early warning system for fixed-fee projects and weak contract management. Persistent realization leakage = hidden margin problem.                                                                                      |
| **Capacity & Productivity (Billable FTE, Billable Hours, GP per FTE)** | Output per unit of labor: billable hours per consultant, **gross profit per billable head**, or GP per recruiter (staffing).                                                                                                                                   | Separates “growth via hiring” from “growth via productivity.” Also key to spotting overstaffed G&A or branch inefficiency.                                                                                                 |
| **Backlog / Bookings / Book-to-Bill**                                  | **Backlog** = contracted future work not yet recognized. **Bookings** = new contracted work signed in period. **Book-to-bill** = bookings ÷ revenue recognized.                                                                                                | Core forward indicator for project/managed services. Low book-to-bill can foreshadow revenue declines post-close even if trailing revenue is strong.                                                                       |
| **Fill Rate / Time-to-Fill / Redeployment (staffing-heavy)**           | Fill rate = % of reqs filled. Time-to-fill = days to place. Redeployment = ability to place workers again quickly after assignment ends.                                                                                                                       | Reveals recruiting engine health and risk of “we can’t hire fast enough” or “we’re filling with low quality.” Impacts revenue, gross margin (overtime/premium pay), and customer retention.                                |

## Part 3: Value Driver Framework

### Question 3: EBITDA value driver tree for Business Services

Below is a generalized tree with **sub-segment variants**. The assistant should pick the branch that matches the deal (professional services vs outsourced/BPO vs staffing).

```
EBITDA
├── Net Revenue (or "Net Service Revenue" / "Gross Profit" depending on how the company presents)
│   ├── Gross Revenue / Gross Billings
│   │   ├── Professional Services (T&M-heavy)
│   │   │   ├── Billable Hours × Avg Bill Rate
│   │   │   │   ├── Billable Hours = Billable FTE × Available Hours × Utilization
│   │   │   │   └── Mix: seniority, skill, geo, client vertical, delivery model (on/offshore)
│   │   │   └── Fixed Fee / Milestone Revenue Recognized
│   │   │       ├── # Projects × Avg Project Fee
│   │   │       └── % complete / milestones / acceptance
│   │   ├── Outsourced / Managed Services (BPO/CX)
│   │   │   ├── # Seats × Price per Seat × Billable Seat Utilization
│   │   │   ├── OR # Transactions × Price per Transaction
│   │   │   └── Mix: channel, complexity, language, geography, client tier, SLA tier
│   │   └── Staffing
│   │       ├── Temp/Contract: Contractor Hours × Avg Bill Rate
│   │       ├── Perm Placement: # Placements × Avg Fee
│   │       └── Mix: skill/vertical, contract duration, VMS/MSP vs direct, geo, premium shifts
│   └── Gross-to-Net Adjustments
│       ├── Discounts / rate concessions / volume pricing
│       ├── Credits, refunds, service credits (SLA penalties)
│       ├── Write-offs / disputes / bad debt (sometimes below gross profit, but often effectively here)
│       ├── Pass-through netting (reimbursables, travel, 3rd-party costs shown gross vs net)
│       └── FX (if relevant), rebates/earn-backs (MSP/VMS programs)
├── COGS / Direct Costs (delivery costs)
│   ├── Direct Labor (W-2 delivery staff)
│   │   ├── Salaries & wages
│   │   ├── Benefits + payroll taxes
│   │   ├── Variable comp / bonuses (delivery-linked)
│   │   └── Bench cost (if classified in COGS) / paid non-billable
│   ├── Contract Labor / Subcontractors (1099, partner firms)
│   │   ├── Subcontractor rates × hours
│   │   └── Mix: subcontractor % of delivery, premium/urgent roles
│   ├── Staffing-specific direct costs
│   │   ├── Temporary wages (largest line)
│   │   ├── Payroll taxes + benefits + workers comp
│   │   └── Chargebacks / timecard errors / overtime premiums
│   ├── Delivery Opex (sometimes in COGS)
│   │   ├── Delivery tools/licenses, telecom (call center)
│   │   ├── Background checks / credentialing
│   │   └── Travel (if not reimbursed)
│   └── Gross Margin / Contribution Margin
└── OpEx (SG&A)
    ├── Sales & Marketing (S&M)
    │   ├── Sales headcount × comp plans (commissions)
    │   ├── Pipeline gen spend (events, marketing)
    │   └── Bid/proposal costs (if expensed)
    ├── Recruiting (staffing-heavy, but also pro services)
    │   ├── Recruiter headcount × productivity
    │   ├── Advertising/job boards, sourcing tools
    │   └── Referral/placement bonuses
    ├── G&A / Corporate overhead
    │   ├── Leadership + finance + HR + IT + legal
    │   ├── Facilities / rent / utilities
    │   └── Insurance (E&O, cyber, general liability)
    └── Other
        ├── Bad debt expense (if below gross profit)
        ├── Systems (ATS/CRM/ERP), data & security
        └── One-time items / restructuring (QoE focus)
```

**Key mix effects that matter (and often hide in “blended” metrics)**

* **Skill/seniority mix:** senior consultants raise bill rates but can reduce utilization if harder to staff.
* **Client mix:** enterprise/MSP clients may have lower rates but steadier volumes.
* **Contract type mix:** fixed-fee can look high margin until overruns hit realization.
* **Geography/delivery model mix:** offshore/nearshore raises margin but can affect win rates and quality/SLA costs.
* **Vertical mix:** cyclicality (construction/industrial staffing vs healthcare/IT) changes risk profile.

## Part 3 (cont.): Cost Structure & Margin Drivers

### Question 4: Full cost structure and what drives margins

#### A) Gross Margin / COGS

**What’s in COGS (typical)**

* **Professional services:** delivery payroll (salaries), benefits/taxes, delivery bonus, subcontractors, delivery tools/licenses, travel not reimbursed, project-specific costs.
* **Outsourced/BPO:** frontline agent wages, supervisors, site costs sometimes, telecom, platform costs, training, attrition-related training/replacement costs.
* **Staffing:** temporary wages, payroll taxes, benefits, workers comp, sometimes background checks and onboarding.

**Typical margin reality (ranges are highly segment-dependent)**

* **Staffing:** reported *gross margin %* can look “low” because wages sit in revenue/COGS; what matters is **gross profit per hour** and **spread**.
* **Professional services/BPO:** gross margin depends on utilization + delivery cost model + pricing power; firms with offshore delivery and strong utilization tend to outperform.

Rather than anchor on a single “industry margin,” the diligence framing should be:

* What is **gross profit per unit** (per hour, per seat, per transaction, per placement)?
* What is the trend and why (price vs wage vs mix vs utilization)?

**What drives cost per unit**

* Pay rates / salary bands and wage inflation
* Burden rates (benefits, taxes, workers comp) and claims experience
* Subcontractor reliance and premium rates for scarce skills
* Overtime, shift premiums, and “rush fill” costs
* Training and churn (especially BPO and high-turnover staffing)

**What makes gross margin move**

* **Utilization** (pro services)
* **Spread compression** (staffing)
* **Realization leakage** (fixed-fee / scope creep)
* **Mix** (skills, client type, geo, contract type)
* **Subcontractor mix** (often margin dilutive)

#### B) Operating Expenses (full breakdown)

Below is a practical P&L map (what an associate should expect to see and how to think about it).

**1) Sales & Marketing (typically semi-variable)**

* **Drivers:** sales headcount, quota coverage, commission rate, marketing programs, bid/proposal activity
* **What to watch:** sales productivity, pipeline conversion, reliance on a few rainmakers
* **Controllable?** mostly yes (but cutting too hard can hit bookings)

**2) Recruiting / Talent Acquisition (variable to growth)**

* **Drivers:** recruiter headcount, sourcing tools spend, advertising, referral/placement bonuses
* **What to watch:** recruiter productivity and cost-per-hire; whether recruiting cost is “hidden” in G&A

**3) G&A / Corporate**

* **Drivers:** leadership layers, shared services, IT, HR, finance, legal, compliance
* **Fixed vs variable:** more fixed (but can scale with acquisitions)
* **Controllable?** yes, but time to realize savings can be long

**4) Facilities / Branch / Site costs**

* **Drivers:** # branches/sites, seat count, occupancy rate, local overhead
* **Outsourced/BPO nuance:** site footprint and seat utilization can materially impact margin

**5) Technology & Systems**

* **Drivers:** ATS/CRM/ERP licenses, VMS integration, cybersecurity, analytics tools
* **Diligence nuance:** “IT spend is low” can be a future capex/op-ex trap

**Headcount-driven vs non-headcount**

* Headcount-driven: delivery labor, recruiters, sales, G&A staff
* Non-headcount: software, rent, telecom, insurance, professional fees, advertising

**Corporate/central vs operational/site-level**

* Central: finance, HR, IT, legal, leadership
* Operational: branch managers, onsite supervisors, delivery PMO, local recruiting

#### C) Unit economics (how profitability is created)

Pick the unit that matches the business model:

**Professional services (per consultant)**

* Profit per consultant ≈ (Bill rate − fully loaded cost per hour) × billable hours
  Key levers: utilization, bill rate, delivery cost, mix, realization.

**Staffing (per contractor hour)**

* Gross profit per hour ≈ bill rate − pay rate − burden
  Then scale by hours and subtract recruiter/branch overhead.
  Key levers: spread, timecard accuracy, overtime control, mix, DSO/bad debt.

**BPO/outsourced (per seat or per transaction)**

* Contribution per seat-month ≈ price per seat − cost per seat (wages + overhead + platform)
  Key levers: occupancy/seat utilization, attrition/training cost, SLA penalties, automation.

#### D) Margin differentiation: high-margin vs low-margin operators

**Higher-margin operators typically have:**

* Specialization (scarce talent / regulated niches) → pricing power
* Strong demand generation and account control → less MSP/VMS margin pressure
* Better utilization management (forecasting, staffing, bench discipline)
* Lower attrition (delivery + recruiting) → lower replacement and training drag
* More scalable delivery model (nearshore/offshore, shared delivery centers)
* Strong contract governance (scope, change orders, SLAs) → higher realization

**Lower-margin operators often show:**

* Commodity service lines competing on price
* High subcontractor % to meet demand (margin dilutive)
* High overtime and churn
* Weak collections and high disputes
* “Growth” that is really **gross billings growth** with flat or declining gross profit per unit

## Part 3 (cont.): Working Capital

### Question 5: Working Capital value driver tree

Business services working capital is often about **AR + unbilled/WIP** and **timing mismatch between payroll and collections** (especially staffing).

```
Operating Working Capital
├── Receivables (AR) + Contract Assets
│   ├── Billed AR
│   │   ├── Billing frequency (weekly/biweekly/monthly)
│   │   ├── Payment terms (Net 15/30/45/60+)
│   │   ├── Client approval cycle (timesheets, milestones)
│   │   └── Disputes/deductions (rate card, timecard errors, SLA penalties)
│   ├── Unbilled AR / WIP / Contract Assets (ASC 606)
│   │   ├── % complete estimates (fixed-fee projects)
│   │   ├── Milestone acceptance timing
│   │   └── Change orders pending approval
│   └── Allowance / bad debt
│       ├── Client credit quality
│       ├── Concentration risk
│       └── Dispute history / chargebacks
├── Inventory (usually minimal)
│   ├── Typically N/A for pure services
│   └── If facilities/field services: supplies, uniforms, spare parts (watch obsolescence/controls)
├── Payables (AP) + Accrued Liabilities
│   ├── Vendor terms (Net 30/45/60)
│   ├── Subcontractor payment cadence (often faster than client terms)
│   ├── Accrued payroll, bonuses, commissions
│   ├── Payroll taxes payable
│   └── Insurance accruals (workers comp, health, claims)
└── Other industry-specific items
    ├── Deferred revenue / contract liabilities (retainters, prepaid managed services)
    ├── Deposits / retainers / client prepayments (less common except some managed services)
    ├── MSP/VMS program rebates/earn-backs accruals
    └── Litigation/claims reserves (can be current or non-current)
```

#### Receivables: what drives DSO (and typical nuances)

* **DSO drivers**

  * Invoicing speed and accuracy (timecards, rate compliance)
  * Client approval workflow (especially VMS/MSP)
  * Dispute resolution speed
  * Billing model (milestones can push AR out)
* **Common nuance**

  * Staffing often has **weekly payroll** but **Net 30–60 collections** → working capital strain.
  * Pro services fixed-fee can build **unbilled AR/WIP** if milestone acceptance lags.

#### Payables: what drives DPO

* Vendor terms, scale, and procurement discipline
* Subcontractors often demand faster pay than large enterprise clients pay you

#### Cash conversion cycle (CCC)

* Pure services: inventory near zero, so CCC ≈ DSO − DPO (plus effects of accruals)
* Staffing nuance: effective “payroll cycle” can behave like negative DPO (you pay labor quickly), making CCC longer than it looks from AP alone.

#### Seasonality / lumpiness

* Staffing demand often tracks macro cycles (manufacturing, logistics peaks, holiday ramps).
* Project services can be lumpy by milestone acceptance and year-end client spending.

**Industry-specific cash traps**

* “Unbilled AR” growing faster than revenue (scope disputes, acceptance delays)
* Factoring reliance to fund payroll (not always bad, but can signal structural WC pressure)
* SLA penalties/chargebacks reducing cash after revenue recognition
* Rapid growth in temp staffing without AR discipline (growth eats cash)

## Part 3 (cont.): Capex

### Question 6: What does Capex look like?

Most business services are **asset-light**, but capex can jump with **delivery centers**, **call centers**, or **systems implementations**.

#### Growth Capex

* **New branches / offices / delivery centers**

  * Fit-out, IT equipment, phones, furniture, networking/security
  * For BPO: “seat build” (workstations, telecom, redundancy)
* **New market launches**

  * Local compliance setup, recruiting infrastructure, minimal physical assets unless a site-based model
* **Ramp to maturity**

  * Often more about **hiring and utilization ramp** than physical ramp; the economic ramp is typically driven by sales pipeline and recruiting capacity.

#### Maintenance Capex

* Laptop/desktop refresh, phones, networking
* Office refresh and small renovations
* Generally low as a % of revenue (but varies by site intensity)

#### Technology/Digital Capex (often the real “investment”)

* ATS/CRM (staffing), timekeeping & billing systems, ERP/GL
* VMS/MSP integrations, client portals, automation/RPA
* Data/security investments (especially if handling PII/PHI or regulated client data)
* Major “ERP replacement” or “timekeeping/billing migration” is a classic disruption risk

**Implementation watch-outs**

* Data migration and timecard/billing integrity (revenue leakage risk)
* Project overrun and change management costs
* Parallel runs and duplicated licensing costs during transition

## Part 5: Red Flags

### Question 8: 8–10 red flags to look for

| Red Flag                                                        | Early Signal (CIM/VDD)                                                   | Data Room Signal                                                                  | Why it’s a problem                                                   | How to investigate                                                                                             |
| --------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Revenue definition games (gross vs net, pass-through)**       | “Record revenue gross of pass-through” or unclear “net revenue” phrasing | Large pass-through/reimbursables; inconsistent revenue definitions across periods | Can inflate growth and distort margins / comps                       | Get a **gross-to-net bridge** and reconcile revenue definition to GL; request pass-through detail.             |
| **Utilization propping results / hidden bench**                 | “Best-in-class utilization” without metrics                              | Utilization spikes, overtime high, bench costs shifted to G&A                     | Unsustainable EBITDA; risk of post-close reset                       | Request utilization by month by practice/level, overtime %, bench headcount; ask policy for classifying bench. |
| **Spread compression from wage inflation**                      | “Wage investments” / “tight labor market”                                | Pay rates rising faster than bill rates; GP/hour declining                        | Margin erosion even if revenue grows                                 | Analyze bill/pay spread and burden by segment; look for repricing cadence and contract escalators.             |
| **Over-reliance on subcontractors**                             | “Flexible delivery model” / “partner network”                            | Subcontractor % rising; margin lower on those projects                            | Subcontractors reduce control, raise costs, increase compliance risk | Split margins employee vs subcontractor; review subcontractor terms and client restrictions.                   |
| **Customer concentration masked by “diversified base”**         | “Diverse customers” but no top customer chart                            | Top 1–3 customers drive majority of gross profit                                  | Rebid/termination risk; pricing pressure                             | Request top customer revenue **and gross profit**; review contract renewals and rebid dates.                   |
| **Weak contract governance (fixed-fee overruns)**               | “Strategic fixed-fee wins”                                               | Write-downs, WIP build, late change orders, declining realization                 | Future margin correction likely                                      | Project-level realization + write-downs; review change order process and PMO discipline.                       |
| **AR disputes / credits / chargebacks**                         | “Strong collections” but high DSO in exhibits                            | High credits, write-offs, VMS deductions                                          | Cash leakage and earnings quality issue                              | Review dispute log, credit memo trends, client approval cycle, timecard controls.                              |
| **Compliance exposure (worker classification / co-employment)** | “Uses 1099 contractors” / “independent contractor network”               | High 1099 mix; missing contracts; audits/litigation                               | Fines, back taxes, reputational risk                                 | Ask about classification policy, audits, legal claims; review worker agreements and client indemnities.        |
| **Under-reserved claims (workers comp / E&O / self-insurance)** | Minimal mention of claims                                                | Large claims post-close; volatile insurance expense                               | EBITDA not sustainable; cash risk                                    | Review loss runs, reserve methodology, self-insured retention, broker reports, claims history.                 |
| **Add-backs masking run-rate costs**                            | Heavy “normalized EBITDA” narrative                                      | Repeated “one-time” costs; growth investments missing                             | Run-rate EBITDA overstated                                           | Demand add-back schedule with support; test recurrence (same vendor, recurring months).                        |

## Part 6: First Data Requests

### Question 9: Standard first-round data requests (before the room opens)

Goal: unlock the **value driver tree** quickly and avoid the “revenue definition trap.”

#### P1 (Critical)

1. **Monthly P&L (36 months)**

   * **Granularity:** monthly, by service line/practice, plus total
   * **Format:** Excel export (not PDF)
   * **Why:** bridges, seasonality, margin trend, QoE base
2. **Revenue + Gross Profit by Customer**

   * **Granularity:** monthly for top 50 (or top 80% of revenue), trailing 24–36 months
   * Include: contract type (T&M/fixed/managed; MSP/VMS vs direct)
   * **Why:** concentration, retention, pricing power, durability
3. **Delivery headcount and hours**

   * Monthly headcount (billable/non-billable), billable hours, available hours
   * Breakdown by practice/level/location
   * **Why:** utilization + capacity story
4. **Bill rate / pay rate / burden (staffing) OR bill rate / cost rate (pro services)**

   * By month and major segment (role family, geography, client tier)
   * **Why:** spread / pricing story and wage inflation exposure
5. **AR aging + credit memos/write-offs**

   * Monthly aging for 24 months; include disputes/credits if tracked
   * **Why:** WC risk and revenue quality
6. **Backlog/bookings (if project/managed services)**

   * Backlog roll-forward; bookings by month; pipeline summary
   * **Why:** forward visibility
7. **Top customer contracts (MSA/SOW/rate cards)**

   * Top 10–20 customers by revenue or GP
   * **Why:** repricing cadence, termination clauses, SLA penalties, assignment, rebates

#### P2 (Important)

8. **Project-level margin file (if applicable)**

   * For fixed-fee and large projects: revenue, direct cost, write-downs, % complete, change orders
9. **Recruiter productivity + recruiting funnel (staffing)**

   * Recruiters by month; fills/starts; time-to-fill; conversion funnel
10. **Insurance and claims summary**

* Workers comp loss runs, E&O claims, self-insured retention, renewals

11. **Headcount roster with comp bands**

* Aggregated by role/location (not personally identifiable if avoidable)

12. **Facilities/site footprint**

* Branch list/sites with opening dates, lease terms, seat count (if BPO)

## Part 9: Accounting & Recognition

### Question 12: Industry-specific accounting and revenue recognition issues

#### Revenue Recognition (ASC 606 / IFRS 15 concepts applied to this industry)

**Professional services**

* **T&M:** revenue typically recognized as hours are delivered (over time), based on contractual rates.
* **Fixed-fee/milestone:** revenue recognized over time using progress measures (often input methods like costs incurred) or at milestones/acceptance depending on terms.
* **Common judgment areas / risks**

  * % complete estimates (optimism can pull revenue forward)
  * Change orders not formally approved (recognition vs collectability)
  * Cut-off around period-end time entry and approvals
  * Classification of reimbursables as revenue vs net

**Outsourced / managed services (BPO)**

* Revenue recognized over time as services are delivered (seat-month or transaction volumes).
* Variable consideration: SLA penalties/service credits can reduce revenue (timing and estimation matter).
* Cut-off: volume reporting integrity (transactions, tickets, usage) and client acceptance.

**Staffing**

* Key accounting nuance: **principal vs agent** (gross vs net presentation).

  * Many staffing firms record **gross billings as revenue** with wages in COGS (principal).
  * Some arrangements (e.g., certain MSP/VMS or pass-through models) may be **net fee** (agent).
* Risks:

  * Misclassification can distort revenue and margin comparability.
  * Cut-off issues if timecards aren’t approved by period end but hours worked occurred.

#### Deferred revenue / contract assets

* Deferred revenue is less prominent in classic staffing; more relevant for retainers/prepaid managed services.
* Contract assets (unbilled AR/WIP) are common in fixed-fee professional services.

#### Cost Recognition & Capitalization

**Costs that might be capitalized (watch list)**

* Internal-use software development (timekeeping/billing/ATS custom builds)
* Incremental contract acquisition costs (certain commissions) if they meet criteria and expected to be recovered (policy-driven; often inconsistent in practice)

**Aggressive vs normal**

* **Normal:** most recruiting and sales costs expensed as incurred.
* **Aggressive:** capitalizing broad recruiting costs, or pushing delivery costs below the line.

#### Balance Sheet Estimates & Reserves

Common reserves in this industry:

* **Allowance for doubtful accounts** (especially with concentrated customers or dispute-heavy programs)
* **Project loss reserves** (onerous contracts / fixed-fee losses)
* **Workers comp and liability reserves** (staffing-heavy)
* **Professional liability (E&O) and cyber reserves** (professional services/BPO)
* **Accrued payroll, bonuses, commissions, PTO**
* **SLA penalties / service credits** accruals (BPO)

#### Cash vs accrual considerations

* Staffing often has a structural cash timing mismatch (pay weekly, collect later).
* Factoring can mask underlying WC needs—important for net debt and ongoing cash economics.

#### Comparability issues (why peer comparison is hard)

* Gross vs net revenue presentation and pass-through treatment
* Bench cost classification (COGS vs SG&A)
* Subcontractor vs employee mix differences
* Lease accounting (IFRS 16 / ASC 842) affecting EBITDA comparability
* Different treatments of variable consideration (SLA credits) and project % complete

## Part 12: Anything Else

### Question 15: What else should an FDD team know?

**1) “Revenue” can be a trap in staffing.**
Always triangulate: **hours × spread = gross profit**, and focus on gross profit trends and unit economics.

**2) Small operational leaks compound.**
Timecard errors, rate non-compliance, credits, SLA penalties, and weak change order discipline can quietly shave hundreds of bps off margins.

**3) People dynamics are the business.**
Attrition, recruiter capacity, and key account dependence can be bigger risks than market size.

**4) Definitions vary across firms.**
Utilization, billable hours, “net revenue,” and whether bench sits in COGS vs SG&A are frequent sources of confusion—force definitions early.

**5) Cyclicality matters by vertical.**
Industrial/logistics staffing behaves very differently from healthcare/IT; outsourcing demand can be defensive, but pricing pressure can spike in downturns.

**6) Systems and controls matter more than you think.**
If timekeeping/billing/ATS are weak or being replaced, expect revenue leakage and disruption risk.
