## Part 1: Industry Identification

### Question 1: What words / phrases / concepts signal Real Estate & Construction (Development, Property Management, Building Services)?

Think “**assets/space + contracts + projects + leases + job costing**.” In kickoff docs, you’ll typically see a mix of **real estate operating vocabulary** (rent/leases/NOI) and/or **project/contracting vocabulary** (backlog/WIP/change orders).

### A) Real estate ownership / operations (owner-operator) signals

* **Portfolio language:** “portfolio,” “assets,” “properties,” “communities,” “sites,” “square footage,” “doors/units,” “beds,” “GLA” (gross leasable area)
* **Leasing & rent:** “rent roll,” “in-place rent,” “market rent,” “asking rent,” “effective rent,” “rent escalations,” “concessions,” “free rent,” “abatements,” “lease-up,” “stabilized”
* **Occupancy metrics:** “occupancy,” “physical occupancy,” “economic occupancy,” “vacancy,” “leased %,” “tenant turnover,” “renewals”
* **Real estate profitability metrics:** “NOI” (net operating income), “same-store NOI,” “NOI margin,” “cap rate,” “yield on cost,” “cash-on-cash”
* **Lease structures:** “NNN,” “modified gross,” “CAM reimbursements,” “tenant reimbursements,” “operating expense recoveries,” “WALT” (weighted average lease term), “lease expiry schedule”
* **Capex/turn costs:** “TI/LC” (tenant improvements / leasing commissions), “unit turns,” “make-ready,” “R&M,” “capital repairs,” “deferred maintenance”

### B) Property management (third-party / fee-for-service) signals

* **Scale:** “units under management,” “doors under management,” “AUM” (sometimes used loosely), “properties managed”
* **Fee vocabulary:** “management fees,” “bps of rent,” “% of collected rent,” “per-unit-per-month (PUPM),” “leasing fees,” “renewal fees,” “ancillary fees”
* **Operational workflow:** “leasing office,” “maintenance work orders,” “turnovers,” “vendor management,” “resident services,” “collections,” “delinquency,” “evictions”
* **Platforms:** “Yardi,” “RealPage,” “AppFolio,” “property management system (PMS)” (often shows up)

### C) Development (merchant builder / developer / homebuilder) signals

* **Pipeline/stage gating:** “land acquisition,” “entitlements,” “zoning,” “planning permission,” “permits,” “site work,” “horizontal development”
* **Project economics:** “GDV” (gross development value), “yield on cost,” “development margin,” “IRR,” “stabilized value,” “promote,” “JV,” “GP/LP”
* **Sales cadence (residential):** “sales velocity,” “absorption,” “net orders,” “cancellations,” “closings,” “backlog (homebuilder context),” “pre-sales”
* **Construction finance:** “construction loan,” “draw schedule,” “lender inspections,” “interest capitalization,” “cost to complete”
* **Balance sheet:** “CIP” / “construction in progress,” “land bank,” “WIP inventory,” “spec inventory”

### D) Building services / specialty contracting signals (HVAC, MEP, facilities, maintenance)

* **Services language:** “facility management,” “HVAC service,” “MEP,” “fire/life safety,” “elevator,” “janitorial,” “security,” “landscaping,” “snow removal”
* **Contract structure:** “service agreements,” “maintenance contracts,” “preventive maintenance (PM),” “T&M” (time & materials), “fixed-price,” “SLAs”
* **Unit volume:** “work orders,” “tickets,” “truck rolls,” “service calls,” “install base,” “sites serviced,” “technician utilization,” “billable hours”
* **Quality signals:** “call-backs,” “warranty claims,” “first-time fix rate”

### E) Construction contractor signals (GC/subs) that commonly overlap with building services

* **Project controls:** “job costing,” “SOV” (schedule of values), “pay apps,” “AIA billing,” “RFIs,” “submittals,” “punch list”
* **Commercial terms:** “change orders,” “retainage,” “liquidated damages,” “GMP,” “lump sum,” “cost-plus”
* **Forward indicators:** “backlog,” “book-to-bill,” “bid pipeline”
* **Accounting artifacts:** “WIP schedule,” “over/under billings,” “contract assets/liabilities,” “cost-to-complete,” “margin fade”

## Part 2: Units That Matter

### Question 2: 5–8 KPIs a generalist must understand

These are the “don’t embarrass yourself” KPIs that cover the three common models (property ops, development, services/contracting).

| KPI                                                                     | Definition (how operators use it)                                                                                                   | Why it matters in diligence                                                                                                                                               |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Occupancy** (Physical % / Economic %)                                 | **Physical:** % of units/SF leased. **Economic:** rent actually collected as % of potential (captures concessions + delinquencies). | Early read on demand, pricing power, and near-term revenue risk. The physical vs economic gap often reveals concessions or bad debt.                                      |
| **Net Operating Income (NOI)** / **NOI Margin**                         | Property-level profit: rental/other income **minus property operating expenses** (before corporate G&A, interest, depreciation).    | For owners/operators, NOI is the core “engine.” NOI bridges more cleanly to valuation (cap rates) than EBITDA. Also forces clarity on what’s property-level vs corporate. |
| **Effective Rent** (per unit / per SF) & **Rent Growth**                | “What tenants actually pay” after concessions/abatements, tracked over time and vs market.                                          | Documents can show “headline” rent growth while effective rents are flat/negative due to concessions. Drives real revenue trajectory.                                     |
| **Same-Store NOI / Like-for-Like (LFL) Growth**                         | Performance of a consistent asset set (excluding acquisitions, dispositions, lease-ups, major repositionings).                      | Separates **true operating performance** from “we bought more stuff” or “we’re still stabilizing.” A key quality-of-earnings lens.                                        |
| **Lease Expiry Profile** (WALT, expirations by year) & **Renewal Rate** | When leases roll + how often tenants renew (for multifamily, “renewal %” and “turnover”; for commercial, WALT/roll schedule).       | The single biggest forward risk indicator for commercial portfolios and many service contracts: near-term roll cliffs can crush NOI or force capex (TI/LC).               |
| **Backlog** & **Book-to-Bill** (services/contracting)                   | **Backlog:** contracted work not yet recognized as revenue. **Book-to-bill:** new bookings ÷ revenue recognized.                    | Shows revenue visibility and whether growth is durable. But backlog quality matters: margin, cancellation rights, unpriced change orders, customer credit.                |
| **Project/Job Gross Margin** & **Margin Fade**                          | Margin expected at bid vs margin realized, by job/project. “Fade” = systematic decline as jobs progress/close.                      | The #1 “gotcha” in contractors: poor estimating/project controls can look fine early but collapse later through write-downs and claims.                                   |
| **Working Capital Drag** (DSO + Retainage + Underbillings/WIP)          | How much cash is tied up in A/R, retention, and unbilled work (contract assets) vs funded by payables/deferred revenue.             | Explains why EBITDA doesn’t convert to cash. In contractors, WC can swamp earnings; in property ops, prepaids/deposits and timing can distort cash.                       |

## Part 3: Value Driver Framework

### Question 3: What does the EBITDA value driver tree look like?

Because this “industry” includes **three distinct economics**, the assistant should choose the closest archetype and render the corresponding tree:

1. **Owner/operator real estate (NOI-centric)**
2. **Property management (fee-for-service)**
3. **Building services / contracting (job-margin + backlog)**
4. **Development (project-based, often inventory/CIP driven)**

Below is a combined, modular tree that works for most deals.

```
EBITDA
├── Net Revenue / Property Income
│   ├── (Owner/Operator) Rental & Other Property Income
│   │   ├── # Units or SF × In-place Rent (per unit / per SF)
│   │   ├── Occupancy (Physical) × Rent Levels
│   │   ├── Other Income (parking, pet, storage, fees)
│   │   ├── Reimbursements (CAM/expense recoveries, NNN)
│   │   └── Gross-to-Net Adjustments
│   │       ├── Concessions / Free Rent / Abatements
│   │       ├── Bad Debt / Delinquencies / Write-offs
│   │       └── Early Terminations / Credits
│   │
│   ├── (Property Mgmt) Fee Revenue
│   │   ├── Units/SF Under Mgmt × Fee Rate (bps of rent or PUPM)
│   │   ├── Leasing Fees (# new leases × fee) + Renewal Fees
│   │   ├── Project/Construction Mgmt Fees (if applicable)
│   │   └── Ancillary Fees (maintenance coordination, admin fees)
│   │
│   ├── (Building Services) Service Revenue
│   │   ├── Recurring Contracts: # sites × monthly contract price
│   │   ├── T&M: # service calls × avg invoice value
│   │   ├── Projects/Installs: # projects × avg contract value
│   │   └── Gross-to-Net (credits, rebates, warranty concessions)
│   │
│   └── (Development/Construction) Project Revenue Recognized
│       ├── Real Estate Sales: # closings/units × avg sale price
│       ├── Construction: % complete × contract value (ASC 606)
│       ├── Approved Change Orders
│       └── Variable Consideration (claims/incentives) [risk!]
│
├── COGS / Direct Costs / Property Operating Expenses
│   ├── (Owner/Operator) Property Opex (drives NOI)
│   │   ├── Utilities
│   │   ├── Repairs & Maintenance (R&M)
│   │   ├── On-site Payroll (property staff)
│   │   ├── Property Taxes (often non-controllable step-ups)
│   │   ├── Insurance (volatile in many markets)
│   │   ├── Turn/make-ready + trash/landscaping/snow
│   │   └── Bad Debt (if not netted above)
│   │
│   ├── (Property Mgmt) Direct Labor & Direct Costs
│   │   ├── On-site staff payroll (if borne by manager)
│   │   ├── Leasing staff / commissions (if internal)
│   │   └── Subcontracted maintenance (if not pass-through)
│   │
│   ├── (Building Services / Contracting) Job Direct Costs
│   │   ├── Field Labor (hours × wage rate × burden)
│   │   ├── Subcontractors (hours/units × sub rates)
│   │   ├── Materials/Parts (units × cost; inflation sensitivity)
│   │   ├── Equipment & Fleet (fuel, maintenance, depreciation)
│   │   ├── Jobsite costs (permits, rentals, consumables)
│   │   └── Warranty/Callbacks/Rework (quality driver)
│   │
│   └── (Development) Project Costs (often capitalized)
│       ├── Land + Site work
│       ├── Hard Costs (GC/subs/materials)
│       ├── Soft Costs (architect/engineering, permits, legal)
│       ├── Interest Capitalized
│       └── Capitalized Overhead (policy risk)
│
└── Operating Expenses (OpEx / SG&A)
    ├── Corporate G&A (finance, HR, legal, IT)
    ├── Regional/Branch Overhead (ops leaders, dispatch, offices)
    ├── Sales / BD / Estimating / Bid Costs
    ├── Insurance (GL, WC, bonding costs, deductibles)
    ├── Technology & systems (PMS/ERP/field service)
    └── Other (professional fees, recruiting, training)
```

**Mix effects that matter (often overlooked):**

* **Contract type mix:** T&M vs fixed-price vs GMP vs cost-plus (risk transfer changes margin volatility)
* **Customer/tenant quality mix:** credit quality drives bad debt and renewal stability
* **Geography mix:** taxes, insurance, wage rates, union prevalence, permitting timelines
* **Asset type mix:** multifamily vs office vs industrial vs retail (different capex + leasing dynamics)
* **Service mix:** recurring maintenance vs project installs (recurring is typically higher quality revenue)

### Question 4: Full cost structure and what drives margins

Because the “industry” is really multiple business models, here’s the complete P&L view by archetype, plus the unit economics lens that a generalist needs.

---

## A) Owner/operator real estate (NOI-centric P&L)

### Revenue (property income)

* Base rent
* Reimbursements (CAM/NNN, tax/insurance recoveries)
* Other income (parking, storage, pet, application/admin fees)
* Less: concessions/free rent/abatements, bad debt (presentation varies)

### Property operating expenses (the NOI “COGS”)

Typical major buckets:

* Utilities
* Repairs & maintenance
* Contract services (landscaping, trash, security, janitorial)
* On-site payroll
* Property taxes (often step-change on reassessment)
* Insurance (can spike materially)
* Turnover costs / make-ready
* Marketing (esp. multifamily lease-up)
* HOA/ground lease (if applicable)

**What drives “NOI margin”:**

* Occupancy and effective rent (the big lever)
* Concessions and delinquency
* Property tax reassessments + insurance market conditions (often “non-controllable”)
* Turnover and make-ready costs (proxy for customer satisfaction/asset quality)
* Utilities and maintenance intensity (asset age + deferred maintenance)

**Unit economics:**

* **Per unit/month (PUPM)**: revenue, opex, NOI
* **Per SF**: especially commercial

**Margin differentiation (owner/operators):**

* High margin: strong submarket + asset quality + low turnover + tight expense control + proactive maintenance
* Low margin: weak demand + high concessions + high delinquency + deferred maintenance + tax/insurance shocks

---

## B) Property management (fee-for-service P&L)

### Revenue

* Management fees (bps of rent or PUPM)
* Leasing fees / renewal fees
* Construction/project management fees (if offered)
* Ancillary/admin fees (varies by contract)
* **Pass-through reimbursements** (can be in revenue or netted out—major comparability issue)

### COGS (what’s “direct”)

* On-site payroll (if the manager provides staff as part of the contract)
* Leasing payroll / commissions (if internal)
* Direct vendor costs (if the company takes responsibility rather than passing through)
* Background checks / leasing admin costs (small but present)

### OpEx

* Regional managers / supervision
* Corporate G&A
* Sales / business development

**Key margin drivers:**

* Fee rate and contract structure (minimum fees, escalators, termination rights)
* Staff-to-unit ratios (labor productivity)
* Vendor management (if margin includes maintenance coordination/markup)
* Client concentration + loss rates (contract churn)

**Typical profitability shape (rule-of-thumb, wide variance):**

* EBITDA tends to be **mid-single to mid-teens** for pure third-party management; higher if there’s meaningful ancillary/maintenance revenue and good scale.

---

## C) Building services / specialty contracting (service + project P&L)

### Revenue

* Recurring service contracts (monthly/annual)
* T&M service calls
* Small projects / installs (replacement capex, retrofits)
* Material pass-through (presentation may be gross or net)

### COGS / direct costs (the big buckets)

* Field labor (wage + burden)
* Subcontractors (overflow capacity / specialized trades)
* Materials/parts
* Fleet and equipment costs
* Warranty/callbacks/rework

### OpEx

* Dispatch/call center
* Branch leadership
* Sales + estimating
* Corporate G&A
* Insurance (GL/WC), licensing, training

**Unit economics:**

* **Per technician:** billable hours × effective billing rate – labor burden – callbacks
* **Per service call / work order:** revenue – labor – parts – allocated overhead
* **Per site contract:** contract price – expected labor hours – expected parts – overhead

**Margin differentiation:**

* High margin: high recurring mix, disciplined pricing, strong dispatch + first-time fix, high utilization, low callbacks, escalators tied to wage/material indices
* Low margin: underpriced fixed contracts, poor scheduling, heavy overtime, high warranty, weak collections, high subcontractor reliance

---

## D) Development (project-based economics; “EBITDA” can be misleading)

### Revenue recognition model varies

* Often **point-in-time at closing** for unit sales (residential)
* Sometimes **over time** if criteria met (certain contracts/jurisdictions) — diligence needs to confirm policy

### Costs (often capitalized rather than expensed)

* Land and site costs
* Hard costs (GC/subs/materials)
* Soft costs (A&E, permits, legal, developer fees)
* Interest capitalization
* Potential capitalized overhead

**What drives “margin” in development:**

* Sales price / absorption vs budget
* Construction cost inflation vs budget
* Cycle time (entitlements + construction duration → interest carry)
* Change orders and scope creep
* Mix (unit types, finishes, upgrade take-rate)

**Generalist warning:** You can see strong “accounting profit” with **negative cash** due to inventory/CIP build, interest carry, and timing of closings.

## Part 3: Working Capital & Capex

### Question 5: Working Capital value driver tree

Working capital differs sharply by archetype.

```
Operating Working Capital
├── Receivables
│   ├── (Owner/Operator) Tenant A/R
│   │   ├── Rent billing cycle (monthly, typically in advance)
│   │   ├── Delinquency / eviction pipeline
│   │   └── Bad debt policy + payment plans
│   ├── (Property Mgmt) Client A/R (fees) + reimbursables
│   └── (Services/Contracting) Trade A/R
│       ├── Billing cadence (monthly, milestone, T&M weekly)
│       ├── Pay apps / approval cycle
│       ├── Dispute rates + short pays
│       └── Retainage receivable (often 5–10% withheld)
│
├── Contract Assets / Liabilities (construction accounting)
│   ├── Underbillings (revenue recognized > billings)
│   └── Overbillings (billings > revenue recognized)
│
├── Inventory (if applicable)
│   ├── (Services) Parts inventory / truck stock / staging
│   └── (Development) WIP / lots / finished units [often huge, but not “OWC” in the classic sense]
│
├── Payables
│   ├── Vendor payables (materials, subs)
│   ├── Payroll accruals
│   └── Timing dynamics (subs may demand faster pay than owners pay you)
│
└── Other current items (often big)
    ├── Security deposits (liability; trust/escrow handling)
    ├── Tenant prepaids / deferred rent (liability)
    ├── Accrued insurance / self-insured reserves
    ├── Property tax accruals
    └── Deferred revenue (service contracts billed in advance)
```

#### Receivables: what drives DSO and nuances

* **Owner/operators:** “DSO” isn’t the right lens; focus on **delinquency %, aging, collections effectiveness, and bad debt**. Rent is often due at period start; delinquencies create rapid aging.
* **Services/contracting:** DSO driven by:

  * Pay app approval cycles, disputes, documentation requirements
  * Customer type (public sector often slower)
  * Retainage (functionally increases DSO)
  * Contract terms and change order approval timeliness

**Common nuance:** “A/R is fine” can hide **unbilled revenue** or **unapproved change orders** that aren’t in A/R yet.

#### Inventory

* **Building services:** parts inventory risk = obsolescence, shrink, poor controls, dead stock.
* **Development:** inventory/WIP is the business; key risk is **slow absorption** + **cost to complete** + **impairment**.

#### Payables: what drives DPO

* Subcontractor terms vs owner payment terms (cash squeeze if you pay subs faster)
* Supplier concentration and ability to negotiate
* Early pay discounts vs liquidity needs

#### Cash conversion patterns / cash traps

* **Owner/operators** can be **cash-generative** with rent collected early, but can have large periodic cash uses (tax bills, insurance renewals, major repairs, TI/LC).
* **Contractors** can be cash traps via **underbillings, retainage, claims**, and paying subs ahead of collections.
* **Developers** are inherently cash-absorbing until closings/exit.

### Question 6: What does Capex look like?

Again: separate **property capex** from **services capex** from **development capital deployed**.

## A) Owner/operator property capex

**Growth capex**

* Acquisitions + value-add repositioning
* Expansions (adding units/SF), amenity upgrades to drive rent premiums

**Maintenance capex**

* Roofs, HVAC replacement, elevators, paving
* Unit turns / make-ready capex
* Life-safety systems, compliance upgrades
* Rule of thumb: maintenance capex can be **meaningful** and is often the “real” depreciation.

**Tech/digital**

* Smart access, energy management, tenant portals, building automation
* PMS upgrades and data/reporting

## B) Property management capex

Generally lighter, mostly:

* PMS/ERP implementation, reporting tools
* Office + basic IT

## C) Building services / contracting capex

**Growth capex**

* New branches/geographies (often needs working capital more than capex)
* Equipment to add capabilities (e.g., HVAC install, specialty tools)
* Small tuck-in acquisitions (not capex, but integration costs)

**Maintenance capex**

* Fleet replacement (vans/trucks)
* Tools and test equipment replacement
* Warehouse fixtures, safety equipment

**Tech/digital**

* Field service management software (dispatching, routing, mobile time capture)
* Job costing/ERP (critical for contractors)
* CRM and contract management systems

**Ramp-up to maturity (typical pattern)**

* New branch/market often takes **months to a couple of years** to reach stable utilization and customer density (route density is a real economic driver).

## D) Development “capex”

Developers often deploy capital into **land and construction in progress** (inventory/CIP), which is not classic PP&E capex but is the main capital need:

* Land acquisition costs
* Site work and vertical construction
* Interest carry
* Predevelopment/entitlement costs (high risk if projects don’t proceed)

## Part 5: Red Flags

### Question 8: 8–10 red flags (early signal + data room signal + why + how to investigate)

| Red Flag                                                    | Early Signal (CIM/VDD)                                                                 | Data Room Signal                                                             | Why it’s a problem                                                         | How to investigate                                                                                         |
| ----------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **“Stabilized” / “pro forma” earnings doing heavy lifting** | “Stabilized NOI,” “lease-up underway,” “below-market rents with mark-to-market upside” | Large concessions, slow absorption, negative LFL NOI, aggressive assumptions | Underwriting relies on future execution; can mask current underperformance | Request same-store NOI, effective rent bridge, lease-up cohort performance, leasing traffic and conversion |
| **Occupancy stable but cash isn’t**                         | “Strong demand” + strong NOI narrative                                                 | Rising tenant A/R aging, delinquency spike, payment plans                    | Economic occupancy lower than physical; bad debt risk                      | Monthly delinquency, aging, collections policy, eviction pipeline, credit screening changes                |

| **Backlog touted as strength, but low quality**             | “Record backlog,” “strong pipeline”                                                    | Backlog concentrated, low-margin fixed-price, unapproved COs                 | Revenue visibility overstated; margin downside                             | Backlog by contract type + margin; change order status; cancellation terms; top customer review            |
| **Margin fade / late job write-downs**                      | “Disciplined project execution”                                                        | WIP shows repeated margin reductions; big closeout losses                    | Indicates weak estimating/project controls; future earnings risk           | Track bid vs latest GM by job; change orders; claims; PM turnover; post-mortems                            |
| **Underbillings / unbilled revenue grows**                  | “Working capital investment for growth”                                                | Large contract assets, long-aged underbillings                               | Cash trap; may be uncollectible if disputed                                | Aging of unbilled, approval status, documentation, customer acceptance terms                               |
| **Retention/retainage pile-up**                             | “Significant public/large projects”                                                    | Retainage receivable large and old                                           | Cash trapped; potential disputes/defects                                   | Retainage aging, punch list status, acceptance/certification process                                       |
| **Deferred maintenance / capex starvation**                 | “Lean operating model” + low capex                                                     | PCRs show major systems near end-of-life; high emergency repairs             | NOI not sustainable; future capex cliff                                    | Capex history by property; major system inventory; planned capex; compare to repairs trend                 |

## Part 9: Accounting & Recognition

### Question 12: Industry-specific accounting and revenue recognition issues

This space has multiple “gotcha” areas; the assistant should explicitly flag which ones apply based on the company model.

## Revenue recognition

### A) Construction / contracting / many specialty building services (ASC 606 / IFRS 15)

* **Over time recognition** is common when the customer controls the asset as it’s built or there’s no alternative use + enforceable right to payment.
* **Measure of progress** often **cost-to-cost** → heavy reliance on **cost-to-complete estimates**.
* **Variable consideration**: change orders, claims, incentives, liquidated damages. Aggressive recognition = recognizing claims early or assuming unapproved change orders are collectible.
* **Cut-off risks**:

  * Accelerating % complete at period-end
  * Understating remaining costs
  * Recognizing revenue on unapproved COs/claims
* **Contract assets/liabilities**: under/over billings can be large and must reconcile to WIP.

### B) Building services (recurring contracts + T&M)

* Recurring service revenue generally recognized **ratably** as services are delivered.
* T&M recognized as work is performed (time/parts).
* **Principal vs agent**: whether pass-through subcontractor costs are presented gross or net.
* **Credits/warranty**: need reserves or netting (depends on policy).

### C) Property management (fees)

* Management fees recognized as services are provided (typically monthly).
* Leasing/renewal fees: policy varies (at signing vs over lease term); diligence should check consistency and materiality.
* Reimbursables/pass-through: gross vs net presentation is a comparability landmine.

### D) Real estate owners/operators (leases)

* Lease income recognition can include:

  * Straight-line rent impacts (non-cash smoothing)
  * Variable rent and recoveries
  * Lease incentives and abatements
* Bad debt treatment and presentation can materially affect NOI comparability.

## Cost recognition & capitalization

### Development / real estate projects

* **Capitalization policy** is critical:

  * Which payroll/overhead costs get capitalized into CIP/inventory?
  * Interest capitalization policy and when capitalization starts/stops
  * Treatment of predevelopment/abandoned project costs
* Aggressive capitalization can **inflate EBITDA** by shifting costs below the line.

### Contractors

* Mobilization and certain contract fulfillment costs may be capitalized depending on policy (ASC 340 considerations).
* **Loss contracts / onerous contracts**: should be reserved promptly when total estimated costs exceed contract consideration.

## Balance sheet estimates & reserves

* **Allowance for doubtful accounts** (tenants or customers)
* **Warranty reserves / callbacks**
* **Claims/litigation reserves**
* **Insurance reserves** (especially if self-insured retentions are meaningful)
* **Environmental liabilities** (Phase I/II issues, remediation)
* **Asset impairment** (development inventory, real estate held for sale, underperforming properties)

## Cash vs accrual considerations

* **Contract accounting** can create big gaps between revenue and cash:

  * Retainage
  * Underbillings
  * Disputed billings
* **Straight-line rent** can overstate cash generation versus GAAP revenue.
* Developers can show profits while consuming cash due to inventory build.

## Comparability issues

* NOI vs EBITDA framing (owner/operators)
* Gross vs net treatment of pass-through costs
* Different capitalization policies (especially development)
* Different revenue recognition approaches for leasing fees, change orders, claims

## Part 11: Ownership & Related Party Patterns

### Question 14: Common ownership structures + related party transactions

## Ownership structures commonly seen

* **Family-owned / founder-led contractors**

  * Risks: informal controls, owner-centric relationships, personal expenses, key-person dependency
* **Developer with project-level SPVs/JVs**

  * Risks: off-balance sheet obligations, promotes, guarantees, related-party land deals, complexity in consolidation

## Part 12: Anything Else

### Question 15: What else should an FDD team know?

* **First-time practitioner trap #1:** treating this like a normal “product gross margin” business.

  * Owner/operator real estate is **NOI + capex** economics, not just EBITDA.
* **First-time practitioner trap #2:** confusing **headline rent** with **effective rent**. Concessions can fully erase “rent growth.”
* **First-time practitioner trap #3:** believing backlog at face value. Backlog quality depends on **contract type, margin, cancellation rights, and change order status**.
* **First-time practitioner trap #4:** ignoring **working capital mechanics** (retainage, underbillings, pay app approvals). Cash can be the real issue.
* **First-time practitioner trap #5:** missing capitalization policy risk (especially developers). Capitalized overhead/interest can inflate reported earnings.
* **Terminology to master quickly:**

  * NOI, same-store NOI, TI/LC, WALT, NNN/CAM, concessions/abatements
  * Backlog, WIP, over/under billings, retainage, change orders, margin fade
* **External factors that can dominate outcomes:**

  * Interest rates / cap rates (valuation + feasibility)
  * Insurance markets (property + WC/GL)
  * Wage/material inflation (services/contracting)
  * Local regulation (rent control, building performance standards, permitting)
